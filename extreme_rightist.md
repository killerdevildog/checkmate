# Why Conservatives, Libertarians, Constitutionalists, and Patriots Should Support Operation Checkmate

> A bill written to make government accountable to the same law as everyone else — and to put **the Constitution and the citizen back at the center**, where the founders intended them to be.

If you are a constitutional conservative, a libertarian, a Second Amendment advocate, a religious-liberty defender, a federalist, or a self-described patriot — you have spent your political life arguing that **government has grown beyond its lawful bounds**. That federal agencies have become unaccountable. That state and federal officials have used power against citizens with no real consequence to themselves. That the Constitution, on paper, is increasingly disconnected from the law as actually enforced.

**Operation Checkmate** is a citizen-written bill that addresses these concerns directly — not as rhetoric, but with **personal civil and criminal liability** attached to the officials who cross constitutional lines. Judicial immunity, qualified immunity, prosecutorial immunity, sovereign immunity — all abrogated within the scope of the Act. Citizens, not federal prosecutors, get the means to investigate and force accountability.

## Documented patterns the bill addresses

These are not talking points. They are documented federal and state actions, with names, dates, and outcomes.

### Federal-officer use of force without accountability — Ruby Ridge

In August 1992, the U.S. Marshals Service and the FBI conducted a siege at the Idaho cabin of Randy Weaver. During the operation:

- Marshals shot and killed **Sammy Weaver**, age 14.
- An FBI sniper, **Lon Horiuchi**, shot and killed **Vicki Weaver** while she was standing in the cabin doorway holding her 10-month-old daughter.

A subsequent **Senate Judiciary Subcommittee** investigation (1995) found significant federal misconduct, including rules of engagement that exceeded constitutional limits. The Weaver family received a **$3.1 million civil settlement**. Horiuchi was indicted for involuntary manslaughter in Idaho state court — but the federal court dismissed the prosecution under the doctrine of *In re Neagle*, 135 U.S. 1 (1890): federal officers cannot be prosecuted by states for acts done under federal authority.

**That doctrine — the doctrine that allowed an FBI sniper who killed a mother holding her infant to walk free of state prosecution — is expressly abrogated by Operation Checkmate.** See **§14.7** ("Federal Officer Status Is Not a Shield"): a federal officer's conduct in Utah that constitutes a state-law offense under this Act is reachable in Utah state court regardless of federal-officer status. The dual-sovereignty doctrine (*United States v. Lanza*, 260 U.S. 377 (1922); *Gamble v. United States*, 587 U.S. 678 (2019)) constitutionally supports concurrent state prosecution.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§14.7** — abrogates *Neagle* within Utah for offenses defined in this title; federal grand-jury referral via §8.10(a) and concurrent Utah-state prosecution via §8.5
- **§4.1.1** — abrogates qualified immunity (*Harlow v. Fitzgerald*, 457 U.S. 800 (1982)); the "clearly established law" inquiry has no application
- **§1.3 Battery (Government Context)** — direct deprivation of life, liberty, or property through unlawful force; Class 2 felony or higher
- **§11 Death of a Citizen Resulting from Violation** — capital classification where death results
- **§10 Forfeiture** — convicted federal officials forfeit pensions, benefits, and federal contracts (the bill reaches federal officers acting in Utah)
- **§8 Citizen Council** — twelve citizens can investigate and indict by Joint Declaration of Prosecution; no need for the cooperation of the U.S. Attorney's office that may have been the source of the problem

### Federal siege and use of fire — Waco

In April 1993, the **Branch Davidian compound** in Waco, Texas, was the site of a 51-day federal siege initiated by the ATF. The ATF's initial raid on February 28, 1993 resulted in the deaths of four ATF agents and several Davidians. After the siege, on April 19, 1993, FBI tactical operations using CS gas and tank breaching led to a fire that killed **76 Davidians, including 25 children**.

Multiple investigations followed (DOJ, House Government Reform Committee, Special Counsel John Danforth) and confirmed significant federal failings — including the use of pyrotechnic devices that the FBI initially denied, and the absence of fire-suppression assets despite the use of CS gas in a wooden structure.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.3 Battery** for direct deprivations of life
- **§1.10 Anarchy** for federal action without lawful basis (where elements established)
- **§1.6 Cabalism** — small-group coordination of federal officials producing unjust effect on citizens (the planning of the operation is reachable as small-group conduct)
- **§1.9 Treason** — where the conduct constitutes "constitutional warfare" against citizens, the U.S. Constitution, or the Utah Constitution (a high bar, but reachable in extreme cases)
- **§14.7** — federal officers reachable in Utah state court for the same conduct, with no *Neagle* immunity

### Federal land overreach and prosecutorial misconduct — Bundy Ranch

In April 2014, the **Bureau of Land Management** sent armed agents to seize cattle from rancher **Cliven Bundy** in Bunkerville, Nevada, over disputed grazing fees on federal land. The standoff drew protesters from across the country; BLM withdrew without bloodshed.

Federal prosecutors later charged Bundy and his sons with conspiracy and assault on a federal officer. In **January 2018**, U.S. District Judge **Gloria Navarro** dismissed the charges **with prejudice**, finding **prosecutorial misconduct** so severe — including the suppression of exculpatory evidence (Brady violations), false statements about FBI surveillance, and undisclosed evidence of an armed BLM presence — that the prosecution had violated the defendants' due process rights.

This is, in microcosm, exactly what Operation Checkmate addresses: a federal prosecution built on suppressed evidence, against rural citizens whose only meaningful recourse was a years-long fight in federal court.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.14 Prosecutorial Duty to Disclose Adverse Evidence** — codifies and expands *Brady v. Maryland*, 373 U.S. 83 (1963), with criminal liability for prosecutors who fail to disclose
- **§1.15 Malicious Prosecution** — Class 1 felony for continuing to prosecute a citizen the prosecutor knows or recklessly disregards as innocent of the offense charged
- **§1.16 Duty to Seek Reduction or Dismissal** — Class 3 felony for failure to seek dismissal when evidence collapses; capital where failure causes death
- **§4.1.1** — *Imbler v. Pachtman* prosecutorial immunity expressly abrogated
- **§14.7** — federal prosecutors reachable in Utah for conduct in Utah constituting offenses under this Act

### LaVoy Finicum and federal-officer false statements

In January 2016, after the Malheur National Wildlife Refuge occupation in Oregon, **LaVoy Finicum** was killed at a traffic stop while attempting to flee from federal and state officers. An **FBI agent, W. Joseph Astarita**, was indicted in June 2017 for **lying about firing his rifle** during the encounter. Astarita was acquitted at trial, but the underlying pattern — a federal officer providing false statements about the use of force — is exactly the conduct this bill makes reachable.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.18 Judicial Duty to Engage on the Merits** (in any subsequent federal proceedings) — courts cannot rubber-stamp prosecutorial filings or accept conclusory affidavits from federal officers
- **§1.14 Prosecutorial Duty to Disclose** — adverse evidence about officer truthfulness must be disclosed to the jury
- **§14.7** — federal officers' state-law violations reachable in Utah state court

### IRS targeting of disfavored political groups

From 2010 to 2013, the **Tax-Exempt Organizations division of the IRS**, under Lois Lerner, applied **heightened scrutiny and delayed processing** to applications from Tea Party, "patriots," "9/12," and similar conservative groups. The **Treasury Inspector General for Tax Administration (TIGTA)** confirmed the targeting in a May 2013 report. In **2017**, the U.S. Department of Justice formally **apologized** to plaintiff Tea Party groups and entered a settlement.

This is the federal-government version of the selective-enforcement pattern — using regulatory authority to disadvantage citizens based on their political views.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.7 Government Racketeering** — combinations of officials using authority to coerce, punish, or pressure citizens for political reasons; the IRS targeting program is precisely this conduct
- **§1.6 Cabalism** — small-group coordination producing unjust effect on citizens
- **§1.10 Anarchy** — administrative action without lawful basis (heightened scrutiny applied for political reasons rather than statutory criteria)
- **§1.13 Official Intimidation** — drawn-out investigations to wear down resistance is express conduct under this section
- **§14.7** — federal officials reachable in Utah for conduct affecting Utah citizens

### Operation Choke Point — using banking regulation to defund disfavored industries

From approximately **2013 to 2017**, the **DOJ, FDIC, and OCC** ran "Operation Choke Point," a coordinated effort that pressured banks to **terminate relationships with industries deemed "high-risk"** — including firearms dealers, payday lenders, ammunition retailers, and others, regardless of whether those businesses were operating lawfully. House Government Oversight investigations documented the program; it was officially terminated in 2017 after a change in administration.

This is the use of banking-regulator authority to accomplish what Congress had not authorized: the de facto strangulation of constitutionally-protected commerce.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.7 Government Racketeering** — coordinated use of regulatory power to extract benefit, punish, or coerce; Class 2 felony individually, Class 1 for patterns
- **§1.6 Cabalism** — small-group coordination producing unjust effect on lawful businesses
- **§1.9 Treason — regulatory-warfare prong** — sustained, coordinated use of regulation to break or burden citizens' livelihoods (express category in §1.9)
- **§14.7** — federal officials reachable

### Religious liberty — government compulsion vs. conscience

The Supreme Court has, in recent years, repeatedly addressed cases in which government bodies sought to **compel** religious citizens or institutions to participate in conduct against conscience:

- ***Burwell v. Hobby Lobby Stores***, 573 U.S. 682 (2014) — closely-held corporation could not be compelled to fund contraceptive coverage in violation of religious belief.
- ***Masterpiece Cakeshop v. Colorado Civil Rights Commission***, 584 U.S. 617 (2018) — Colorado Civil Rights Commission's hostility to religious belief violated the Free Exercise Clause.
- ***Little Sisters of the Poor Saints Peter and Paul Home v. Pennsylvania***, 591 U.S. ___ (2020) — protected religious orders from contraceptive mandates.
- ***Kennedy v. Bremerton School District***, 597 U.S. 507 (2022) — high school football coach could pray on the field after games.

Each was decided in favor of the religious party. Each took years and substantial litigation to reach. **The state actors who initiated the compulsion** — civil rights commissioners, school officials, regulatory officials — generally faced no personal consequences.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.5 Protected Rights** — expressly enumerates *"the right to freely exercise one's religion, to worship one's god (by any name or none), or to hold no religion at all."*
- **§1.10 Anarchy** — government compulsion without lawful basis (where the conduct violated the Free Exercise Clause)
- **§4.1.1** — qualified, judicial, and prosecutorial immunity all abrogated
- **§1.13 Official Intimidation / §1.12 Terrorism** — coercive regulatory or licensing action targeting a citizen's religious practice or family wellbeing

### Federal surveillance of religious practice — 2023 FBI Richmond memo

In early 2023, an internal FBI Richmond field-office document — *"Interest of Racially or Ethnically Motivated Violent Extremists in Radical-Traditionalist Catholic Ideology"* — was leaked, revealing that the field office had proposed using undercover sources to **surveil traditional Catholic parishes** based on their theological views. After public exposure, the FBI retracted the memo, and FBI Director Christopher Wray testified about it before the House Judiciary Committee.

This is precisely the kind of state-level surveillance of religious belief that the First Amendment was written to prohibit.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.5 Protected Rights** — express religious liberty
- **§1.7 Government Racketeering** — coordinated use of investigative authority to chill religious practice
- **§1.13 Official Intimidation** — surveillance and the threat of surveillance is reachable as inducing undue anxiety
- **§14.7** — federal officials engaging in religiously-targeted surveillance in Utah are reachable in Utah state court

### Federal land enforcement — particular concern in Utah

Approximately **63% of Utah** is federally owned land (BLM, USFS, NPS, BIA, military). This produces continuous tensions between Utah ranchers, miners, sportsmen, and rural communities on one hand, and federal land-management agencies on the other. National monument designations (Bears Ears, Grand Staircase-Escalante), grazing-fee disputes, sage-grouse listings, motorized-access closures, and water-rights disputes all generate enforcement actions where Utah citizens face federal officers wielding administrative authority.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.7 Government Racketeering** — coordinated use of regulatory power against ranchers, miners, or other lawful land users to extract penalties, fees, or property
- **§1.9 Treason — regulatory-warfare prong** — sustained, coordinated regulatory pressure that breaks or burdens Utah citizens' ability to use land or pursue lawful livelihood
- **§1.6 Cabalism** — small-group coordination producing unjust effect on Utah families and businesses
- **§14.7** — federal officials' conduct in Utah is reachable in Utah state court under this Act
- **§8.14 Logical Consistency Review** — federal regulations applied in Utah that conflict with the Utah Constitution or with federal-state separation principles can be subject to citizen-led review

### Second Amendment enforcement — administrative rule-making against gun owners

In recent years, the ATF has issued a series of administrative rules — pistol-brace classifications, "frame or receiver" rules, "engaged in the business" rules — that have **converted lawful gun ownership into criminal liability** without congressional action. Multiple federal courts have struck down or limited these rules (*Garland v. Cargill*, 602 U.S. ___ (2024); *Mock v. Garland*, 5th Cir. 2023; the *Bruen* line). Citizens caught by these rules during their period of enforcement, however, faced raids, arrests, and forfeitures.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.5 Protected Rights** — expressly enumerates *"the right to keep and bear arms, including the lawful ownership, possession, and carry of firearms"*
- **§1.10 Anarchy** — administrative action exceeding statutory authority is conduct outside the law
- **§15 Nullity of Repugnant Government Instruments** — *any* government instrument (warrant, regulation, agency policy) that is repugnant to the U.S. Constitution, the Utah Constitution, or this Act is **null and void within Utah**, codifying *Marbury v. Madison*'s principle that "a law repugnant to the Constitution is void"
- **§1.7 Government Racketeering** — schemes that convert lawful conduct into prosecutable offenses for revenue or power

## A through-line: federal-state federalism and citizen sovereignty

The American constitutional structure was designed around three principles the right has spent generations defending:

1. **Enumerated, limited federal powers.** The Tenth Amendment reserves to the States or to the people all powers not delegated to the federal government.
2. **Dual sovereignty.** The federal and state governments are separate sovereigns. Federal officers are not above state law; state officers are not above federal law. Both answer to citizens.
3. **The citizen as the source of consent of the governed.** Government derives its just powers from the consent of the governed.

Operation Checkmate operationalizes all three:

- **It is a state-law accountability statute** that reaches federal officers' conduct in Utah without claiming to amend federal law (§14.7).
- It **cites and relies on the dual-sovereignty doctrine** (*Lanza*, *Abbate*, *Bartkus*, *Heath*, *Gamble*) — the same doctrine the founders' constitutional structure presumed.
- It **abrogates** *In re Neagle* (1890) — the case that allowed an FBI sniper at Ruby Ridge to escape state prosecution — as inconsistent with both the Tenth Amendment and the dual-sovereignty principle.
- It places investigative and indictment authority **in the hands of citizen councils**, not in offices that have grown unaccountable.

Whether you call yourself a constitutional conservative, a libertarian, a federalist, a Tenther, a Second Amendment absolutist, a religious-liberty advocate, or simply a patriot — this bill aligns with the structural principles you have spent your life defending.

## How this bill reduces the daily anxiety of dealing with federal and state agencies

- **Real personal liability** for federal officials, not just institutional civil settlements years later from the Treasury.
- **No more immunity.** Qualified, judicial, prosecutorial, sovereign — abrogated within this Act (§4.1, §4.1.1, §15).
- **No more *Neagle*.** Federal officers can be prosecuted in Utah state court for state-law offenses, just like any other person (§14.7).
- **A way to actually prosecute.** Citizen Councils — twelve or more Utah citizens — can investigate and indict by Joint Declaration of Prosecution, without depending on the U.S. Attorney's office or the local DA (§8).
- **Repugnant agency rules are null on day one.** Any federal regulation, agency memo, or administrative practice that is repugnant to the Constitution or to this Act is null in Utah, and Utah citizens are not liable for refusing to comply (§15).
- **The right to keep and bear arms is expressly enumerated** under the protected rights of §1.5, alongside the right to freely exercise religion and the right to be a parent.
- **Forfeiture of pensions, federal benefits, and contracts** for convicted officials (§10), with no indemnification from agency funds (§1.1.2).
- **No statute of limitations** on civil or criminal actions under this title (§9.3) — federal abuses from years past remain reachable.

## A note on framing

This bill is **not** a manifesto, a militia document, or a "sovereign citizen" doctrine. **Citizens remain fully subject to the law, including this Act.** What this Act provides is a mechanism for the law to be **applied equally** to those who hold federal and state power — just as it has always been applied to those who do not. This is the restoration of constitutional structure through ordinary legal mechanisms — petitions, councils, prosecutions, and votes — exactly the tools the framers intended.

The bill targets official **misconduct** at the level of recklessness or higher (per the §2 mens rea framework). Officials — federal, state, county, or municipal — who do their jobs lawfully and within the limits of their authority have nothing to fear. **Bad actors who exceed those limits, particularly federal officials who have grown accustomed to acting without state-level accountability, finally do.**
