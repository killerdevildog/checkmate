# Why Citizens Facing Financial Hardship Should Support Operation Checkmate

> A bill written to make government accountable to the same law as everyone else — and to stop the use of poverty itself as a tool of state extraction.

If you live paycheck to paycheck, are working two jobs, are recently unemployed, are on disability or a fixed income, are a single parent, or are otherwise stretched thin financially — **you already know that government is more dangerous to you than to people who can write a check and make a problem go away.**

A traffic ticket can become a warrant. A warrant can become a jailing. A jailing can become a job loss. A job loss can become an eviction. An eviction can become a CPS investigation. Every step of that cascade has been **engineered, sometimes intentionally, into the way municipal and state systems extract revenue from working people.**

**Operation Checkmate** addresses this directly. It places real, personal civil and criminal consequences on the government officials who run those systems — and gives ordinary citizens, not the prosecutorial gatekeepers, the means to enforce them.

## Documented patterns the bill addresses

### Predatory municipal fines and fees — turning citizens into revenue

The U.S. Department of Justice, after the killing of Michael Brown, issued the **Ferguson Report (March 4, 2015)** documenting how the City of Ferguson, Missouri turned its police force into a revenue-collection arm. Findings:

- **16,000 outstanding arrest warrants** for unpaid municipal fines in a city of approximately 21,000.
- Police were directed by city leadership to **increase ticketing** to support the city budget.
- Fines stacked: a $50 traffic ticket would generate, with surcharges, late fees, "warrant recall" fees, and bench-warrant arrest costs, total liabilities of hundreds or thousands of dollars.
- Citizens unable to pay were arrested, jailed, and re-fined for failure to appear.

Ferguson is the named example. The pattern is **widespread across municipalities** — particularly small towns dependent on highway-traffic revenue.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.7 Government Racketeering** — explicitly includes *"schemes to extract civil fines, asset forfeitures, ticketing quotas, towing or impoundment fees, or similar revenue from citizens without lawful basis"* and *"coordinated harassment via repeated stops, citations, inspections, or process targeting an individual, business, or community."* Class 2 felony per instance, **Class 1 felony for patterns**.
- **§1.6 Cabalism** — small-group coordination producing unjust effect on citizens. The Ferguson scheme was exactly this: a small group of officials (city manager, finance director, police chief) coordinating to extract revenue from a population that could not push back.
- **§1.13 Official Intimidation** — repeated unsolicited contact, drawn-out proceedings, bureaucratic obstruction designed to discourage citizens from contesting fines.
- **§1.10 Anarchy** — fines and arrests imposed without lawful basis are conduct outside the law.

### Cash bail and pretrial detention — punishment by poverty

In a typical American county, the difference between sleeping in your own bed tonight and sleeping in jail for the next six weeks is **whether you can post $500 in cash**. People with means post bail and continue working, parenting, and preparing their defense. People without means lose jobs, lose housing, lose custody, and often plead guilty to crimes they didn't commit just to get out.

***ODonnell v. Harris County***, 251 F. Supp. 3d 1052 (S.D. Tex. 2017) — federal court found that Harris County, Texas's bail-bond practices systematically detained indigent misdemeanor defendants in violation of equal protection and due process. The DOJ has documented similar patterns in jurisdictions across the country.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.3 Battery (Government Context)** — unlawful detention is Battery; pretrial detention solely because the defendant cannot afford bail is reachable where the elements are met.
- **§8.14 Logical Consistency Review** — a unanimous 36-member Citizen Council can require review of any cash-bail statute or local rule that is logically inconsistent with the U.S. or Utah Constitution's equal-protection and due-process protections. *"It's the way it's always been done"* is **not** a legitimate ground for the reviewing judge to refuse engagement.
- **§1.20 Judicial Duty to Apply Mandatory Rules** — judges who routinely set unaffordable bail without the case-specific assessment required by *Bearden v. Georgia*, 461 U.S. 660 (1983), are reachable.

### Civil asset forfeiture — taking your money without charging you with a crime

**Civil forfeiture** allows police to seize cash, vehicles, or property from a citizen without ever charging them with a crime. The citizen must then sue the government — often spending more in legal fees than the property is worth — to get it back.

- **Tan Nguyen** — had $50,000 in cash seized during a traffic stop in Humboldt County, NV (2013). Never charged. Eventually got it back after years of litigation.
- **Carole Hinders** — Iowa restaurateur. The IRS seized **$33,000** from her bank account under "structuring" laws (depositing under $10,000 to avoid reporting, which was actually her bank's recommendation). Never charged. Eventually got it back after a public outcry.
- **Routine corridor enforcement on interstate highways** has produced thousands of similar cases — disproportionately affecting working-class drivers who carry cash for legitimate reasons.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.7 Government Racketeering** — explicitly covers *"civil asset forfeiture without conviction"* as a covered scheme.
- **§1.6 Cabalism** — explicitly covers *"the taking, seizure, forfeiture, encumbrance, or destruction of property in a manner that makes it difficult or impossible for a citizen to run a lawful business, earn a living, or sustain a household."*
- **§1.10 Anarchy** — seizure of property without lawful basis is conduct outside the law.
- **§9 Retroactive Civil Applicability** with **§9.3 No Statute of Limitations** — past forfeitures can still be brought as civil actions without a time bar.

### Driver's license suspensions for unpaid fines — the cycle

This is perhaps the most direct example of poverty being weaponized: a citizen gets a fine they can't pay → license suspended → can't drive to work → can't earn → can't pay → fines accumulate → license stays suspended. Many citizens then drive on a suspended license out of necessity, get pulled over, and face criminal charges.

***Robinson v. Purkey*** (M.D. Tenn. 2018) — federal court enjoined Tennessee's practice of suspending licenses for unpaid court debts without ability-to-pay determinations. Similar litigation has been brought in other states. Many jurisdictions still do this.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.10 Anarchy** — license suspensions imposed without ability-to-pay determinations, where due process required them, are conduct outside the law.
- **§1.6 Cabalism** — explicitly includes *"taking, seizure, forfeiture, encumbrance, or destruction of property in a manner that makes it difficult or impossible for a citizen to run a lawful business, earn a living, or sustain a household,"* including *"revocation, denial, or non-renewal of licenses, permits, or certifications used in trade or profession."*
- **§1.7 Government Racketeering** — schemes to extract revenue through license-suspension penalties are explicitly reachable.
- **§8.14 Logical Consistency Review** — driver's-license-suspension statutes that are logically inconsistent with constitutional protections (due process, equal protection, the right to travel) can be nullified by a unanimous 36-member Citizen Council.

### Court fees, surcharges, and payment-plan fees that compound the original fine

A $200 ticket becomes $400 with court costs, $500 with the late fee, $700 with the warrant recall, $1,000 with the bench-warrant arrest charges. Each step is a separate fee. Each accumulates interest. Each one feeds back into the courts and the police that imposed the original fine — *creating a direct conflict of interest*.

***Cain v. White***, 937 F.3d 446 (5th Cir. 2019) — affirmed that judges in Orleans Parish (New Orleans) had violated due process by imposing fines while themselves being funded by those fines. The court explicitly held that judges cannot have a financial stake in the fines they impose.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.17 Judicial Duty of Impartiality and Recusal** — a judge has an affirmative, continuing duty to recuse where the judge or the court has a *"personal, financial, professional, political, or familial interest in the outcome."* Concealing grounds for recusal is a Class 2 felony. Concealing the structural conflict (court funded from the fines it imposes) is reachable.
- **§1.7 Government Racketeering** — fee-stacking schemes used to extract revenue without lawful basis are explicitly covered.
- **§1.6 Cabalism** — small-group coordination producing financial impoverishment of citizens is reachable.

### Welfare fraud over-prosecution; corporate fraud under-prosecution

Local prosecutors aggressively pursue charges over $200 SNAP overpayments while declining to charge corporations responsible for tens of millions in wage theft, payroll fraud, or benefit-program embezzlement. The Center for Public Integrity, ProPublica, and the National Employment Law Project have documented this asymmetry repeatedly.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.8 Obstruction of Justice — Selective Non-Prosecution** — a prosecutor who fails to charge a person *"because of [their] title, status, [or] group affiliation"* — including affiliation with a corporation, business association, or industry group — commits obstruction. The class of the obstruction **mirrors the class of the underlying offense** that went uncharged. A corporate wage-theft case unprosecuted reaches the prosecutor at the corresponding class.
- **§1.7 Government Racketeering** — selective enforcement against vulnerable communities while shielding well-connected ones is exactly the conduct this section targets.

### Child support enforcement that prevents earning

A parent who falls behind on child support — often because they lost a job — faces wage garnishment, license suspension (driver's, professional, even fishing), bank-account seizure, and in many jurisdictions, jail. Each of these reduces the parent's ability to earn, making the arrears grow faster.

**Walter Scott**, killed by a North Charleston, SC officer in April 2015, ran from a traffic stop in part because he had outstanding child-support arrears and feared incarceration. The video of his shooting led to the officer's guilty plea — but the underlying dynamic, where back-child-support functioned as a pretext for the cycle of fear, is the pattern this bill addresses.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.6 Cabalism** — explicitly covers *"enforcement of any law, order, or judgment that unreasonably affects a citizen's family, including child-support... orders calibrated or enforced in a manner that makes it nearly impossible for a parent (mother or father) to thrive — as distinguished from merely 'getting by' — and that thereby damages the parent's ability to provide for, be present with, or maintain a healthy relationship with their children."*
- **§1.5 Protected Rights** — expressly enumerates the right to be a parent and the right to earn a living.
- **§8.14 Logical Consistency Review** — child-support enforcement statutes that are logically inconsistent with constitutional protections (due process, *Bearden v. Georgia* requirements, equal protection) can be nullified.

### Towing and impound predation

A car towed for an expired registration sits in a private lot accumulating storage fees until the fees exceed the car's value. The owner — often a working-class commuter for whom the car *is* the path to work — loses the car. Many jurisdictions have rules requiring affordable redemption; many do not enforce them.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.6 Cabalism** — express coverage for *"seizure or impoundment of vehicles, tools, equipment, inventory, or premises essential to a citizen's livelihood."*
- **§1.7 Government Racketeering** — where towing/impound contracts route revenue to officials or their connected businesses, the conduct is racketeering.

### Eviction proceedings without counsel

In the typical eviction, the landlord has an attorney; the tenant does not. Default judgments are routine. Tenants who do appear face hearings that last minutes, with the judge often siding reflexively with the landlord. The result: families pushed into homelessness, often without ever having a real opportunity to be heard.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.18 Judicial Duty to Engage on the Merits** — judges must address the substance of the tenant's defenses, not rubber-stamp the landlord's filings. **Class 2 felony** reckless prosecution for failure to engage; bars *"adopting a party's proposed findings or order verbatim without independent review."*
- **§1.20 Judicial Duty to Apply Mandatory Rules and Correct Errors** — failure to apply the protections of due process and any state-law tenant rights is reachable.

### Probation, parole, and electronic monitoring fees

Citizens released on probation or parole are often charged for the privilege of their own monitoring — drug-testing fees, electronic-bracelet fees, monthly supervision fees, mandatory class fees. Failure to pay can extend the supervision or send the person back to jail. The Brennan Center for Justice, the Marshall Project, and Human Rights Watch have documented this.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.7 Government Racketeering** — fee structures that extract revenue from people convicted of crimes (including those whose convictions are later vacated) without lawful basis are reachable.
- **§1.6 Cabalism** — explicitly covers patterns producing impoverishment of citizens.

### Predatory plea bargains — pleading guilty because you can't afford to fight

A defendant who cannot afford bail, who has lost work, whose family is in crisis, faces a "deal": plead guilty to a lesser charge today and go home, or sit in jail for months waiting for trial — even if you're innocent. Roughly 95% of criminal cases end in plea bargains. The Innocence Project has documented many wrongful convictions that began with a poverty-coerced plea.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.13 Official Intimidation** — coercion of a citizen into action against their interest is reachable as a Class 3 felony, escalating to Class 1 with malice.
- **§1.15 Malicious Prosecution** — continuing to prosecute a citizen the prosecutor knows or recklessly disregards as innocent is a Class 1 felony, even where the case ends in a plea.
- **§1.16 Duty to Seek Reduction or Dismissal** — Class 3 felony for failure to act on evidence that the charge does not fit; capital where failure causes the citizen's death.
- **§1.1.5 Juror Rights and Verdict Options** — the **"Unequivocally Innocent"** verdict provides citizens who do go to trial a verdict that bars retrial and triggers prosecutor review; the **"Guilty and Forgiven"** verdict allows juries to acknowledge conduct while declining to impose punishment.
- **§8.15 Conviction Review** — a unanimous 64-member Citizen Council can nullify, reduce, or extend public forgiveness for any conviction, including those entered on poverty-coerced pleas.

## A through-line: poverty has been weaponized

The pattern across all of the above is the same. **The state has discovered that poverty is profitable** — not directly to its leaders' bank accounts (usually), but to municipal budgets, to court systems funded by their own fines, to private contractors collecting forfeiture and supervision fees, and to a political ecosystem that depends on revenue extraction from people who cannot push back.

The poor cannot afford lawyers, cannot post bail, cannot challenge forfeiture, cannot afford filing fees, cannot take time off work for hearings, cannot move when a CPS case opens against them, and cannot sustain the years-long fight that even a successful claim requires. **The system runs on this asymmetry.**

Operation Checkmate shifts the asymmetry. It puts personal civil and criminal consequences on the officials who run these systems — and gives ordinary citizens, **without lawyers, without filing fees, without the cooperation of the prosecutorial offices that benefit from the status quo,** the means to investigate and force prosecution.

## How this bill reduces the daily anxiety of financial precarity

The anxiety of being financially unstable in this country is not separate from the anxiety of being a citizen subject to government. It is the same anxiety. **Money buys protection.** Operation Checkmate aims to extend that protection to citizens regardless of income.

- **No filing fees, no lawyer required** to participate in a Citizen Council under §8. Twelve citizens can investigate and indict by Joint Declaration of Prosecution.
- **No statute of limitations** on civil or criminal actions under this title (§9.3) — claims are not lost because the citizen couldn't afford to bring them when they happened.
- **Real personal liability** for the individual officials, not just institutional settlements years later.
- **No more immunity.** Judicial, prosecutorial, qualified, sovereign — all abrogated within this Act (§4.1, §4.1.1, §15).
- **Forfeiture of pensions, benefits, and contracts** for convicted officials (§10) — and **no indemnification from entity funds** (§1.1.2). Bad actors face their own consequences, not the taxpayers'.
- **Right to be a parent** and **right to earn a living** are expressly enumerated (§1.5) — and offenses that interfere with either are reachable.
- **Conviction Review** for the wrongfully convicted, including poverty-coerced pleas (§8.15).
- **Logical Consistency Review** that can nullify cash-bail statutes, license-suspension statutes, fine-stacking ordinances, and other provisions that turn poverty into a crime (§8.14).
- **The cost-shifting and access provisions** of §14.4 ensure that citizens challenging adverse amendments are not deterred by bonds, fees, or costs.

## A note on framing

This bill is **not** about working-class citizens vs. anyone. The provisions described above are not "poverty provisions" — they are accountability provisions that apply to every citizen, equally. They matter especially to citizens whose lack of resources has, in the existing system, translated into lack of legal protection.

The bill targets official **misconduct** at the level of recklessness or higher (per the §2 mens rea framework). Officials who do their jobs lawfully and in good faith have nothing to fear. **The system that uses citizens as revenue, that punishes poverty as if it were a crime, has long enjoyed protection it did not earn. This bill withdraws that protection.**
