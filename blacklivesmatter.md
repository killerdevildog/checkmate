# Why Black Citizens Should Support Operation Checkmate

> A bill written to make government accountable to the same law as everyone else — with specific, personal consequences for officials who target citizens based on who they are.

If you are Black in America, you already know that **interactions with government are often life-altering and sometimes life-ending in ways they aren't for other citizens**. Traffic stops. Welfare checks. Custody investigations. Drug enforcement. Schools. Prosecutorial charging decisions. Jury verdicts. Each of these has produced a catalog of documented patterns of harm — many of them with names, dates, and outcomes that are public record.

**Operation Checkmate** doesn't single out the Black community for special protection. It makes officials accountable to every citizen, equally. But because the Black community has carried a long, named, and continuing history of specific patterns of government abuse — and because the foundational "color of law" cases at the heart of this bill (*Classic*, *Screws*, *Monroe*) were all about Black civil rights — this bill matters here in particular ways.

## Documented patterns the bill addresses

### Police use of lethal and excessive force

**George Floyd** was killed in Minneapolis on May 25, 2020 by Officer Derek Chauvin, who knelt on his neck for over nine minutes while Mr. Floyd said *"I can't breathe"* and bystanders pleaded with the officer to stop. Chauvin was eventually convicted of murder — but only after a global protest movement, and only after an entire system that initially declined to prosecute had been forced to act.

**Breonna Taylor** was killed in her own apartment in Louisville on March 13, 2020 during a no-knock raid. The warrant rested on falsified information. No officer was convicted for her death directly; one was convicted of falsifying the warrant.

**Tamir Rice**, 12 years old, was shot by Cleveland police in November 2014 within roughly two seconds of officers arriving at the park where he was playing. No officer was charged.

**Atatiana Jefferson** was shot through her own window in Fort Worth, Texas in October 2019 during a wellness check. The officer was convicted of manslaughter.

**Botham Jean** was killed in his own apartment in Dallas in September 2018 by an off-duty officer who entered the wrong unit. She was convicted of murder.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.3 Battery (Government Context)** — Class 2 felony, or higher depending on severity, for actually depriving, restraining, harming, or violating a citizen's rights, liberty, or property through direct action.
- **§1.10 Anarchy** — a stop, search, raid, or use of force without lawful basis is conduct outside the law, personally liable.
- **§4.1 / §4.1.1** — qualified immunity is **abrogated** for any conduct under this title; the "clearly established law" inquiry that has shielded officers does not apply.
- **§11 Death of a Citizen Resulting from Violation** — where the violation results in the citizen's death, the offense is subject to the maximum penalty available, with §6 enhancements; capital classification where a citizen's suicide is a direct or proximate result.
- **§10 Forfeiture** — convicted officers forfeit pensions, state-funded benefits, and any government contracts. Their liability cannot be paid out of the city's general fund (no indemnification from entity funds — §1.1.2).
- **§8 Citizen Council** — when local prosecutors decline to charge their own officers (a documented pattern), twelve or more citizens can investigate, file a Joint Declaration of Prosecution, and force the County Prosecutor or the Office of the Governor to actually prosecute.

### Failure to prosecute — selective non-prosecution of officer misconduct

The pattern after a police killing or beating of a Black citizen has too often been the same: an internal investigation, a quiet exoneration, the same prosecutor's office that works with the same police department declining to charge, and a years-long fight to even get to a grand jury. Where charges have been filed, they have often come only after sustained public pressure.

**Eric Garner** — killed by NYPD officer's chokehold in July 2014. Officer was not criminally charged at the state level for years; eventually fired five years later.

**Philando Castile** — killed during a traffic stop in Falcon Heights, Minnesota in July 2016 while reaching for his ID, as he had announced. The officer was charged but acquitted.

**Daniel Prude** — died in Rochester, NY in March 2020 after officers held him face-down on the ground with a spit hood during a mental-health crisis. A grand jury declined to indict.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.8 Obstruction of Justice — Selective Non-Prosecution** — a government official who fails to prosecute, arrest, or charge a private person *because of that person's title, status, or affiliation* (state agent, professional association, fraternal order, etc.) commits **obstruction**. The class of the obstruction **mirrors the class of the underlying offense** — meaning if the unprosecuted conduct is a homicide, the prosecutor's failure to charge is reachable at the corresponding class.
- **§1.14 Prosecutorial Duty to Disclose Adverse Evidence** — prosecutors must disclose to the **jury** all evidence that would render the prosecution's claim false or establish innocence.
- **§1.15 Malicious Prosecution** — Class 1 felony for continuing to prosecute a citizen the prosecutor knows is innocent.
- **§1.16 Duty to Seek Reduction or Dismissal** — Class 3 felony for failure to act when evidence collapses, with capital elevation if failure results in death.
- **§8 Citizen Council** — twelve citizens can compel prosecution by Joint Declaration of Prosecution; the prosecuting office's discretion to decline is **limited** to demonstrable conflict of interest or facial factual insufficiency. "Professional courtesy," "institutional reluctance," and "general prosecutorial discretion" are explicitly disallowed grounds.

### Wrongful convictions — and the right to be released

The National Registry of Exonerations and the Innocence Project have documented that **Black Americans are roughly seven times more likely to be wrongfully convicted of murder** than white Americans, and similarly disproportionately wrongfully convicted of sexual assault and drug crimes. Every name on that list is a person who lost years — often decades — to a system that did not act on the truth.

**Walter McMillian** — Alabama, sentenced to death in 1988 for a murder he did not commit. Exonerated in 1993 after Bryan Stevenson and the Equal Justice Initiative uncovered prosecutorial misconduct, witness coaching, and suppressed evidence.

**Anthony Ray Hinton** — Alabama, spent **30 years on death row** for a murder he did not commit. Exonerated in 2015 after the U.S. Supreme Court vacated his conviction in *Hinton v. Alabama*, 571 U.S. 263 (2014), based on ineffective assistance of counsel.

**The Central Park Five** — Antron McCray, Kevin Richardson, Yusef Salaam, Raymond Santana, and Korey Wise. Wrongfully convicted in 1990 of the assault and rape of a jogger in Central Park. Their convictions were vacated in 2002 after the actual perpetrator confessed and DNA matched him.

**Crosley Green** — Florida, convicted of murder in 1990 and sentenced to death; sentence reduced to life. After three decades of litigation showing prosecutors withheld exculpatory evidence, his conviction was overturned in 2021.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.14 Prosecutorial Duty to Disclose Adverse Evidence** — codifies and **expands** *Brady v. Maryland*, 373 U.S. 83 (1963), by requiring affirmative disclosure to the **jury**, not merely to defense counsel, and by attaching **criminal liability** to violations rather than only the appellate remedy of vacatur.
- **§1.1.4 Mandatory Juror Documentation** — every convicting juror must produce a written record addressing every item of evidence and explaining the reasoning for finding each element beyond a reasonable doubt. Without this record, the conviction is not legitimate and is subject to vacatur. This addresses convictions returned by juries that did not engage with the evidence.
- **§1.1.5 Juror Rights and Verdict Options** — six verdict options including **"Unequivocally Innocent"** (an affirmative finding of actual innocence) and **"Guilty and Forgiven"** (jury nullification with affirmative forgiveness).
- **§8.15 Conviction Review** — a unanimous Citizen Council of **64 or more citizens** can nullify a conviction, reduce it, spare a person from the death penalty, or extend public forgiveness restoring civil and political rights and barring collateral consequences. This applies to convictions returned at any time, including before the Act's effective date — meaning historical wrongful convictions remain reachable.
- **§9 Retroactive Civil Applicability** with **§9.3 No Statute of Limitations** — civil claims against prosecutors, judges, and officers responsible for wrongful convictions can be brought without time bar; survive against the actor's estate.
- **§10 Forfeiture** — convicted officials forfeit pensions, benefits, and contracts; cannot be indemnified.

### Selective enforcement and the drug war

Black Americans are arrested for marijuana possession at roughly 3.6 times the rate of white Americans despite roughly equal usage rates (ACLU, *A Tale of Two Countries*, 2020). The federal sentencing disparity between crack and powder cocaine — 100:1 from 1986 to 2010, reduced to 18:1 by the Fair Sentencing Act — translated directly into disproportionate Black incarceration. Stop-and-frisk in New York City was struck down as unconstitutional in *Floyd v. City of New York*, 959 F. Supp. 2d 540 (S.D.N.Y. 2013), with evidence that Black and Latino men comprised more than 80% of stops despite being a minority of the population.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.7 Government Racketeering** — combinations of public officials using authority to intimidate or compel unlawful payment from citizens; selective enforcement that targets specific groups for revenue extraction (ticketing quotas, civil-fine schemes, asset forfeiture without conviction); coordinated regulatory harassment. Class 2 felony per instance, **Class 1 felony** for patterns.
- **§1.6 Cabalism** — small-group coordination producing unjust effect on citizens, including disproportionate enforcement that produces fear, profit, or unjust treatment.
- **§1.10 Anarchy** — stops, frisks, or searches without lawful basis.
- **§1.13 Official Intimidation** — pretextual stops, "you fit the description" stops, repeated unsolicited contact, drawn-out investigations to wear down resistance — Class 3 felony, escalating to **§1.12 Terrorism** (Class 1 felony) for sustained patterns.

### Civil asset forfeiture

Civil asset forfeiture — where police seize cash, vehicles, or property from a citizen without ever charging them with a crime — has a documented disparate impact on Black communities, particularly along corridor highways used for drug interdiction.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.7 Government Racketeering** — explicitly includes "asset forfeiture without conviction" and "ticketing quotas" as covered conduct.
- **§1.6 Cabalism** — explicitly includes "the taking, seizure, forfeiture, encumbrance, or destruction of property in a manner that makes it difficult or impossible for a citizen to run a lawful business, earn a living, or sustain a household."
- **§1.10 Anarchy** — seizure of property without lawful basis is conduct outside the law.
- **§9 Retroactive Civil Applicability** — civil claims for past forfeitures can be brought without time bar.

### Family-court and CPS disproportion

Black mothers are roughly twice as likely to have their children removed by child-welfare agencies as white mothers, often based on subjective assessments of "fitness" rather than concrete safety concerns. Once a child is removed, the path to reunification is substantially longer and outcomes are worse.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.5 Protected Rights** expressly includes *"the right to be a parent — to raise, educate, discipline, and direct the upbringing of one's children, free from unwarranted state interference."*
- **§1.6 Cabalism** — its "unjust effect" definition includes *"enforcement of any law, order, or judgment that unreasonably affects a citizen's family — including child-support orders calibrated or enforced in a manner that makes it nearly impossible for a parent to thrive."* Reaches CPS removals not supported by evidence.
- **§1.12 Terrorism (Government Context)** — explicitly includes *"explicit or implicit threats to take a citizen's children or otherwise interfere with the parent-child relationship"* — Class 1 felony, capital where it results in death.
- **§4.1.1** — judicial immunity abrogated; family-court judges who rule on the basis of identity rather than fitness are personally liable.
- **§1.17 Judicial Duty of Impartiality and Recusal** — a judge with personal bias must recuse on the record without being asked.

### Police failure to investigate when Black citizens are victims

The same patterns of unequal enforcement appear in the **other direction** — when Black citizens are victims and reports go unaddressed. Missing-Black-women cases, hate crimes, and assaults in poor and working-class Black communities are too often investigated less aggressively than the same crimes in other neighborhoods.

**How the bill reaches this:**

- **§1.8 Obstruction of Justice — Selective Non-Prosecution** — failing to charge or investigate because of the **victim's** identity (or to protect a perpetrator's status) is reachable obstruction.
- **§8 Citizen Council** — citizens can compel investigation and prosecution where the local office has failed.

## A through-line: the foundational "color of law" cases were about Black civil rights

This bill's central trigger — *"under color of law"* — was defined by the Supreme Court in cases that arose from civil rights violations against Black Americans:

- **L.1 — *United States v. Classic*, 313 U.S. 299 (1941)** — voting rights case in Louisiana; Black voters whose ballots were intentionally miscounted by election commissioners.
- **L.2 — *Screws v. United States*, 325 U.S. 91 (1945)** — Sheriff Screws and two officers beat **Robert Hall**, a Black man, to death in police custody in Baker County, Georgia. The case established that an officer's actions in excess of state law are still actions "under color of" state law.
- **L.3 — *Monroe v. Pape*, 365 U.S. 167 (1961)** — Chicago police officers raided **James Monroe**'s home (a Black family's home) without a warrant, made the family stand naked in the living room while they searched, and detained Mr. Monroe for ten hours without charges. The Supreme Court held that § 1983 reached this conduct as "under color of law."

These are the foundational color-of-law cases. The doctrine that anchors most of Operation Checkmate's substantive offenses was built **on the bodies and homes of Black Americans**. The bill picks up where those cases left off, making the doctrine criminally enforceable against the officials who continue to abuse it.

## How this bill reduces the daily anxiety of dealing with government

The anxiety that comes with being pulled over, with answering the door, with a child being interviewed at school, with a spouse being detained — it is rational. It is the result of repeated, documented experiences with officials who, in the moment, may use their authority unfairly without real consequence to themselves.

Operation Checkmate addresses this directly:

- **Real personal liability** for individual officials, not just institutional settlements years later.
- **No more immunity.** Judicial, prosecutorial, qualified, sovereign — all abrogated within this Act (§4.1, §4.1.1, §15).
- **A way to actually prosecute** through the Citizen Council mechanism (§§8.1–8.5) — independent of the offices that may be the source of the problem.
- **Six verdict options** for juries — including **"Unequivocally Innocent"** (which bars retrial and triggers prosecutor review) and **"Guilty and Forgiven"** (jury nullification with affirmative forgiveness).
- **Mandatory juror documentation** addressing the bias-by-silence pattern that produces wrongful convictions (§1.1.4).
- **Conviction Review** for the wrongfully convicted — a parallel safeguard for cases the appellate system has failed (§8.15).
- **Forfeiture of pensions, benefits, contracts** for convicted officials (§10) — and **no indemnification from entity funds** (§1.1.2). Bad actors face their own consequences, not the taxpayers'.
- **The right to be a parent** is expressly enumerated (§1.5).
- **No statute of limitations** on civil or criminal actions under this title (§9.3).

## A note on framing

This bill is **not** about Black citizens vs. police, or vs. the government. The provisions described above are not "Black community provisions" — they are accountability provisions that apply to every citizen, equally. They matter especially to citizens whose history with government has included specific, documented, named patterns of harm.

The bill targets official **misconduct** at the level of recklessness or higher (per the §2 mens rea framework). Officials who do their jobs lawfully and in good faith have nothing to fear. **Bad actors in any profession make the whole profession look corrupt; this bill clears the field.**
