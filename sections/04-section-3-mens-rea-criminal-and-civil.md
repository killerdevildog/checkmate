## Section 3 — Mens Rea: Criminal and Civil

### 3.1 Two Tracks; Same Culpability Words

This Act uses **two tracks of mens rea**:

1. **Criminal mens rea** — the mental state required to impose criminal liability, criminal classification, and criminal penalties.
2. **Civil mens rea** — the mental state required to impose civil liability, damages, forfeiture, structural remedies, removal remedies, or other non-criminal relief.

The words used for both tracks are the same: **negligent**, **reckless**, **intentional**, and **malicious**. The meaning of each word is the same in criminal and civil proceedings. What changes is the consequence and the burden of proof, not the definition of the mental state.

#### 3.1.1 Reasonable Defined — Government Actor

For purposes of any section of this Act that uses **reasonable** in relation to a **government actor**, **reasonable** means logical, right-minded, constitutionally aware, and capable of checking whether the actor's conduct is actually lawful. Reasonable does **not** mean ordinary office custom, common departmental practice, typical agency behavior, status quo operation, or what most government actors actually do in the same position.

This definition of **reasonable government actor** is separate from the ordinary **reasonable person** standard. A reasonable person standard asks what an ordinary person in the citizen's position would understand, fear, perceive, or do. A reasonable government actor standard asks what a government actor exercising state power actually should have known, checked, perceived, or done because of that actor's office, oath, authority, training, access to law, and constitutional duty.

For purposes of mens rea, **reasonable** means the standard of a government actor who actually should have known the law, duty, risk, contradiction, or constitutional limit because:

- The actor exercised state power over a citizen;
- The actor had an oath, office, role, training, authority, access to law, access to policy, or access to supervision sufficient to check the legality of the act;
- The actor had a duty to compare the contemplated enforcement, order, policy, practice, or omission against this Act, the Constitution of the United States, the Utah Constitution, and any controlling law;
- The illegality, contradiction, risk, or duty was knowable by reason, logic, ordinary legal checking, the nature of the office, or the obviousness of objective reality.

It is **not** a defense that no actual officer, judge, prosecutor, agency employee, or government actor in the same office commonly performs that constitutional check. By the nature of the word, **reasonable** means logical, right-minded, and grounded in lawful thinking; it does not mean the current norm, the status quo, or whatever the office usually does. Policy, training custom, office habit, supervisor instruction, or lack of a real-world example does not define what is reasonable under this Act. A government actor who enforces law has an affirmative duty to check whether the enforcement is actually lawful under supreme law, not merely authorized by statute, rule, policy, or habit.

**Reliance on Supreme Court precedent alone is not reasonable where the precedent is logically inconsistent with supreme law, this Act, or other Supreme Court doctrine.** A government actor may use judicial precedent as evidence of legal reasoning, but may not treat a case as a substitute for independent constitutional checking where the doctrine conflicts with the principle that government power is defined and limited.

This rule is necessary because the Supreme Court has itself used conflicting descriptions of government power. For example:

- In *Marbury v. Madison*, 5 U.S. 137 (1803), the Court stated that government powers are **"defined and limited."**
- In *McCulloch v. Maryland*, 17 U.S. 316 (1819), the Court stated that the federal government is **"one of enumerated powers."**
- In *House v. Mayes*, 219 U.S. 270 (1911), the Court stated that state police power **"exists independently of, the federal Constitution."**
- In *Noble State Bank v. Haskell*, 219 U.S. 104 (1911), the Court stated that police power **"extends to all the great public needs."**
- In *Bacon v. Walker*, 204 U.S. 311 (1907), the Court stated that police power reaches **"the greatest welfare of the state."**

These statements cannot all be used as unrestricted grants of authority at the same time. A reasonable government actor must resolve any such conflict in favor of the written Constitution, this Act, protected rights, due process, and the limitation of government power, not in favor of broad police-power language, office convenience, or institutional habit.

### 3.2 Criminal Mens Rea

For criminal enforcement under this Act, the culpability levels are:

1. **Negligent** — the actor failed to exercise reasonable care under the reasonable-government-actor definition of Section 3.1.1 and failed to perceive a substantial risk that the actor actually should have perceived by reason, logic, office, training, authority, circumstances, constitutional duty, or obviousness of objective reality.
2. **Reckless** — the actor consciously disregarded a substantial and unjustifiable risk, ignored a risk that was obvious from the facts, or proceeded while aware that the conduct might violate law, rights, duty, truth, or due process.
3. **Intentional** — the actor acted consciously, purposefully, or deliberately, or acted with awareness that the result was substantially certain to follow.
4. **Malicious** — the actor acted intentionally plus with ill will, hatred, evil motive, retaliatory purpose, desire to punish, desire to intimidate, desire to conceal wrongdoing, or desire to harm a citizen.

Unless a specific offense states a different minimum mental state, the default minimum mental state for criminal liability is **reckless**. Negligent conduct under color of authority may still be criminal where this Act expressly classifies negligence, failure to serve, infractions, or negligent official conduct as criminally enforceable.

**Criminal Classification:**

- Negligent criminal conduct is classified as stated in the specific offense, and where no specific classification is provided, as an **infraction** or **Class 1 misdemeanor** under the negligence rules of this Act.
- Reckless criminal conduct uses the baseline classification stated in the offense.
- Intentional criminal conduct elevates the offense by one tier unless the specific section provides a different classification.
- Malicious criminal conduct elevates the offense by two tiers and triggers maximum aggravation, including capital classification where this Act authorizes capital treatment.

### 3.3 Civil Mens Rea

For civil enforcement under this Act, the same culpability levels apply:

1. **Negligent** — the actor failed to exercise reasonable care under the reasonable-government-actor definition of Section 3.1.1 and failed to perceive a substantial risk that the actor actually should have perceived by reason, logic, office, training, authority, circumstances, constitutional duty, or obviousness of objective reality.
2. **Reckless** — the actor consciously disregarded a substantial and unjustifiable risk, ignored a risk that was obvious from the facts, or proceeded while aware that the conduct might violate law, rights, duty, truth, or due process.
3. **Intentional** — the actor acted consciously, purposefully, or deliberately, or acted with awareness that the result was substantially certain to follow.
4. **Malicious** — the actor acted intentionally plus with ill will, hatred, evil motive, retaliatory purpose, desire to punish, desire to intimidate, desire to conceal wrongdoing, or desire to harm a citizen.

Civil mens rea supports civil liability, damages, restitution, forfeiture, removal from office, structural reform, injunctive relief, declaratory relief, and any other civil remedy authorized by this Act.

**Civil Consequences:**

- Negligent civil conduct is actionable where it affects a citizen's rights, liberty, property, safety, family, livelihood, due process, access to justice, or access to government process.
- Reckless, intentional, and malicious civil conduct support enhanced civil remedies, structural remedies, forfeiture, and removal remedies where authorized by this Act.
- Civil liability may proceed even where criminal prosecution is not brought, is declined, or does not result in conviction.

### 3.4 Burden and Standard of Proof

- In criminal prosecutions, the prosecution must prove the offense elements and required criminal mens rea **beyond a reasonable doubt**.
- In civil actions, the claimant must prove the offense elements and required civil mens rea by a **preponderance of the evidence**, unless a specific section requires a higher civil standard.
- The same conduct may satisfy both criminal mens rea and civil mens rea. A criminal acquittal does not bar civil liability where the civil standard is met.

#### 3.4.1 Beyond a Reasonable Doubt Defined

For purposes of this Act, **beyond a reasonable doubt** means proof that leaves the trier of fact firmly convinced that every element of the offense and the required criminal mens rea are true.

A doubt is **reasonable** when it arises from reason, logic, evidence, lack of evidence, contradiction, credibility problems, constitutional irregularity, unreliable procedure, or any other fact that would cause a logical and right-minded person to hesitate before authorizing criminal punishment.

A doubt is **not reasonable** merely because it is imaginary, speculative, invented to avoid an unpleasant conclusion, based on sympathy for a government actor, based on hostility toward a citizen, or based on a belief that government actors should be protected from accountability.

The phrase **beyond a reasonable doubt** uses the ordinary trier-of-fact standard. It is separate from the **reasonable government actor** definition in Section 3.1.1. Section 3.1.1 defines what a government actor actually should have known or done; this subsection defines the level of proof required before criminal punishment may be imposed.

### 3.5 No Lesser Mental State as a Defense

> Lack of a higher mental state is **not a defense** to a lower one. A government actor who proves they did not act maliciously is still liable at the intentional, reckless, or negligent level if those elements are met.

A defendant cannot escape liability by establishing only that they did not act maliciously, or only that they did not act intentionally. The Council, prosecutor, claimant, or trier of fact may charge, plead, prove, and find any level supported by the evidence.

### 3.6 Specific Offenses May Require Higher Mental States

Some substantive offenses in this title, by their nature, require a higher minimum mental state as set out in their respective sections. Where a specific offense states a higher minimum mental state, that specific requirement controls for that offense.

### 3.7 Office, Policy, and Orders Are Not a Defense at Any Level

Consistent with Sections 1.10 and 2.A:

- **Internal policy** is not a defense at any mental state level
- **"Following orders"** is not a defense at any mental state level
- **Office, title, or status** is not a defense at any mental state level
- The doctrine of **qualified, judicial, prosecutorial, sovereign, legislative, executive, or governmental immunity** does not apply at any mental state level
