# Why Operation Checkmate Is Not a Bill of Attainder

A foreseeable objection to Operation Checkmate is that, by focusing on government actors and abrogating the immunity doctrines that have protected them, the Act amounts to a **bill of attainder** prohibited by the United States Constitution (Article I, §9, cl. 3 and Article I, §10, cl. 1) and by the Utah Constitution (Article I, §18).

It does not. The objection misstates what a bill of attainder is, misreads how the Act is structured, and ignores more than 150 years of parallel criminal statutes that have stood — unchallenged on attainder grounds — against the same kind of objection.

This document sets out the legal doctrine, applies the operative test, and shows by direct comparison to existing criminal code that Operation Checkmate is no more a bill of attainder than a DUI statute, a perjury statute, an embezzlement statute, or 18 U.S.C. § 242 — the federal civil-rights criminal provision that has reached "whoever, under color of any law" deprives a citizen of rights since the Reconstruction era.

---

## 1. What a Bill of Attainder Actually Is

A bill of attainder is **a legislative act that inflicts punishment on specific named persons, or on easily ascertainable members of a class, without a judicial trial**.

The classical definition comes from *United States v. Lovett*, 328 U.S. 303, 315 (1946):

> "Legislative acts, no matter what their form, that apply either to named individuals or to easily ascertainable members of a group in such a way as to inflict punishment on them without a judicial trial are bills of attainder prohibited by the Constitution."

The historical concern was English Parliament passing bills naming particular persons — by name — and pronouncing them outlaws, traitors, or felons by legislative fiat, stripping their lives, liberty, or property without ever convening a court. The Bill of Attainder Clauses exist to prevent that specific abuse: **legislatures using lawmaking power to convict and punish, bypassing the judicial branch entirely**.

The leading modern cases applying the doctrine are:

- **Cummings v. Missouri**, 71 U.S. (4 Wall.) 277 (1867) — a state oath required of priests, lawyers, and teachers to disclaim past Confederate sympathy was an attainder because it functioned as legislative punishment for past identity without trial.
- **Ex parte Garland**, 71 U.S. (4 Wall.) 333 (1867) — companion to *Cummings*, striking down a similar oath imposed on federal attorneys.
- **United States v. Brown**, 381 U.S. 437 (1965) — invalidated a federal statute making it a crime for members of the Communist Party to serve as labor-union officers, because the law identified its targets by past membership rather than by conduct.
- **Nixon v. Administrator of General Services**, 433 U.S. 425 (1977) — articulated a functional test for "punishment," looking at (a) the traditional forms of punishment, (b) whether the law functionally operates as punishment, and (c) whether the legislative record shows punitive intent.
- **Selective Service System v. Minnesota Public Interest Research Group**, 468 U.S. 841 (1984) — refined the test into the three elements now applied: **specificity**, **punishment**, and **lack of judicial trial**.

The three-element test, in operative form, asks:

1. **Specificity.** Does the law identify the persons it reaches by name, by past identity, or by an ascertainable group defined by attributes the persons cannot change?
2. **Punishment.** Does the law itself inflict punishment, as understood by tradition (loss of life, liberty, property, employment, status) or by function (operating in a way that historically constitutes punishment)?
3. **Lack of Judicial Trial.** Does the law impose that punishment without the protections of a judicial proceeding — without indictment, without trial, without proof, without the right to confront witnesses?

A statute is a bill of attainder only if **all three** elements are present. Operation Checkmate fails the test on **every one of them**.

---

## 2. The Three-Element Test Applied to Operation Checkmate

### 2.1 Specificity — The Act Does Not Identify Persons. It Identifies Conduct.

Operation Checkmate names no person. It identifies no specific currently-serving official. It strips no one's status by legislative declaration. It defines categories of **conduct** — assault under color of authority, battery, carceralism, malicious prosecution, judicial imposture, terrorism, official intimidation, cabalism, and the rest of the umbrella offenses — and provides that **whoever performs the conduct** is liable.

This is exactly the structure of every ordinary criminal statute. The murder statute does not target "murderers as a class." It defines the conduct of murder and reaches whoever commits it. The bank-robbery statute does not target "people predisposed to rob banks." It defines the conduct and reaches whoever commits it. The Act follows the same structure.

The "government actor" or "under color of authority" terminology used throughout the Act is **not** a class-identifier of persons targeted by the law. It is a **conduct element** — a specification of the kind of action the offense requires. The conduct elements answer the question *what makes this offense distinct from generally applicable criminal law*. They do not answer the question *which persons does the law target*. (See section 3 below for the comparison to parallel statutes.)

The class the Act reaches is defined entirely by **future conduct subject to the actor's choice**. A government actor — or any private actor who acts under color of authority — who chooses not to commit the conduct the Act defines is not reached by the Act. The class is therefore not an "ascertainable group" in the attainder sense; it is the open-ended class of future actors who will choose to commit the defined conduct.

The Supreme Court has been clear that this is not specificity in the attainder sense. *De Veau v. Braisted*, 363 U.S. 144, 160 (1960), upheld a New York law barring convicted felons from union office, holding that legislative disqualification based on conduct subject to choice — rather than on past identity or class membership — is not a bill of attainder. The Act here is even further from attainder than the statute upheld in *De Veau*: the Act reaches the conduct itself, not derivative status flowing from prior conviction.

### 2.2 Punishment — Imposed Only After Trial, Never by the Act Itself

The Act inflicts no punishment by its own operation. It does not declare any current official's office forfeit by force of the statute. It does not strip pensions, end careers, impose fines, or take liberty merely by being enacted.

Every consequence under the Act is imposed only after a citizen, prosecutor, Citizen Council, or court applies the operative provisions to a specific actor's specific conduct, on a developed record, with the ordinary protections of a criminal proceeding. The Act creates the **legal predicate** under which prosecution may proceed; it does not perform the prosecution itself.

Bills of attainder, by contrast, *are* the punishment. The English Parliament passing a bill that "John of Lancaster is hereby declared a traitor and his lands forfeit" inflicts the punishment by the act of passage. Operation Checkmate does no such thing for any actor.

### 2.3 Lack of Judicial Trial — The Act Demands a Trial. It Does Not Replace One.

Every offense the Act defines is reached through ordinary criminal process: investigation, charging, indictment or Citizen Council Joint Declaration, arraignment, discovery, motion practice, trial by jury under the Sixth Amendment and the Utah Constitution, the right to counsel, the right to confront witnesses, the right against self-incrimination, the right to remain silent, the right to compulsory process, and the right to appeal.

The Citizen Council mechanism in Section 9 is **not** a substitute for judicial trial — it is a substitute for the prosecutorial discretion that has historically refused to charge officials. The Joint Declaration of Prosecution issued by twelve citizens has the legal force of a grand-jury indictment; what follows is a full criminal prosecution in court. No actor under this Act is punished by the operation of the statute. Every actor reached by the Act is punished, if at all, by the verdict of a jury after a trial conducted under the ordinary constitutional protections owed to any criminal defendant.

This is the opposite of what a bill of attainder does. The Act expands access to the judicial process. It does not bypass it.

## 3. Comparison to Parallel Criminal Statutes — The Acid Test

The simplest way to see that Operation Checkmate is not a bill of attainder is to compare it to existing criminal laws that share the structural feature objected to — a "specific-actor" element built into the offense — and that have stood for decades or centuries without ever being thought to raise an attainder problem.

### 3.1 Driving Under the Influence

A DUI statute makes it a crime to **operate a vehicle while impaired**. The offense has a specific-actor element: a person who is not operating a vehicle cannot commit DUI. The statute "targets" only those who operate vehicles while impaired.

Nobody calls a DUI statute a bill of attainder against drivers. The reason is straightforward: the operating-a-vehicle element is part of *what the offense is*. It distinguishes DUI from public intoxication, from drug possession, from any other offense the same person might commit while not behind the wheel. It clarifies *the conduct the offense reaches*. It does not target "drivers as a class."

Operation Checkmate's "under color of authority" element functions identically. It is part of what distinguishes battery-under-color-of-authority from generally applicable assault. It clarifies the conduct the offense reaches. It does not target "government employees as a class."

### 3.2 Perjury

The perjury statute reaches a person who, **under oath**, knowingly makes a materially false statement. A person not under oath cannot commit perjury, no matter what they say. The offense has an actor-and-circumstance element: the actor must be a person who has been placed under oath.

This is not an attainder against witnesses. The under-oath element is constitutive of the offense. It defines the conduct. It says nothing about the legislative targeting of any class.

### 3.3 Embezzlement and Breach of Fiduciary Duty

Embezzlement reaches a person entrusted with the property of another who converts that property to their own use. A person without fiduciary access cannot commit embezzlement; the offense is structurally available only to those in a fiduciary position.

Nobody treats embezzlement statutes as bills of attainder against treasurers, accountants, lawyers, brokers, or anyone else with fiduciary responsibility. The fiduciary element is part of the offense, not a targeting of the class.

### 3.4 Tax Evasion

The federal tax-evasion statute reaches a person who **willfully attempts to evade or defeat a tax**. A person without a tax obligation cannot commit tax evasion. The offense has a specific-actor element — being a taxpayer with an obligation — that is constitutive, not targeting.

### 3.5 Securities Fraud

The federal securities-fraud statutes reach persons who, **in connection with the purchase or sale of any security**, engage in defined fraudulent conduct. A person not connected with securities transactions cannot commit securities fraud. The offense has a circumstance element — securities-connected conduct — that defines the offense.

### 3.6 The Closest Parallel — 18 U.S.C. § 242

The most direct parallel to Operation Checkmate is the federal criminal civil-rights statute, **18 U.S.C. § 242**, which provides in operative part:

> "Whoever, **under color of any law, statute, ordinance, regulation, or custom**, willfully subjects any person ... to the deprivation of any rights, privileges, or immunities secured or protected by the Constitution or laws of the United States ... shall be fined under this title or imprisoned not more than one year, or both."

This statute has been on the books, in its current form, since the Civil Rights Act of 1866 (then 14 Stat. 27 §2), with the "under color of any law" language tracing to that Reconstruction-era enactment. It has been amended and reaffirmed across the intervening 150+ years. It has been applied against thousands of state and federal officials. It has been before the Supreme Court repeatedly — *Screws v. United States*, 325 U.S. 91 (1945); *United States v. Price*, 383 U.S. 787 (1966); *United States v. Lanier*, 520 U.S. 259 (1997); and many others — and has been **upheld** in every constitutional challenge.

**Nobody has ever held that 18 U.S.C. § 242 is a bill of attainder against government officials.** Nobody has ever seriously argued it. The "under color of any law" element is constitutive of the offense. It defines the conduct the statute reaches. It is not a targeting of officials as a class.

Operation Checkmate uses the same constitutive element with the same legal effect. If 18 U.S.C. § 242 is not a bill of attainder — and it is not — then Operation Checkmate's parallel use of the same element is not a bill of attainder either.

### 3.7 Honest Services Fraud — 18 U.S.C. § 1346

The federal honest-services-fraud statute reaches schemes to deprive another of the intangible right of honest services. It is applied principally against **public officials and their private agents** — a class even narrower than the Act's. It has stood since 1988. It has been the subject of repeated constitutional challenges (most prominently *Skilling v. United States*, 561 U.S. 358 (2010), which limited but did not invalidate the statute). It has never been struck down as a bill of attainder.

## 4. The "Under Color of Authority" Element Reaches Private Actors Too

The misconception that Operation Checkmate targets "government employees as a class" further breaks down on the fact that the **under-color-of-authority element is not restricted to actual government employees**.

A private citizen who impersonates a police officer and effects the conduct the Act defines — pulls a person over, conducts a search, takes a child, imposes a citation, asserts state-like authority to deprive a citizen of liberty or property — is reachable under this Act. The private impersonator's conduct **is** "under color of authority" within the meaning of the Act, because the element looks to the apparent exercise of state power, not to the actor's W-2.

The federal courts have applied 18 U.S.C. § 242 to private actors who jointly acted with government officials, who acted with apparent state authority, or who otherwise functioned as instruments of state power, even where the private actor held no government office. *Adickes v. S. H. Kress & Co.*, 398 U.S. 144 (1970); *Lugar v. Edmondson Oil Co.*, 457 U.S. 922 (1982); *Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24 (1980). The same principle applies under Operation Checkmate.

This means the Act's target class is not "government officials." It is the broader class of **anyone who exercises or purports to exercise state authority against a citizen in a manner the Act prohibits**. That class includes:

- Sworn officers — police, sheriffs, deputies, federal agents
- Prosecutors and judges
- Regulators, agency officials, administrators
- Public-private contractors performing government functions
- Foster placements, institutional custodians, and other delegated custodians of citizens
- Private actors deputized to perform state functions
- Private impersonators of officials
- Any other person who acts under color of authority within the meaning of the Act

A class defined by who chooses to exercise state authority against citizens is the opposite of what the attainder doctrine forbids. The attainder doctrine forbids legislative targeting of identifiable persons based on attributes they cannot change. The Act reaches conduct that any of the persons listed above will be liable for **only if they choose to perform it** — and that any private person becomes liable for the moment they choose to act as if state authority backed their conduct.

This is conduct-based liability, with a clarifying actor element. It is not attainder.

## 5. The Targeted Officials in the Attainder Cases vs. Operation Checkmate

The clearest way to see the distance between Operation Checkmate and the Bill of Attainder Clauses is to look at what the cases that actually struck down attainders were *about*.

- **Cummings v. Missouri** (1867) — a state oath requiring priests, lawyers, and teachers to swear they had never sympathized with the Confederacy. The class was defined by **past identity** ("anyone who held sympathies during the past war"). The punishment was **automatic** ("you cannot practice your profession unless you swear"). There was **no trial** before the disqualification attached.
- **Ex parte Garland** (1867) — same structure, applied to federal attorneys.
- **United States v. Brown** (1965) — criminal liability based on **past or present Communist Party membership**, no other conduct required. The class was defined by identity. The conduct that triggered liability was nothing more than holding union office while being a Party member.

Each of those statutes punished persons for what they were — Confederate sympathizers, Party members — and did so automatically, by operation of the statute. Each was struck down.

Operation Checkmate punishes no one for what they are. It punishes no one for past identity. It does not strip any current official of office, pension, or status by force of its enactment. It reaches the conduct of any actor — government employee, private impersonator, deputized contractor, fiduciary in a custodial role — who chooses to exercise state authority against a citizen in the ways the Act prohibits. Every consequence is imposed only after trial.

The contrast is structural and complete.

---

## 6. The Utah Constitution Confirms the Same Result

Article I, §18 of the Utah Constitution provides:

> "No bill of attainder, ex post facto law, or law impairing the obligation of contracts shall be passed."

The Utah Supreme Court has interpreted this provision consistently with federal doctrine — the same three-element test, the same focus on identification, punishment, and lack of trial. Nothing in Utah's jurisprudence treats conduct-element statutes with an actor specification as attainders. Utah's existing criminal code is full of such statutes: DUI, perjury, securities fraud, embezzlement, conflict-of-interest provisions, official-misconduct statutes, and many others. None has been treated as an attainder under Article I, §18, and Operation Checkmate's structure does not differ from theirs in any constitutionally relevant respect.

---

## 7. Conclusion

Operation Checkmate is not a bill of attainder because:

1. **It identifies no person.** It defines conduct.
2. **It targets no ascertainable class.** It reaches anyone who chooses to perform the prohibited conduct under color of authority — government employees, private deputized actors, and private impersonators alike.
3. **It inflicts no punishment by its own operation.** Every consequence is imposed only after trial.
4. **It expands the judicial process. It does not bypass it.** The Citizen Council mechanism replaces captured prosecutorial discretion, not the courts.
5. **Its structure is identical to parallel criminal statutes** that have stood, unchallenged on attainder grounds, for decades or centuries — DUI, perjury, embezzlement, tax evasion, securities fraud, honest-services fraud, and most directly, 18 U.S.C. § 242 with its identical "under color of any law" element.

The objection that Operation Checkmate is a bill of attainder reflects a misunderstanding of what a bill of attainder is. The doctrine forbids legislative punishment of identified persons without trial. Operation Checkmate punishes no person without trial, identifies no person at all, and reaches conduct rather than identity. The objection is foreclosed by *Lovett*, *Brown*, *Nixon*, *Selective Service*, *De Veau*, and 150 years of accepted federal civil-rights criminal jurisprudence under 18 U.S.C. § 242.

The same rules apply to everyone. That is the constitutional principle Operation Checkmate enforces — and it is the opposite of what the Bill of Attainder Clauses forbid.
