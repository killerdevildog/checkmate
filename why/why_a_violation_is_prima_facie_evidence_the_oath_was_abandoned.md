# Why a Violation of These Criminal/Civil Statutes Is Prima Facie Evidence That the Oath of Office Was Abandoned

Section 5.10 of Operation Checkmate establishes a single-paragraph operative rule:

> Any violation of any provision of this Act by a government actor who has taken an oath of office to uphold and defend the Constitution of the United States, the Constitution of the State of Utah, or both, constitutes prima facie evidence that the actor's oath of office has been violated.

This document explains why that rule is the correct evidentiary standard — why a violation of the substantive provisions of this Act is, by the structural relationship between the oath and the Constitution this Act enforces, sufficient evidence (absent the actor's rebuttal) that the actor has abandoned the oath the actor took on entering office. The document develops the constitutional grounding for the rule, the actual text of the oaths it reaches, the operative content of what "upholding and defending the Constitution" actually requires, the logical chain by which any violation of the Act establishes prima facie abandonment of that duty, and the limited but real opportunity the actor retains to rebut.

The rule is not novel. It is the operative form of a constitutional principle as old as the Constitution itself: that the oath of office is the structural mechanism by which the people bind their delegated officers to the Constitution, and that violation of the constitutional duty the oath protects is, in operative reality, abandonment of the oath itself.

---

## 1. The Oath of Office Is Constitutionally Mandatory — Not Ceremonial

The first foundational point: the oath of office is not optional, not symbolic, and not merely traditional. It is **constitutionally mandatory**, by the text of the United States Constitution itself.

### 1.1 Article VI, Clause 3 of the U.S. Constitution

Article VI, Clause 3 provides:

> "The Senators and Representatives before mentioned, and the Members of the several State Legislatures, and all executive and judicial Officers, both of the United States and of the several States, **shall be bound by Oath or Affirmation, to support this Constitution**; but no religious Test shall ever be required as a Qualification to any Office or public Trust under the United States."

The constitutional command is direct: every senator, representative, member of a state legislature, executive officer (federal or state), and judicial officer (federal or state) **shall be bound by oath or affirmation** to support the Constitution. The Constitution itself requires that every public officer in every level of American government swear or affirm an oath bound to the Constitution.

The oath is therefore not a ceremonial nicety the officer may have or not have. It is a constitutional precondition of holding office at all. A person purporting to exercise the authority of a public office without having been bound by the required oath is, by operative effect, acting without the constitutional authority the office confers.

### 1.2 Utah Constitution Article IV, Section 10

The Utah Constitution mirrors and elaborates the federal command. Article IV, Section 10 provides:

> "All officers made elective or appointive by this Constitution or by the laws made in pursuance thereof, before entering upon the duties of their respective offices, shall take and subscribe the following oath or affirmation: 'I do solemnly swear (or affirm) that I will support, obey and defend the Constitution of the United States and the Constitution of this State, and that I will discharge the duties of my office with fidelity.'"

The Utah oath has four operative obligations:

1. **Support** the Constitution of the United States
2. **Obey** the Constitution of the United States and the Constitution of Utah
3. **Defend** the Constitution of the United States and the Constitution of Utah
4. **Discharge the duties of office with fidelity**

Each of these obligations is operative — not symbolic. The Utah officer is required by the Utah Constitution itself to swear or affirm to perform each of them. The oath is the structural mechanism by which the people of Utah, through the Utah Constitution, bind their delegated officers to the constitutional order.

### 1.3 The Federal Oath — 5 U.S.C. § 3331

The federal statutory oath for executive-branch officers, prescribed by Congress under its constitutional authority, provides:

> "An individual, except the President, elected or appointed to an office of honor or profit in the civil service or uniformed services, shall take the following oath: 'I, [name], do solemnly swear (or affirm) that I will **support and defend the Constitution of the United States** against all enemies, foreign and domestic; that I will bear true faith and allegiance to the same; that I take this obligation freely, without any mental reservation or purpose of evasion; and that I will well and faithfully discharge the duties of the office on which I am about to enter.'"

The federal oath, like the Utah oath, requires the officer to support and defend the Constitution, to bear true faith and allegiance to it, and to discharge the duties of office faithfully. The "without any mental reservation or purpose of evasion" clause is particularly operative: the officer is bound not just nominally but in earnest, with the explicit denial of any private reservation that would limit the operative force of the oath.

### 1.4 Peace Officer Oaths in Utah

Utah peace officers are required, under Utah Code § 53-13-103 and analogous provisions, to take an oath of office that includes the obligation to support and defend the Constitution and to discharge the duties of office faithfully. The oath is administered before the officer is sworn in and is a precondition of lawful exercise of peace-officer authority.

### 1.5 The Common Operative Content

Across the various oaths — federal, state, peace-officer, judicial, military — the **operative content** is consistent: every officer bound by oath to American public office is bound to **support and defend the Constitution** and to **discharge the duties of office with fidelity**. The specific wording varies, but the structural obligation is the same.

This is what the oath actually requires. It is not a ceremonial recitation; it is a constitutionally-mandatory commitment to specific operative duties that bind the officer for the entire duration of office.

## 2. What "Uphold and Defend the Constitution" Actually Requires

The phrase "uphold and defend the Constitution" — or its variations "support, obey and defend," "support and defend," "preserve, protect and defend" (the Presidential oath, Article II §1 cl. 8) — is not vague. It has operative content.

### 2.1 To "Support" the Constitution

To support the Constitution is to act, in office, in a manner consistent with the Constitution's text, structure, and operative requirements. The officer must not exercise the authority of office in a way that contradicts the Constitution. The officer must not deploy state power for purposes the Constitution forbids. The officer must not authorize, participate in, or fail to prevent (within the scope of the office's authority) conduct that the Constitution prohibits.

Support is an active obligation, not a passive one. An officer who "supports" the Constitution only by not openly attacking it has not satisfied the obligation. Support requires the affirmative use of the office's authority in service of the constitutional order — to enforce the rights the Constitution protects, to respect the structural limits the Constitution imposes, to refuse to participate in conduct the Constitution forbids.

### 2.2 To "Defend" the Constitution

To defend the Constitution is to act, in office, to protect the Constitution against violation — by others and by the officer's own institutional culture. The officer must not turn aside when the Constitution is violated by colleagues, by superiors, by other branches, by other levels of government. The officer must use the lawful authority of office to push back against constitutional violations the office has the capacity to address.

Defense is an active obligation. An officer who "defends" the Constitution only by personal non-violation has not satisfied the obligation. Defense requires the affirmative use of the office's authority to resist constitutional violations within the office's reach.

### 2.3 To "Obey" the Constitution

To obey the Constitution is to subordinate the officer's personal preference, institutional incentive, professional convenience, and policy view to the operative requirements of the constitutional text. The officer who obeys the Constitution does not place personal will above the constitutional rule. The officer who obeys the Constitution treats the constitutional limits as binding even when those limits are inconvenient, costly, or in tension with the office's preferred outcome.

### 2.4 To "Discharge the Duties of Office with Fidelity"

To discharge the duties of office with fidelity is to perform the office's lawful functions in good faith, consistent with the office's purpose, in service of the public the office was instituted to serve. Fidelity is the operative form of acting within the office's lawful scope, for the office's lawful purposes, with the lawful means the office's authority permits.

### 2.5 The Composite Obligation

Combining the four operative duties — support, defend, obey, fidelity — the officer's oath obligation is:

> **To exercise the lawful authority of office, in good faith, for the office's lawful purposes, in support and defense of the Constitution against violation, with subordination of personal preference and institutional incentive to the Constitution's operative requirements.**

This is what every officer in American government swore to do. This is what the oath constitutionally requires. This is the standard against which the officer's conduct in office is measured.

## 3. The Constitution the Oath Protects Is the Constitution This Act Enforces

The structural premise of §5.10 is that the Constitution the oath protects is the **same Constitution** this Act enforces. The Act is, in operative terms, a state-level enforcement mechanism for the substantive content of the Constitution — particularly the Bill of Rights, the Fourteenth Amendment, the Article IV §4 Guarantee Clause, the Article I §9 and §10 limits on legislative power, and the Article III limits on judicial power.

The connection is direct:

- The Act's §1.1.3 (Battery) enforces the citizen's right to security against unlawful deprivation — directly tracking the Fourth and Fourteenth Amendments' protections.
- The Act's §1.1.5 (Protected Rights) enforces the full scope of rights enumerated in the Bill of Rights, retained under the Ninth Amendment, and recognized in state constitutional and common-law traditions.
- The Act's §1.1.12 (Terrorism) enforces the Fourteenth Amendment's protection against governmental conduct designed to harm citizens.
- The Act's §1.1.25 (Legislate from the Bench) enforces the constitutional separation of powers — Article III's confinement of the judicial function to judgment rather than legislation.
- The Act's §1.1.45 (Non-Republican Government Act) enforces Article IV §4's guarantee of republican government.
- The Act's §1.1.46 (Judicial Imposter) enforces the constitutional requirement that judicial functions be performed by judicial actors.
- The Act's §1.1.47 (Carceralism) enforces the constitutional requirement that government respond to citizens within the lawful limits of its authority and not criminalize human distress that the government has the means to address.
- The Act's §5.1 (Immunity Gap) enforces the equal application of law that the Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause and the Article I limits on bills of attainder require.

Each substantive offense of the Act is, in operative content, an enforcement of constitutional protection that the oath binds the officer to support and defend. **The Constitution the oath protects and the Constitution this Act enforces are the same Constitution.**

## 4. The Logical Chain — Why a Violation Is Prima Facie Evidence of Oath Abandonment

The logical chain that produces §5.10's evidentiary rule is direct:

1. **The oath binds the officer to support, obey, and defend the Constitution.** (Article VI Cl. 3; Utah Const. Art. IV §10; 5 U.S.C. § 3331; peace-officer oaths; judicial oaths; military oaths.)
2. **The Constitution the oath protects is the same Constitution this Act enforces.** (§3 above.)
3. **The substantive offenses of this Act define conduct that violates the Constitution.** (Each offense is, in operative content, an enforcement of constitutional protection.)
4. **Therefore: a violation of any substantive offense of this Act is a violation of the constitutional duty the oath binds the officer to perform.**
5. **A violation of the constitutional duty the oath protects is, in operative reality, an abandonment of the oath.** (See §5 below on why "abandonment" is the operative concept.)
6. **Therefore: a violation of any provision of this Act is prima facie evidence that the officer's oath has been violated — that, in operative effect, the oath has been abandoned.**

The chain is structurally airtight. Each link follows from the link before it. The conclusion — that violation of the Act establishes oath abandonment prima facie — is not novel; it is the operative consequence of the structural relationship between the oath and the Constitution.

## 5. Why "Abandonment" Rather Than "Mere Violation"

§5.10 of the Act uses the language "the actor's oath of office has been violated." This document uses the stronger language of **abandonment** to capture the operative reality of what the violation establishes.

### 5.1 The Distinction

A "violation" of an oath, in a narrow technical sense, can be momentary — an officer slips, acts inconsistent with the oath in a single instance, and corrects course. The narrow violation may not by itself indicate that the officer has stopped intending to honor the oath.

**Abandonment**, by contrast, captures the operative reality of what the officer has done when the officer commits the kind of substantive violation §5.10 reaches. The substantive offenses of this Act are not slips. They are conduct that:

- Deprives a citizen of constitutional rights (§1.1.3)
- Coerces or extracts under color of authority (§1.1.13, §1.1.7)
- Operates outside the scope of constitutional authority (§1.1.10, §1.1.18)
- Substitutes pretextual cover for actual lawful basis (§1.1.32)
- Participates in cabalist coordination against citizens (§1.1.6)
- Engages in carceral response to citizens in crisis without performing the inquiry duty (§1.1.47)
- Exceeds judicial or executive authority in ways the Constitution forbids (§1.1.25, §1.1.46)
- Pushes the operative character of government toward a non-republican form (§1.1.45)

Conduct of this kind is not a slip. It is operative conduct in which the officer has, in effect, **set the oath aside** — has acted as though the constitutional duties the oath protects do not bind the officer in the operative situation. The officer has, in the operative sense, **abandoned** the oath.

### 5.2 Why the Distinction Matters

The distinction matters because the consequences of mere violation and the consequences of abandonment are different. A momentary violation might warrant correction, training, or administrative response. Abandonment — the operative renunciation of the constitutional duties the oath protects — warrants the structural consequences §5.10 invokes: forfeiture (§11.1), permanent ineligibility (§11.2), decertification (§11.4), disbarment, removal from professional standing, and the criminal accountability the Act's substantive provisions establish.

The substantive offenses of this Act are calibrated to reach conduct that constitutes abandonment, not mere slip. The mens rea standards of Section 4 (recklessness or higher) ensure that the violations the Act reaches involve at least a reckless disregard for the constitutional rights the officer was sworn to protect — and reckless disregard is, in operative effect, abandonment.

### 5.3 Why §5.10 Uses "Violated"

§5.10 uses the more textually-cautious word "violated" because that is the operative legal term that triggers the existing oath-violation criminal statutes and the forfeiture machinery of Section 11. The statutory text needs to match the existing legal vocabulary to function. But the **operative content** of what the violation establishes — what the conduct that triggers §5.10's rule actually represents in operative terms — is **abandonment**. The bill says "violated"; the operative meaning is "abandoned."

## 6. Why Prima Facie Is the Correct Evidentiary Standard

§5.10 establishes the violation as **prima facie evidence** of oath violation — not as conclusive evidence. The choice of evidentiary standard is deliberate and structural.

### 6.1 What "Prima Facie Evidence" Means

Prima facie evidence is evidence that is **sufficient, on its face**, to establish the fact in question — and that shifts the burden of going forward to the opposing party to rebut. It is not conclusive evidence (which would foreclose any rebuttal), but it is sufficient evidence (absent rebuttal) to support a finding.

Under §5.10, the operative effect is:

- The proven violation of the Act establishes oath violation **without further evidence**.
- The actor may **rebut** the prima facie evidence by showing that, despite the violation, the actor did not abandon the oath — for example, by demonstrating reasonable mistake of law not constituting recklessness under Section 4, by demonstrating lawful authority that justified the conduct, or by demonstrating exigent circumstances that excused the conduct.
- The burden of producing rebuttal evidence is on the actor.
- If the actor produces no rebuttal evidence sufficient to overcome the prima facie showing, the oath violation is established.

### 6.2 Why This Standard Is Appropriate

Three structural reasons support prima facie as the correct standard:

**First, the actor is in the best position to know.** The actor is the only person who knows what the actor was thinking, intending, considering, and weighing at the moment of the conduct. Requiring the actor to produce rebuttal evidence places the evidentiary burden on the party with the actual information.

**Second, the substantive violation is the operative event.** The proven violation of the Act is itself the operative event that produces the constitutional harm to the citizen. That event, by the structural relationship between the oath and the Constitution, IS the abandonment of the oath in operative terms. Treating it as prima facie evidence captures this operative reality.

**Third, public confidence in the oath requires visible consequences for visible breaches.** If a sworn officer can commit a substantive violation of this Act and face no oath consequence — because each oath violation requires a separate proof — the operative force of the oath collapses. The oath becomes a piece of paper rather than the structural mechanism the Constitution requires it to be. §5.10's prima facie rule ensures that visible breaches produce visible oath consequences, sustaining the public confidence the constitutional design depends on.

### 6.3 The Rebuttal Opportunity Is Real

The prima facie standard is not a kangaroo court. The actor retains a genuine opportunity to rebut. Specifically, the actor may show:

- That the violation rested on a reasonable mistake of fact or law that does not constitute recklessness under Section 4 of the Act
- That the conduct was performed under lawful authority the actor had reason to believe was valid
- That the conduct was performed under exigent circumstances within the meaning of §1.1.3.3 (Emergency Exigency)
- That the conduct fell within the safe harbor of the Performed Duty with an Abundance of Care standard set out in the Statement of Intent
- That the actor was performing a lawful duty under predicate (§1.1.3.2) where the actor's analysis of the predicate was reasonable on contemporaneously available facts
- Any other showing that, despite the substantive violation, the actor's overall conduct of office did not constitute abandonment of the oath

The rebuttal opportunity preserves the actor's due process. The prima facie standard simply places the burden on the actor — the party with the information — to make the showing.

## 7. The Pre-Existing Oath-Violation Statutes

Utah already has criminal statutes that reach oath violations. Utah Code § 76-8-202 (Unauthorized assumption of public office and related crimes), Utah Code § 76-8-204 (Bribery to influence official action), and various other provisions reach conduct that violates the public-office oath in specific operative ways.

The federal criminal code similarly reaches oath violations — including 18 U.S.C. § 1622 (subornation of perjury), 18 U.S.C. § 241 and § 242 (conspiracy and substantive violations against rights under color of law), and other provisions.

**§5.10 does not replace these existing statutes.** It operates alongside them. Where the actor's conduct independently violates an existing oath-related statute, that statute applies on its own terms. §5.10 adds the prima facie evidence rule that makes oath violation established by the substantive violation of this Act, without requiring separate proof of oath violation under the existing statutes.

The cumulative effect: an actor who commits a substantive violation of the Act faces (1) direct liability under the Act for the substantive violation, (2) prima facie established oath violation under §5.10, and (3) any applicable criminal liability under pre-existing federal or state oath-violation statutes. The consequences run cumulatively.

## 8. What §5.10 Does Not Do

The rule is operative within defined limits. §5.10 does not:

1. **Make oath-taking a prerequisite to liability under this Act.** A government actor who never took an oath — because the office does not require one, because the actor was a contractor, because the actor was a private impersonator, because the ceremony was skipped — is still liable under the Act for any substantive violation. The oath-violation rule of §5.10 attaches only where the actor took an oath; the direct liability under the Act attaches regardless. See §1.1.1.
2. **Require a separate prosecution for oath violation.** The prima facie evidence rule operates as part of the consequence-allocation machinery of the Act, particularly Section 11 (forfeiture, ineligibility, decertification). It does not require a separate criminal prosecution under the existing oath statutes (though such prosecution may also occur).
3. **Reach private actors not under color of authority.** §5.10's rule applies only to government actors who took an oath. Private actors not under color of authority (the ordinary citizens of Utah going about their lives) are not government actors and are not bound by any oath of office, so §5.10 has no application to them.
4. **Displace any rebuttal opportunity.** The actor retains the genuine opportunity to rebut the prima facie showing.

## 9. Why This Rule Matters Structurally

§5.10 closes a structural gap that has, in modern American government, become operatively significant.

The pre-existing oath-violation statutes have been historically loosely enforced. Officials who take the oath and then violate the constitutional duties it protects rarely face oath-violation prosecution under the existing statutes, even where their conduct clearly breaches the operative content of the oath. The result, over time, has been the operative erosion of the oath as a binding constraint: officials swear, take office, and operate in disregard of the constitutional duties the oath protects, with no consequence specific to the oath breach.

§5.10 reverses this erosion. Once an actor commits a substantive violation of this Act, the oath violation follows automatically — by the prima facie evidence rule — and the consequences attach automatically under Section 11. The actor cannot escape the oath consequence by arguing that the existing oath-violation statutes are vague, that the oath wording was loose, that no separate proof of oath violation was offered, or that the enforcement mechanism for oath violations has been historically lax.

The operative effect: the oath returns to its constitutional position as a binding constraint on the conduct of office. The structural mechanism the Constitution requires — the binding of public officers by oath to the constitutional order — operates as the Constitution designed it to operate, supported by the prima facie evidence rule that ensures breaches of the oath produce visible consequences.

## 10. Conclusion — The Operative Effect

§5.10 of Operation Checkmate establishes that any violation of any provision of this Act by a government actor who took an oath to uphold and defend the Constitution constitutes prima facie evidence that the oath has been violated.

The rule is the operative consequence of three constitutional realities:

1. **The oath is constitutionally mandatory.** Article VI Clause 3 of the U.S. Constitution requires every public officer to be bound by oath to support the Constitution. Utah Constitution Article IV §10 elaborates the requirement. The oath is not optional or ceremonial; it is the structural mechanism by which the people bind their delegated officers to the Constitution.
2. **The Constitution the oath protects is the Constitution this Act enforces.** The substantive offenses of this Act define conduct that violates the Constitution — particularly the Bill of Rights, the Fourteenth Amendment, the Guarantee Clause, and the structural limits on legislative, executive, and judicial power. Violation of the Act is, in operative content, violation of the Constitution.
3. **Violation of the constitutional duty the oath protects is, in operative reality, abandonment of the oath.** An officer who commits a substantive violation of this Act has acted as though the constitutional duties the oath binds the officer to perform do not bind the officer in the operative situation. The substantive violation IS the abandonment of the oath in operative terms.

§5.10 captures this operative reality by establishing the prima facie evidence rule — sufficient evidence (absent rebuttal) of oath violation by virtue of the substantive violation. The rule preserves the actor's rebuttal opportunity, operates within the existing legal framework of oath-violation statutes, and closes the structural gap that loose oath enforcement has historically left open.

The bill enforces the Constitution directly. §5.10 ensures that where a sworn officer violates that Constitution through conduct the Act reaches, the oath breach is established by the same evidence that establishes the underlying violation — and the consequences of the abandonment of the oath attach cumulatively with the consequences of the substantive violation.

The oath of office returns, under §5.10, to the constitutional position the Founders designed for it: a binding constraint on the conduct of office, supported by visible consequences for visible breaches, sustaining the public confidence in the constitutional order that the oath was instituted to protect.
