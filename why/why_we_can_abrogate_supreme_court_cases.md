# Why Operation Checkmate Can Abrogate Supreme Court Cases

A foreseeable and important objection to Operation Checkmate is that it purports to abrogate, within the State of Utah, several long-standing Supreme Court doctrines — qualified immunity, absolute judicial immunity, absolute prosecutorial immunity, sovereign immunity, and a range of doctrines that have, in operative reality, hollowed out constitutional protections owed to citizens. The objection asks: how can a state statute override Supreme Court precedent?

The answer rests on **two distinct and independently sufficient grounds**, each of which fully justifies what the Act does:

1. **The immunity doctrines are civil-liability doctrines. The Act makes the underlying conduct criminal.** No abrogation of Supreme Court precedent is required to enforce a state criminal statute, because the immunity doctrines — as the Supreme Court itself has framed them — apply only to civil suits for damages. They have never reached criminal prosecution, and they have no operative role to play when the State of Utah charges a government actor with a state-defined criminal offense.

2. **The Tenth Amendment reserves to the states the power to protect their citizens from federal action — including federal judicial doctrine — that exceeds the federal government's constitutional authority.** When a federal judge invents a "doctrine" through judicial legislation that operates beyond the Article III judicial power, especially where the doctrine harms private citizens within Utah, the State has both the authority and the duty to refuse to enforce the doctrine within its borders. This is not a novel claim. It is the operative premise of federalism — the structural protection the Founders built into the Constitution for exactly this circumstance — and it is the reason the Revolutionary War was fought in the first place.

This document develops each ground in turn, identifies the specific Supreme Court cases the Act reaches and explains why each abrogation is constitutional, and identifies the limits of the Act's reach — what it does not claim and what it does not do.

---

## 1. Ground One — The Immunity Doctrines Are Civil Doctrines; Criminal Prosecution Does Not Require Abrogation, It Requires Clarification

The Supreme Court immunity doctrines that the Act removes within its scope — qualified immunity, absolute judicial immunity, absolute prosecutorial immunity, and sovereign immunity in its application to individual officials — were, in every case, articulated by the Supreme Court as **civil-liability** doctrines. They were developed to govern civil suits for damages, not criminal prosecution.

The leading immunity opinions are explicit on this point.

### 1.1 Qualified Immunity Is a Civil Doctrine

The qualified-immunity doctrine traces principally to *Harlow v. Fitzgerald*, 457 U.S. 800 (1982), and its progeny. The doctrine, in operative form, holds that government officials performing discretionary functions are immune from **civil suits for damages** unless they have violated a clearly established constitutional right.

*Harlow* itself framed the rule in those terms: "government officials performing discretionary functions generally are shielded from **liability for civil damages** insofar as their conduct does not violate clearly established statutory or constitutional rights." 457 U.S. at 818 (emphasis added).

The Court has never held that qualified immunity bars criminal prosecution. To the contrary, the Court has applied qualified immunity exclusively in the context of civil §1983 actions and Bivens suits. Criminal prosecution of officials for the same conduct that gave rise to the civil claim has always proceeded without qualified-immunity defenses. *Screws v. United States*, 325 U.S. 91 (1945) — the leading case interpreting 18 U.S.C. § 242 (the federal criminal civil-rights statute) — recognizes no qualified-immunity defense to criminal liability.

A state criminal statute that reaches the same underlying conduct does not need to "abrogate" qualified immunity to operate. The doctrine does not apply to criminal prosecution in the first place.

### 1.2 Judicial Immunity Is a Civil Doctrine

The doctrine of absolute judicial immunity traces to *Bradley v. Fisher*, 80 U.S. (13 Wall.) 335 (1872), through *Stump v. Sparkman*, 435 U.S. 349 (1978), to *Mireles v. Waco*, 502 U.S. 9 (1991). The doctrine holds that judges are immune from **civil suit** for acts performed in their judicial capacity, even where the acts are alleged to be malicious, corrupt, in excess of jurisdiction, procedurally defective, or grievously injurious to a citizen.

Every one of these opinions frames the immunity in civil-liability terms. *Bradley* itself was a civil malicious-prosecution suit. *Stump* was a §1983 civil action. *Mireles* was a §1983 civil action. The Supreme Court has never extended judicial immunity to criminal prosecution.

To the contrary, federal courts have indicted and convicted judges for criminal conduct performed in their judicial capacity — the **Operation Greylord** prosecutions in Chicago (1980s) convicted seventeen sitting judges for criminal conduct in office; the **Kids-for-Cash** scandal resulted in criminal convictions of Pennsylvania judges who had taken bribes connected to juvenile detention placements. None of these prosecutions raised judicial-immunity defenses, because the doctrine has no application to criminal liability.

### 1.3 Prosecutorial Immunity Is a Civil Doctrine

The doctrine of absolute prosecutorial immunity traces principally to *Imbler v. Pachtman*, 424 U.S. 409 (1976). *Imbler* held that a prosecutor is absolutely immune from **§1983 civil liability** for activities intimately associated with the judicial phase of the criminal process — initiating prosecutions, presenting evidence, examining witnesses.

The *Imbler* opinion is explicit that its rule is a civil-liability rule and does not extend to criminal prosecution: "we have no occasion to consider whether like or similar reasons require immunity for those aspects of the prosecutor's responsibility that cast him in the role of an administrator or investigative officer rather than that of advocate." 424 U.S. at 430–431. The Court was addressing the boundary of the **civil** immunity, not extending it to criminal prosecution at all.

Federal prosecutors have been criminally prosecuted for crimes committed in their prosecutorial capacity. State prosecutors have been criminally prosecuted under generally applicable laws for the same kind of conduct *Imbler* addresses. No criminal prosecution of a prosecutor has ever been barred by *Imbler* — the doctrine has no application to criminal liability.

### 1.4 Sovereign Immunity Protects the State, Not the Individual Actor From Criminal Liability

Sovereign immunity, as articulated in *Alden v. Maine*, 527 U.S. 706 (1999), and the line of cases concerning Eleventh Amendment immunity, protects the **State itself** from being sued in federal court without its consent. It is a doctrine about who can be a defendant in a civil suit for damages.

Sovereign immunity has never shielded an individual government actor from criminal prosecution. State and federal officials are routinely prosecuted criminally for conduct performed in office. Sovereign immunity is silent on this question because it has no operative role to play.

### 1.5 The Federal Criminal Civil-Rights Statute Demonstrates the Point

The most direct demonstration that the immunity doctrines do not apply to criminal prosecution is the federal criminal civil-rights statute, **18 U.S.C. § 242**:

> "Whoever, **under color of any law, statute, ordinance, regulation, or custom**, willfully subjects any person ... to the deprivation of any rights, privileges, or immunities secured or protected by the Constitution or laws of the United States ... shall be fined under this title or imprisoned not more than one year, or both."

This statute has been on the books in its present form since the Civil Rights Act of 1866 (then 14 Stat. 27 §2). For more than 150 years it has reached judges (*Lefkowitz v. Newsome*, 420 U.S. 283 (1975); cases applying §242 to judicial conduct), prosecutors (rarer but available), police officers (extensively), and other officials for criminal conduct performed under color of authority.

In not one of these prosecutions has a court applied qualified immunity, judicial immunity, prosecutorial immunity, or sovereign immunity as a defense to criminal liability under §242. The doctrines simply do not reach criminal cases. They were not designed to, and the Supreme Court has not extended them there.

Operation Checkmate operates exactly as 18 U.S.C. § 242 has operated for 150 years: by treating the conduct of officials acting under color of authority as **criminal**, prosecutable through ordinary criminal process, with no civil-liability immunity defense available. Calling this "abrogation" of immunity overstates what the Act does. The Act does not need to overrule *Harlow*, *Bradley*, *Stump*, *Mireles*, or *Imbler*. Those cases say nothing relevant to criminal prosecution. The Act simply enforces conduct as criminal — which the Supreme Court doctrines themselves have always conceded is the constitutional default for the criminal-justice system.

### 1.6 Where the Immunity Doctrines Have Crept Beyond Civil Liability — State Power to Recategorize

To the extent state or lower federal courts have, in particular cases, extended civil-liability immunity reasoning into criminal contexts (rare, but it has happened in some state-court decisions), the State of Utah retains its full Tenth Amendment authority to define what is criminal under state law and to provide that no immunity defense, civil or otherwise, applies to a state criminal prosecution. Utah's police power authorizes Utah's criminal code; the criminal code's coverage is not constrained by federal civil-liability doctrines that the Supreme Court itself has framed as civil-liability doctrines.

This is the simplest and most direct ground for the Act's removal of the immunity doctrines within its scope. The doctrines never reached criminal prosecution. The Act is criminal. The doctrines are inapplicable.

## 2. Ground Two — The Tenth Amendment Reserves to the States the Power to Ignore Ultra Vires Federal Judicial Doctrine

The second ground is broader and more structural. It reaches not only the immunity doctrines but any federal Supreme Court doctrine that:

- Was created by judicial legislation rather than judicial judgment
- Operates beyond the Article III judicial power
- Has the operative effect of stripping rights from citizens, or of authorizing government action against citizens that the Constitution forbids

The Tenth Amendment is direct on this point:

> "The powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution, nor prohibited by it to the States, are reserved to the States respectively, or to the people."

The federal judicial power is delegated by Article III. Article III is specific in scope: the federal judicial power extends to "Cases" and "Controversies" listed in §2, decided by judicial actors applying law to facts. It does **not** include the power to legislate. The federal judiciary has no Article III authority to invent doctrine that operates as if it were law without going through the constitutional process for making law (Article I bicameralism and presentment, the Article V amendment process).

### 2.1 What "Judicial Legislation" Actually Is

The Act treats certain federal Supreme Court doctrines as **judicial legislation** — judicially-invented rules that operate as if they were law without coming from any source the Constitution authorizes to create law. The line between legitimate judicial interpretation and unconstitutional judicial legislation is the central operative question of §1.1.25 of the Act (Legislate from the Bench), and it tracks Hamilton's framing in *Federalist No. 78*:

> "The judiciary ... has no influence over either the sword or the purse; no direction either of the strength or of the wealth of the society; and can take no active resolution whatever. It may truly be said to have **neither force nor will, but merely judgment**."

Judgment is the application of law to facts. Legislation is the creation of legal rules that govern future conduct. Judges have legitimate judgment but no legitimate will. When a federal court creates a doctrine that operates as a legal rule without grounding in constitutional or statutory text — qualified immunity has no textual basis; absolute judicial immunity has no textual basis; absolute prosecutorial immunity has no textual basis; the various Fourth Amendment "exceptions" that have hollowed out the warrant requirement have no textual basis — the court has crossed from judgment into legislation. It has done what Hamilton said the judiciary could not legitimately do.

### 2.2 What the States Can Do With Ultra Vires Federal Doctrine

When the federal judiciary acts beyond Article III, the resulting "doctrine" is not the supreme law of the land — because the Supremacy Clause (Article VI, Clause 2) makes federal law supreme **only** when it is "made in Pursuance" of the Constitution. Doctrine made beyond the federal judicial power is not made in pursuance of the Constitution. It is, in the strict sense, *ultra vires* — beyond the authority of the issuing body. And federal action that is *ultra vires* is not entitled to Supremacy-Clause status.

The Supreme Court has recognized this principle in the **anti-commandeering doctrine**, which reaches a related question: federal mandates beyond the federal government's constitutional authority cannot be imposed on the states. *Printz v. United States*, 521 U.S. 898 (1997), held that the federal government cannot commandeer state executive officials to perform federal regulatory functions. *New York v. United States*, 505 U.S. 144 (1992), held that the federal government cannot commandeer state legislatures into adopting federal regulatory schemes. *Murphy v. NCAA*, 138 S. Ct. 1461 (2018), reaffirmed and extended the doctrine.

The principle that animates the anti-commandeering cases applies with even greater force to *ultra vires* federal judicial doctrine. If the federal government cannot commandeer state legislative or executive functions to enforce federal policy, then a fortiori the federal government cannot — by judicial doctrine made beyond Article III — commandeer the state's criminal-law authority to immunize conduct the state has the police-power authority to criminalize.

The states retain, under the Tenth Amendment and the structural design of federalism, the power to:

- Define crimes occurring within their borders
- Refuse to enforce federal mandates beyond federal constitutional authority
- Protect their citizens from federal action — judicial, legislative, or executive — that exceeds the federal government's authority
- Provide remedies for the violation of citizen rights, including remedies that the federal civil-rights statutes have failed to provide

### 2.3 The Founders Designed Federalism to Do Exactly This

The structural redundancy of state and federal protection of citizen rights was not an accident. It was a deliberate design feature of the Constitution, deliberately included to provide citizens with multiple layers of protection against governmental overreach — federal protection against state abuse, and **state protection against federal abuse**.

The Revolutionary War was fought, in significant part, against:

- **Executive overreach** — the Crown's use of executive authority to impose laws on the colonies without representation
- **Judicial overreach** — the use of admiralty courts, vice-admiralty courts, and other Crown-directed tribunals to deprive colonists of jury trial and ordinary judicial protections
- **Legislative overreach** — Parliament's claim to legislate for the colonies "in all cases whatsoever" without colonial consent

The colonists fought to establish a system in which the citizen would have protection against each of these forms of overreach. The Constitution embodies that system: separation of powers within the federal government, federalism between the federal and state governments, and a Bill of Rights enforcing both structural and substantive limits.

When the federal judiciary — alone among the three federal branches — invents doctrine that overrides the Bill of Rights, hollows out the Fourth Amendment, immunizes federal and state officials from accountability, and operates beyond the Article III power, it is performing the very kind of overreach the Revolution was fought against. The structural protection against this is the state's reserved power under the Tenth Amendment: the power to refuse to enforce federal doctrine that exceeds federal authority, especially where the doctrine harms the state's own citizens.

This is not "nullification" in the sense rejected by *Cooper v. Aaron*, 358 U.S. 1 (1958), and the integration cases. Those cases addressed states refusing to enforce **constitutional** federal commands — the Fourteenth Amendment's equal-protection guarantee as applied by *Brown v. Board of Education*, 347 U.S. 483 (1954). The state's refusal to enforce a *constitutional* federal command is unconstitutional, and the Supreme Court rightly rejected it. The state's refusal to enforce an *ultra vires* federal doctrine — federal action beyond federal authority — is the Tenth Amendment's structural function, not its violation.

The line is sharp: states must enforce the Constitution. States need not, and cannot constitutionally be compelled to, enforce federal action beyond the Constitution.

### 2.4 The Guarantee Clause Reinforces the State's Authority to Act

Article IV, §4 of the United States Constitution guarantees to every state a republican form of government. The Supreme Court's "political question" treatment of the Guarantee Clause in *Luther v. Borden*, 48 U.S. (7 How.) 1 (1849), is itself a candidate for the *ultra vires* analysis above — it removes from judicial review a constitutional provision that the Constitution itself does not designate as non-justiciable.

But independent of any judicial-review question, the Guarantee Clause confirms what the structural design already says: the citizens of each state are constitutionally entitled to be governed by a republic. A federal doctrine that operates against citizens in a manner inconsistent with republican government — that is, in a manner that subordinates the citizen to unaccountable government power — is itself inconsistent with the constitutional design, and the state's protective authority is at its maximum in resisting such a doctrine.

§1.1.45 of the Act (Non-Republican Government Act) operationalizes this principle. The state's authority to refuse to enforce federal doctrine that pushes the operative character of government toward a non-republican form is not just constitutional; it is part of the constitutional order itself.

## 3. The Specific Supreme Court Doctrines the Act Abrogates and the Ground for Each Abrogation

### 3.1 *Harlow v. Fitzgerald*, 457 U.S. 800 (1982) — Qualified Immunity

- **Ground 1 (Criminal Reclassification).** *Harlow* is a civil-liability doctrine. The Act treats the underlying conduct as criminal under state law. *Harlow* does not apply. **Primary ground.**
- **Ground 2 (Ultra Vires Doctrine).** *Harlow* was invented by the Supreme Court without textual or statutory grounding. Subsequent extensions (especially the "clearly established" requirement, which functions to immunize officials from ever-novel constitutional violations) have hollowed out 42 U.S.C. § 1983, a federal statute Congress passed to enforce the Fourteenth Amendment. The doctrine operates as judicial legislation overriding the statute. To the extent it would be applied against the Act, it is *ultra vires* and the State of Utah may refuse to enforce it. **Supporting ground.**

### 3.2 *Bradley v. Fisher*, 80 U.S. (13 Wall.) 335 (1872) / *Stump v. Sparkman*, 435 U.S. 349 (1978) / *Mireles v. Waco*, 502 U.S. 9 (1991) — Absolute Judicial Immunity

- **Ground 1.** All three are civil-liability decisions. The Act is criminal. The doctrine does not apply. **Primary ground.**
- **Ground 2.** Absolute immunity for judicial actors has no constitutional textual basis. The doctrine has been criticized on this ground since *Bradley* itself (Justice Field's majority opinion is a candid acknowledgment of policy reasoning, not constitutional interpretation). The doctrine is judicial legislation, operating *ultra vires* the Article III power. **Supporting ground.**

### 3.3 *Imbler v. Pachtman*, 424 U.S. 409 (1976) — Absolute Prosecutorial Immunity

- **Ground 1.** *Imbler* is explicitly framed as a §1983 civil-liability doctrine. The Act is criminal. **Primary ground.**
- **Ground 2.** Like absolute judicial immunity, absolute prosecutorial immunity has no textual basis. It is policy reasoning extended to functional-immunity rules. *Ultra vires* the Article III power. **Supporting ground.**

### 3.4 Sovereign Immunity / *Alden v. Maine*, 527 U.S. 706 (1999), and the Eleventh Amendment Cases

- **Ground 1.** Sovereign immunity protects the State from being sued. It does not protect individual officials from criminal liability. The doctrine has no application to a state criminal prosecution under the Act. **Primary ground.**
- **Ground 2.** To the extent any federal doctrine purports to extend sovereign immunity to immunize individual officials from criminal accountability for conduct against citizens, that extension exceeds the textual basis of the Eleventh Amendment and is *ultra vires*. **Supporting ground.**

### 3.5 Fourth Amendment "Exceptions" That Have Hollowed Out the Warrant Requirement

The automobile exception, the consent exception, the inventory exception, the plain-view doctrine, the stop-and-frisk doctrine (*Terry v. Ohio*, 392 U.S. 1 (1968)), the dog-sniff doctrine, the canine-alert doctrine, the third-party doctrine, the border-search doctrine, and the various exigency exceptions have, in cumulative operative effect, hollowed out the textual Fourth Amendment warrant requirement.

- **Ground 2 (Primary).** Each of these "exceptions" is judicial creation that operates as additional text the Fourth Amendment does not actually contain. The Fourth Amendment text guarantees the right to be secure against unreasonable searches and seizures and requires warrants on probable cause. The exceptions function to allow searches and seizures the text would not permit. They are judicial legislation, *ultra vires* the Article III power.

The Act treats Utah citizens' Fourth Amendment rights as the constitutional text actually provides, not as the cumulative exceptions have narrowed them. Where a Utah citizen's rights are violated by application of a Fourth Amendment "exception" that has no constitutional textual basis, the Act reaches the violating conduct under §1.1.3 (Battery), §1.1.46 (Judicial Imposter — when an executive actor "applies" a doctrine that the executive has no authority to apply), and §1.1.25 (Legislate from the Bench — where the doctrine itself was the judicial legislation that created the exception).

### 3.6 Political-Question, Standing, and Abstention Doctrines Used to Defeat Citizen Constitutional Claims

Various judicially-created doctrines — the political-question doctrine as applied to Guarantee Clause claims; restrictive standing doctrines applied to citizens raising constitutional challenges; abstention doctrines that route citizens away from federal court without meaningful state-court alternative — have functioned to deny citizens any forum for the vindication of constitutional rights.

- **Ground 2 (Primary).** These doctrines are judicial creations operating as additional text the Constitution does not contain. The Article III "Cases" and "Controversies" requirement is the constitutional text; the various doctrines that have been built on it function as judicial legislation expanding the scope of judicial decline-to-decide power.

Within the State of Utah, where these doctrines have operated to defeat citizen claims under this Act, the State is not bound to enforce the doctrines. Citizens have access to the Citizen Council mechanism (Section 9) and to ordinary Utah criminal process. The state-court forum is constitutionally available, the state's police-power authority over crimes occurring within state borders is unimpeded, and the federal doctrines that would have defeated the federal claim do not defeat the state criminal claim.

## 4. What This Act Does NOT Do

Operation Checkmate's authority to abrogate Supreme Court precedent is bounded. The Act:

1. **Does not claim to bind anyone outside Utah.** The Act operates within Utah's police-power jurisdiction. Federal officers operating against Utah citizens within Utah's borders are reachable under the Act on Utah jurisdictional anchors. Federal officers operating against non-Utah citizens in other states are not.

2. **Does not ignore legitimate Supreme Court interpretation of the Constitution.** Where the Supreme Court has interpreted constitutional text in genuine judicial judgment — *Brown v. Board* applying the Fourteenth Amendment, *Gideon v. Wainwright* applying the Sixth Amendment, *Miranda v. Arizona* applying the Fifth Amendment, *District of Columbia v. Heller* applying the Second Amendment, *SEC v. Jarkesy* applying the Seventh Amendment — the Act treats those interpretations as binding within the bounds of the constitutional text the cases interpret.

3. **Does not claim states can ignore all federal precedent.** The state's authority to refuse to enforce federal doctrine is bounded by the *ultra vires* test. Doctrine that genuinely interprets constitutional or statutory text the Constitution authorizes is binding on the state. Doctrine that exceeds the Article III judicial power is not.

4. **Does not purport to override federal statutes that Congress has constitutional authority to enact.** Federal criminal statutes, federal civil-rights statutes (including 42 U.S.C. § 1983 and 18 U.S.C. § 242), federal regulatory statutes within the commerce power, and other constitutionally-grounded federal law remain in full force. The Act supplements, it does not displace.

5. **Does not function as nullification of constitutional federal law.** The state's authority under the Tenth Amendment is to refuse to enforce **unconstitutional** federal action, not to refuse to enforce constitutional federal action it disagrees with on policy grounds. The Act is not nullification; it is the structural exercise of reserved state power.

The line between what the Act does and what the Act does not do is the same line that animates the rest of the Act's analytical architecture: **judicial doctrine that exceeds Article III is reachable; judicial judgment that applies constitutional text is not.** This is the operative principle of §1.1.25 of the Act, and it is the operative principle of every abrogation the Act effects.

---

## 5. The Two Grounds Are Independent

Each of the two grounds above is independently sufficient to justify the Act's removal of the immunity doctrines and other federal precedent within its scope.

- If only Ground 1 were available — if the federal courts construed the immunity doctrines as reaching criminal as well as civil cases — Ground 2 would still allow the State of Utah to refuse to enforce that extension as *ultra vires* the Article III power.
- If only Ground 2 were available — if no criminal/civil distinction existed in the immunity doctrines — Ground 1 would still allow the State to define the conduct as criminal under state law, with the state's plenary police-power authority over crimes committed within state borders.

The Act has both grounds. Each operates independently. Together, they answer the objection that a state statute cannot override Supreme Court precedent: the state statute does not override the precedent; it operates in a domain (criminal liability) where the precedent does not reach, or in a domain (state protection of citizens from federal overreach) where the state's reserved power is at its maximum and the precedent is *ultra vires*.

---

## 6. Conclusion

Operation Checkmate's removal of the immunity doctrines and other Supreme Court precedent within its scope rests on two independent and sufficient grounds:

1. **The immunity doctrines are civil-liability doctrines.** They have never applied to criminal prosecution. The Act is criminal. No abrogation is required; the doctrines have no operative role to play in a state criminal prosecution. **18 U.S.C. § 242** has demonstrated for more than 150 years that conduct under color of authority is reachable through criminal prosecution without immunity defenses.

2. **The Tenth Amendment reserves to the states the power to refuse to enforce federal judicial doctrine that exceeds Article III.** When federal judges invent doctrine through judicial legislation that operates beyond the constitutional grant of judicial power — and that doctrine harms citizens within the State of Utah — the State has both the authority and the duty to refuse to enforce the doctrine. The Revolutionary War was fought to establish a constitutional order in which citizens would have protection against this kind of overreach, and the Tenth Amendment preserves that protection.

The Act does not nullify constitutional federal law. It does not bind anyone outside Utah. It does not claim authority to override the Supreme Court on questions of constitutional text the Supreme Court has authority to decide. It operates in a domain — criminal liability for conduct against Utah citizens — where the State's authority is plenary and the federal civil-liability doctrines have no operative role.

The same rules apply to everyone. That is the constitutional principle Operation Checkmate enforces. The federal immunity doctrines, in their operative effect, exempt a class of actors from rules that apply to all other citizens. Removing those doctrines within the scope of a state criminal statute is not an attack on the Constitution; it is the State of Utah doing what the Constitution designed federalism to do — protecting its citizens from federal action that has exceeded federal constitutional authority.
