# Why the Act Defines So Many Terms — The IS / IS NOT Principle

A reader encountering Operation Checkmate for the first time will notice that the Act is, by the standards of American legislation, extraordinarily definition-heavy. Section 1.1 alone contains forty-seven defined terms, most of them developed in operative detail across multiple paragraphs, with explicit cross-references, illustrative examples, classification of forms, defenses-limited subsections, and construction rules tying each term to the others. Section 1.2 is a dedicated "Misconceptions" section that names and rejects specific common misreadings before they can take hold. The Act's drafting is far more definitional than the typical state criminal code, far more definitional than the typical state administrative statute, and far more definitional than even most federal civil-rights legislation.

This is by design. The definition-heavy drafting is one of the Act's principal defenses against the single most reliable failure mode of every important American law: **definitional drift** — the slow, decades-long reinterpretation by which key terms come to mean something other than what they meant when written, and the operative law comes to mean something other than what the text actually says.

This document explains the drafting principle that governs every definition in the Act:

> **Each defined term must specify both what it IS and what it IS NOT, in operative detail, in language that withstands reinterpretation over time.**

It also explains the role of the documents in this `why/` folder in the same anti-drift architecture: they are not advocacy materials, marketing copy, or persuasive essays. They are **preservation documents** — written contemporaneously with the Act, explaining the operative intent of each substantive choice, so that posterity reading the Act years or decades from now can verify that the Act still means what it was drafted to mean.

## 1. The Drift Problem — Why Definitions Matter

Every important American legal text has been subjected, over its operational lifetime, to reinterpretation that has changed its operative meaning — sometimes radically — while the text itself remained unchanged. The pattern is consistent:

1. A statute or constitutional provision is enacted with text that uses certain key terms.
2. The key terms are left undefined, or defined in language that admits a range of possible meanings.
3. Courts, agencies, and officials interpret the terms over time, each interpretation narrowing or expanding the meaning in some way.
4. After decades of accreted interpretation, the operative meaning of the text bears little resemblance to what its drafters wrote.
5. The text becomes a totem — invoked by the actual decision-makers (judges, officials, regulators) to legitimize the reinterpretations that have become the operative law.

This is not a hypothetical risk. It is the actual history of much of American constitutional and statutory law.

### 1.1 Examples of Definitional Drift in Operative American Law

**Qualified Immunity.** *Pierson v. Ray*, 386 U.S. 547 (1967), held that police officers in good faith could not be sued under §1983 for an arrest later determined to be unconstitutional. *Harlow v. Fitzgerald*, 457 U.S. 800 (1982), reformulated the standard as "objective reasonable" reliance on then-existing law. By *Saucier v. Katz*, 533 U.S. 194 (2001), and *Pearson v. Callahan*, 555 U.S. 223 (2009), the standard had become whether the constitutional rule violated was "clearly established" — meaning a prior case with nearly identical facts had held the conduct unconstitutional. In operative practice, the standard had drifted from "the officer acted in good faith" to "the officer can violate any constitutional right not previously adjudicated on identical facts and walk away."

The statutory text of 42 U.S.C. § 1983 did not change between 1967 and 2009. The text says nothing about immunity. The drift occurred entirely in the interpretive layer, and the operative law became something the drafters of §1983 (1871) would not recognize.

**Interstate Commerce.** The Commerce Clause empowers Congress to regulate commerce "among the several States." In *Gibbons v. Ogden*, 22 U.S. 1 (1824), Marshall held that interstate commerce included interstate navigation. By the New Deal era (*Wickard v. Filburn*, 317 U.S. 111 (1942)), interstate commerce had been read to include a farmer's growth of wheat for his own consumption on his own land. By *Gonzales v. Raich*, 545 U.S. 1 (2005), interstate commerce included the cultivation of marijuana for personal medical use under state law. The constitutional text never changed. The drift occurred in the meaning of "commerce" and "among the several States."

**"Reasonable" Searches and Seizures.** The Fourth Amendment requires that searches and seizures be "reasonable" and that warrants issue only on probable cause. *Terry v. Ohio*, 392 U.S. 1 (1968), reinterpreted "reasonable" to permit stops and frisks on less than probable cause. *South Dakota v. Opperman*, 428 U.S. 364 (1976), reinterpreted "reasonable" to permit inventory searches without any cause at all. *California v. Acevedo*, 500 U.S. 565 (1991), expanded the automobile exception to permit searches of containers without warrants. *Florence v. Bd. of Chosen Freeholders*, 566 U.S. 318 (2012), held strip searches "reasonable" even for arrestees charged with minor traffic offenses. The Fourth Amendment text did not change. The operative meaning of "reasonable" did — drastically.

**"Due Process."** The Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments' Due Process Clauses say that no person shall be "deprived of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." The phrase has been variously interpreted to require notice and opportunity to be heard (the original Anglo-American meaning), to require substantive limits on legislation (Lochner-era substantive due process), to require nothing beyond rational-basis review (post-1937), to include a right to bodily integrity (*Rochin v. California*, 342 U.S. 165 (1952)), and most recently to be largely procedural except for a small set of "fundamental rights" identified by the Court. The phrase has not changed. The operative meaning has.

**"Speech" and "Freedom of Speech."** The First Amendment protects "freedom of speech" — a single textual phrase that has been read to include and exclude, at various times, commercial speech, symbolic conduct, conduct intertwined with speech, expressive association, anonymous speech, false statements, threatening statements, fighting words, obscenity, indecency, expressive funding, and various other categories. The text is the same as it was in 1791. The operative meaning has been continuously reconstructed.

### 1.2 The Drift Mechanism

In each of these examples, the drift mechanism is the same:

- The text uses a term ("good faith," "interstate commerce," "reasonable," "due process," "speech") that admits a range of possible meanings.
- The first interpretation is one possible meaning within the range.
- Subsequent interpretations refine, narrow, expand, or repurpose the meaning.
- Each interpretation cites the prior interpretation rather than the original text, accumulating layers of meaning that drift further and further from the textual starting point.
- At some point, the operative meaning becomes incompatible with what the original text plainly said — but the text remains formally the same, and the interpretations are treated as authoritative.

The drift is not malicious in every case. Some of it is the result of legitimate efforts to apply old text to new circumstances. Some of it reflects honest disagreement about meaning. Some of it is, however, the work of actors with institutional interests in particular outcomes — officials who benefit from immunity rules; agencies that benefit from expansive regulatory authority; judges who benefit from doctrinal flexibility that lets them reach the outcomes they prefer.

Regardless of the cause, the result is the same: a law that no longer means what it was written to mean.

## 2. The IS / IS NOT Principle — The Act's Drafting Response

Operation Checkmate is drafted to resist this drift. The drafting principle is direct:

> **Every defined term in the Act specifies both what it IS and what it IS NOT — in operative detail, with illustrative examples on both sides, with explicit cross-references to related terms, and with a construction rule resolving ambiguity in favor of the citizen and against the government actor.**

This is not a literary choice. It is a structural defense against drift. A definition that says only what something IS leaves open the question of what it is not — and that open question becomes the joint where reinterpretation enters. A definition that says both what something IS and what it IS NOT closes the joint. Future interpreters reading the text encounter not just a positive statement but a negative one — not just "X means this" but also "X does not mean that" — and the negative statement constrains the interpretive space within which drift can occur.

The principle has deep roots in the history of legal drafting.

### 2.1 The Mathematical Analog

In mathematics and formal logic, a definition is given by both an extension (what the term refers to) and a comprehension (what properties the term entails). A definition that supplies only one of these is incomplete and admits ambiguity. A complete mathematical definition specifies the term's positive content (its identity, its members, its properties) and its negative content (what it excludes, what it is not, what falls outside its scope).

Legal definitions, when rigorous, follow the same structure. The Act's definitional architecture is closer to mathematical than to ordinary statutory drafting, deliberately so.

### 2.2 The Civil-Law Tradition (Code Law)

The civil-law tradition — Roman law, Justinian's Code and Digest, the Napoleonic Code, the German BGB, and the contemporary code traditions of continental Europe and Latin America — is built on the principle that the law should be expressed in comprehensive code form, with each major concept defined in operative detail, with the system designed to leave as little interpretive joint as possible for judicial discretion.

Justinian's *Institutes* (533 AD) opens with definitions: "Justice is the constant and perpetual will to give each person his due. Jurisprudence is the knowledge of things divine and human, the science of what is just and unjust." Each subsequent provision builds on these definitions, with definitions of "person," "thing," "right," "obligation," and the other foundational concepts of Roman law.

The Anglo-American common-law tradition has, historically, taken the opposite approach: leaving terms undefined, allowing courts to develop meaning through case-by-case adjudication. The result, over centuries, has been a body of law that is enormous, internally inconsistent, and constantly drifting. The civil-law tradition's response is to define rigorously up front and minimize subsequent interpretive drift.

Operation Checkmate borrows the civil-law definitional approach within the American statutory framework. The Act is, in this sense, a code more than it is a typical statute. Each defined term is developed at the level of detail a civilian code would require, and the operative effect is to reduce interpretive drift relative to ordinary American statutory drafting.

### 2.3 The Talmudic Tradition

The Talmud — the body of Jewish law developed over centuries of rabbinic commentary on the Mishnah — exemplifies a different but related approach to definitional rigor. Each concept is debated in elaborate detail, with explicit articulation of what it IS, what it IS NOT, what counterexamples test its boundaries, and what edge cases its proponents are committed to. The Talmudic method does not produce a single "definition" of a concept; it produces a documented record of the bounds of the concept, including the boundary disputes.

The Act's definitions, particularly the umbrella offenses with their enumerated forms, defenses-limited subsections, and cross-references, function similarly. The §1.1.6 Cabalism definition specifies what cabalism IS (elements), gives forms of unjust effect (illustrative), specifies what is included in the umbrella by cross-reference to other offenses, identifies what defenses do NOT apply, and ties the term to other terms via a Construction clause. Future readers encounter not just a textual definition but a documented account of the term's operative boundaries.

### 2.4 Modern Textualist Statutory Interpretation

The textualist tradition in modern American statutory interpretation — Justice Scalia's writing on statutory construction, Bryan Garner's drafting manuals, the Federalist Society's interpretive school — holds that statutes should be read for their textual meaning, with definitions controlling where supplied. Justice Scalia and Bryan Garner, in *Reading Law: The Interpretation of Legal Texts* (2012), articulate a series of canons of textual interpretation that all assume well-drafted text. Where the text is poorly drafted — where key terms are undefined, where positive and negative content are not specified — the textualist method has less work to do, and judicial drift fills the vacuum.

The Act is drafted to give textualist interpretation the maximum surface area. Every key term has both positive and negative content. Every construction clause supplies a controlling interpretive rule. Every cross-reference anchors the term in a network of related definitions. A textualist judge reading the Act has substantially less interpretive discretion than the same judge reading a sparsely-drafted statute, because the Act has done much of the interpretive work in advance, in writing.

### 2.5 The Void-For-Vagueness Doctrine — The Floor

The Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments' Due Process Clauses require that criminal laws be sufficiently definite to give ordinary people notice of what conduct is prohibited. The void-for-vagueness doctrine — *Connally v. General Construction Co.*, 269 U.S. 385 (1926); *Grayned v. City of Rockford*, 408 U.S. 104 (1972); *Johnson v. United States*, 576 U.S. 591 (2015) — invalidates criminal statutes that fail this test.

Vague drafting is constitutionally fragile. Drafting that defines rigorously is constitutionally strong. The Act's definition-heavy approach is, among other things, a deliberate insulation of the Act against vagueness challenges — every umbrella offense has elements, every element has operative content, every term has both positive and negative content. The drafting is built to survive every level of vagueness challenge, all the way to the Supreme Court.

## 3. The IS / IS NOT Principle in the Act's Definitions

Several of the Act's definitions exemplify the IS / IS NOT principle in explicit operative form.

### 3.1 §1.1.1 — Government Actor

**Government Actor IS:** any person exercising or purporting to exercise state or federal authority against a citizen, including elected officials, appointed officials, agency employees, sworn officers, contractors, deputized actors, and any person operating under color of state or federal authority.

**Government Actor IS NOT:** a private person acting in a purely private capacity, without any color of state or federal authority, against another private person. The definition does not reach ordinary private conduct between citizens that has no element of state or federal authorization.

**Edge case anchored:** A private person who impersonates a state actor IS a Government Actor within the meaning of the Act — the actor is exercising apparent state authority, and the citizen-victim is being affected as if by a state actor. The definition reaches the operative reality, not the formal status.

### 3.2 §1.1.3 — Battery (Government Context)

**Battery IS:** the actual deprivation, restraint, harm, or violation of a citizen's rights, liberty, or property, whether through direct action or through any indirect process, instrument, order, proceeding, referral, sign-off, transmission, or other intermediate means producing the same operative effect.

**Battery IS NOT:** any lawful protective action taken under the elemental predicate of a defined offense (§1.1.3.2), proportionate to the predicate, calibrated to the minimum necessary, contemporaneously documented, and consistent with the Performed Duty with an Abundance of Care standard. Lawful protective action under valid predicate is the constitutional exercise of delegated authority; it is not battery within the meaning of this section.

**Edge case anchored:** Paper, process, intermediary, court order, agency referral, automated system, or chain-of-officials does NOT cure a battery (§1.1.3.1). The chain of authorizations does not launder the underlying conduct. Every actor in the chain who knowingly, recklessly, or with deliberate indifference participated is personally liable.

### 3.3 §1.1.5 — Protected Rights

**Rights IS:** to be construed in the broadest possible sense, including all rights enumerated in the U.S. Constitution, all unenumerated rights retained by the people under the Ninth Amendment, all rights in state constitutions, all rights recognized in international human-rights instruments, all rights at common law, all rights long recognized by tradition or natural-law principle, and all rights inherent to human dignity and personal sovereignty.

**Rights IS NOT:** limited to those rights a court has previously adjudicated, those rights currently enumerated in any specific statutory text, those rights government convenience would recognize, or those rights the government actor finds easy to honor. The fact that a right is unwritten, unenumerated, or has not previously been formally adjudicated does NOT preclude its protection.

**Edge case anchored:** A government actor confronted with an ambiguous question of whether a particular interest is a "right" must resolve the ambiguity **in favor of the citizen and against the government actor** (§1.1.5.2). The default rule is the citizen's protection, not the government's convenience.

### 3.4 §1.1.5.3 — Practical Means of Exercising a Right

**Practical Means IS:** the operative ability of an ordinary citizen to exercise a right in real life — including the ability to access the means, afford the means, use the means, and obtain the result the right was meant to secure.

**Practical Means IS NOT:** a paper right that exists only on the page of the Constitution, the statute, or the textbook. A right the citizen cannot in fact exercise is, within the meaning of this Act, not a right; it is a permission granted or withheld at government discretion, and the conversion of a right into a permission is itself reachable under the Act.

**Edge case anchored:** A government actor who acknowledges a right in name while denying, pricing out, criminalizing, licensing away, delaying, obstructing, or making impossible the ordinary means of exercising it has, within the meaning of this Act, deprived the citizen of the right itself. The denial of practical means is the denial of the right.

### 3.5 §1.1.6 — "Evil" (Construction Clause)

**Evil IS:** any unjust law, regulation, enforcement action, or treatment of any person or entity, AND any act, omission, instrument, order, policy, practice, or proceeding that is unconstitutional under the U.S. Constitution, any state constitution, or §1.1.5 rights — including any government conduct that abrogates, converts into a privilege, prices out, criminalizes, licenses away, or otherwise nullifies a protected right in operative reality, regardless of whether a court has previously so held.

**Evil IS NOT:** lawful government conduct within constitutional limits, applying lawful authority under valid predicate, with due care for the citizen's rights, and producing the result the constitutional design contemplates.

**Edge case anchored:** Ambiguity as to whether a given act is "evil" within the meaning of the section resolves AGAINST the government actor. The interpretive default is the citizen's protection.

### 3.6 §1.1.47 — Constitutional Grievance / Lawful Remedy

**Lawful Remedy IS:** a remedy the citizen can in fact access and use in the citizen's actual circumstances — including the practical ability to file, prosecute, attend, and obtain a determination without being driven into financial insecurity, housing crisis, hunger crisis, family crisis, medical crisis, or loss of income/employment caused by the act of pursuing the remedy.

**Lawful Remedy IS NOT:** a remedy nominally available in a court, administrative forum, appeals board, or other proceeding that the citizen cannot in fact use; a remedy held by those who can afford it and withheld from those who cannot; or a deflection ("take it up with the court," "file a complaint," "hire a lawyer," "use the administrative process") offered without first determining whether the citizen can in fact use the remedy directed to.

**Edge case anchored:** The test is the COST OF PURSUING the remedy — not the risk of an adverse judgment on the merits. A citizen who can afford to file retains the right to lose on the law or the facts; the right to lose is not the absence of a remedy.

### 3.7 The Pattern

Each of these definitions exemplifies the IS / IS NOT principle in operative form. Each tells the future reader not just what the term means but what the term does not mean, what edge cases it reaches, and what interpretive defaults govern when ambiguity arises. The pattern is consistent across the Act: every defined term is bounded by both its positive content and its negative content, and the interpretive default at every joint is the citizen's protection.

This is the structural defense against drift. Future interpreters confronting the Act do not encounter open joints they can colonize with reinterpretation. They encounter closed definitions that resist drift on both sides.

## 4. The Misconceptions Section — Defending Definitions Against Bad Readings

Section 1.2 of the Act is dedicated to misconceptions — common misreadings of key terms that the drafters identified and explicitly rejected. The misconceptions section operates as the second-line defense against drift: where a future interpreter might be tempted to reinterpret a defined term in a way the drafters anticipated and rejected, §1.2 is the textual rejection.

The pattern in §1.2 is direct: name the misconception, state the misreading the drafters reject, identify the operative rule that controls instead, and provide an operational rule for triers of fact, councils, courts, and other interpreters.

§1.2.1 (Actual Truth vs. Perceived Truth vs. Representation of Truth) is the leading example. The misconception rejected:

> "This Act explicitly rejects the misconception that an official record, a sworn statement, a certified copy, an officer's testimony, a forensic report, a confession, a guilty plea, or any other formal artifact of the legal process **is itself the actual truth**. These are representations. They are evaluated as representations. They do not bind the trier of fact's inquiry into what actually happened."

The misconception identified — that an official artifact is identical to the actual truth — is the kind of bad reading that, if left undisputed, becomes the operative rule over time. By explicitly rejecting it in the text of the Act, the drafters have closed a major interpretive joint where drift would otherwise enter.

The misconceptions section is, in this sense, a textually-anchored rejection of specific drift patterns. It is not the same as the definitions; it operates on top of them, identifying the specific bad readings the drafters anticipated and disposing of them.

## 5. The Why Folder — Preservation Documents for Posterity

The documents in this `why/` folder are not advocacy materials, marketing copy, or persuasive essays. They are **preservation documents** — written contemporaneously with the Act, by the drafters, explaining the operative intent of each substantive choice, so that posterity reading the Act years or decades from now can verify that the Act still means what it was drafted to mean.

The structural reason for these documents:

1. **Drafters die.** The people who wrote the Act will not be around forever to explain what they meant.
2. **Public memory fades.** The political, social, and legal context in which the Act was drafted will be forgotten within a generation, replaced by whatever context becomes current.
3. **Interpretive culture shifts.** The interpretive methods favored by future courts may differ from those of the present. Textualist interpretation, originalist interpretation, purposivist interpretation, living-constitutionalist interpretation each produce different results from the same text; the preferred method changes over time.
4. **Institutional incentives drift.** The institutional actors who interpret the Act over time — courts, agencies, prosecutors, the Citizen Council itself — develop their own incentives, cultures, and preferred outcomes. These can drift away from the drafters' original purposes.

Against these forces, the why-folder documents are textual evidence of original intent that future interpreters cannot avoid encountering. They function similarly to the *Federalist Papers* with respect to the U.S. Constitution: contemporaneous explanatory documents written by the drafters, available to future readers, providing direct evidence of what the drafters meant.

The why-folder documents are not, themselves, the Act. They do not have the force of law. But they have the force of contemporaneous evidence — the highest grade of evidence about what a text meant when it was written. Where future interpretation conflicts with the contemporaneous explanation, the conflict is itself evidence of drift, and the burden falls on the interpreter to explain why the contemporaneous explanation is wrong.

## 6. The Drafting Conventions the Act Uses Throughout

Beyond the IS / IS NOT principle, the Act uses several specific drafting conventions to reinforce its anti-drift architecture:

### 6.1 Operative-Reality Tests

Many definitions in the Act include an operative-reality test — a rule that the term applies to what actually happens in real life, not to what the formal labels say is happening. This appears in:

- §1.1.5.3 (Practical Means) — the practical ability to exercise a right, not the paper right alone
- §1.1.3 (Battery) — the operative effect on the citizen's rights, not the formal regularity of the instrument used
- §1.1.45 (Non-Republican Government Act) — the operative character of the government, not the institutional label
- §1.1.47 (Carceralism, lawful remedy) — what the citizen can actually do, not what the system theoretically allows

The operative-reality test is the structural answer to formalism. A future interpreter cannot defeat the Act by pointing to formal labels that don't match operative reality — the Act expressly looks past the labels to the operative effect.

### 6.2 Explicit Cross-References

Each defined term is cross-referenced to every related term. The result is a network of definitions in which each term is anchored in multiple other terms. A future interpreter cannot easily redefine one term without affecting the others; the cross-references make the network of meanings mutually constraining.

### 6.3 "Without Limitation" Language

Definitions that enumerate forms or examples use "including, without limitation" or "such as" language — making clear that the enumeration is illustrative, not exhaustive. This prevents the most common bad-faith drift pattern: arguing that anything not specifically enumerated falls outside the definition.

### 6.4 Defenses Limited Subsections

Each umbrella offense includes a "Defenses Limited" subsection identifying specific defenses that are NOT available. This is the negative content of the offense in operative form — a list of arguments future actors might raise that the Act rejects in advance.

### 6.5 Construction Clauses

Each umbrella offense includes a Construction clause tying the term to other terms and resolving ambiguity in favor of the citizen. The Construction clauses are the operative interpretive rules that govern when ambiguity arises.

### 6.6 Cumulative-Charging Architecture

The Act expressly contemplates cumulative charges (§1.1.1.1) — multiple offenses charged on the same conduct where the elements of each are met. This prevents a future interpreter from defeating multiple counts by reading them as alternative formulations of a single underlying claim. Cumulative charging is the structural defense against the "you can only get one bite" misreading.

### 6.7 The Statement of Intent as Binding Rule of Construction

The Statement of Intent at the beginning of the Act is, by its own terms, "a binding rule of construction." Every provision is to be construed in favor of actual truth over official narrative, in favor of the citizen, in favor of the actually innocent, against coercion disguised as process, and against any immunity, custom, or practice that shields a government actor from accountability. The interpretive default is built into the Act, not left to whatever interpretive method future courts may favor.

## 7. The Combined Effect — Drift-Resistance at Multiple Levels

The drafting architecture of Operation Checkmate is layered. Each layer provides drift-resistance against a different vulnerability:

1. **The IS / IS NOT principle** in each definition resists drift at the term level.
2. **The misconceptions section** resists drift at the level of bad readings the drafters anticipated.
3. **The cross-references** resist drift by anchoring each term in the network of others.
4. **The construction clauses and ambiguity-resolution rules** resist drift at every interpretive joint.
5. **The operative-reality tests** resist drift through formalism.
6. **The Statement of Intent's binding rule of construction** resists drift at the level of overall interpretive method.
7. **The why-folder documents** resist drift across time by providing contemporaneous evidence of original intent.
8. **Section 1.1.25 (Legislate from the Bench)** makes judicial drift through doctrinal invention itself a substantive offense, providing the criminal-law backstop against the most dangerous form of drift.

No single defense is impregnable. Drift is a constant force, applied by every actor with an institutional incentive to read the Act differently than it was drafted. The Act's layered architecture is intended to make drift in any direction — narrowing protections for citizens, expanding immunities for officials, hollowing out the practical means of exercising rights, reinterpreting the operative scope of an offense, replacing the original meaning with the institutional convenience of the day — substantially more difficult than it would be against an ordinary statute.

## 8. Conclusion — Definitions as Constitutional Discipline

Definitions are not a stylistic choice. They are the operative architecture of a law. A statute that defines its terms rigorously, identifies its operative scope, anchors its terms in a network of related definitions, names the bad readings it rejects, and preserves its original intent in contemporaneous explanatory documents — that statute has substantially more durability than one that does not.

Operation Checkmate is drafted with definitional rigor for the same reason careful contracts are drafted with definitional rigor, careful regulations are drafted with definitional rigor, and the most durable laws in every legal tradition have been drafted with definitional rigor: because the alternative is interpretive drift, and interpretive drift is the universal solvent that turns every important law into a doctrine of whatever institutional convenience requires.

The IS / IS NOT principle, the misconceptions section, the cross-references, the construction clauses, the operative-reality tests, the Statement of Intent's binding rule of construction, the cumulative-charging architecture, and the why-folder preservation documents are all parts of the same anti-drift architecture. They exist for posterity — for the readers, judges, councils, officials, and citizens who will encounter the Act years or decades after the people who drafted it are gone.

The Act is written to mean what it says. The definitions are written to keep meaning what they say. The why documents are written to remind posterity what the Act was drafted to mean.

That is constitutional discipline. That is what the Act is for.
