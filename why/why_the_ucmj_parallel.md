# Why the UCMJ Parallel — The Third Regime of Authority

The Uniform Code of Military Justice (10 U.S.C. §§ 801–946) is the comprehensive disciplinary code that governs the United States military. It is more than seventy years old in its modern form (enacted 1950, building on the earlier Articles of War dating to 1775 and the Articles for the Government of the Navy dating to 1799). It is real, enforced, applied to actors of every rank from private to general officer, and has produced courts-martial in numbers ranging from the thousands per year in active operational periods to hundreds per year in peacetime. It is comprehensive — covering dereliction of duty, cruelty and maltreatment, conduct unbecoming an officer, abuse of authority, and a long list of specific offenses — and it is operatively functional in a way that no comparable American legal architecture is.

Operation Checkmate is, in essence, the parallel disciplinary code for the third regime of authority — the **civilian government acting on citizens** — which has never had one. The structural argument the Act rests on is direct: American law already imposes comprehensive criminal-law-style discipline on two of the three regimes of authority. The third regime, the one that produces the largest operative impact on the daily lives of ordinary citizens, has been left without such a code. The Act corrects the structural anomaly.

This document develops the structural argument from the three regimes of authority, the substantive parallels between the UCMJ's operative offenses and the Act's, the parallel command-responsibility and superior-liability doctrines, the parallel enforcement architecture, the historical reason civilian government was never given a disciplinary code, and the operative answer to the question why it should be given one now.

The companion document [why_criminal_not_just_civil.md](why_criminal_not_just_civil.md) develops the broader strategic choice that criminal liability — not civil liability alone — is the operative response to the failure of civil-rights enforcement. This document develops the specific structural argument from the UCMJ parallel: not only that criminal-law discipline is the correct operative response, but that the operative architecture is already established in American law for one of the three regimes of authority, and that the structural anomaly is the absence of the parallel architecture for the third.

## 1. The Three Regimes of Authority

American constitutional architecture, as developed in [why_does_the_government_even_exist.md](why_does_the_government_even_exist.md), recognizes a structural hierarchy in which the citizen is the constituent sovereign and government exists as the citizen's agent. Within this hierarchy, three operative regimes exercise distinct forms of authority over different operative populations.

### 1.1 The First Regime — The Citizen Subject to Ordinary Criminal Law

The first regime is the citizen subject to the ordinary criminal code. In Utah, this is Title 76, the Utah Criminal Code, comprising several hundred operative sections defining offenses, mens-rea requirements, classifications, penalties, defenses, and procedural rules. The citizen who commits murder, robbery, fraud, theft, assault, perjury, or any other conduct the Code reaches faces the operative consequences the Code provides.

The Utah Criminal Code is comprehensive. It reaches virtually every form of operatively significant conduct citizens engage in toward other citizens or toward operative public interests. The mens-rea architecture is calibrated (intentionally, knowingly, recklessly, negligently). The classification architecture is calibrated (infractions, misdemeanors, felonies, capital). The enforcement architecture is operatively functional (prosecutorial offices, courts, juries, appellate review, the operative consequence of conviction).

The first regime has been governed by an operative disciplinary code throughout American history. The colonial period inherited the English common-law tradition; the federal period built the federal criminal-law architecture; the state period built the state criminal-law architectures; the modern period codified and rationalized the architectures into the Codes that now govern. The first regime's operative discipline is the operative norm — the architecture every citizen experiences as the default operative consequence of conduct.

### 1.2 The Second Regime — The Military Subject to the UCMJ

The second regime is the United States military subject to the Uniform Code of Military Justice. The UCMJ governs members of the Army, Navy, Marine Corps, Air Force, Space Force, and Coast Guard (when serving as part of the Navy or activated under the United States), as well as cadets, midshipmen, and certain other defined categories of persons connected to the military.

The UCMJ is operatively comprehensive. Its punitive articles (10 U.S.C. §§ 877–934) reach the full operative range of conduct that requires discipline within the military context — offenses against the operative discipline of the unit (Articles 77–87b), offenses against the operative authority of superiors (Articles 88–95), offenses involving moral turpitude and operative misconduct (Articles 119–134), and the general article (Article 134) reaching "all disorders and neglects to the prejudice of good order and discipline in the armed forces, all conduct of a nature to bring discredit upon the armed forces, and crimes and offenses not capital."

The UCMJ's enforcement architecture is operatively functional. Courts-martial — summary, special, and general — operate as the operative tribunals. The Manual for Courts-Martial provides the operative procedural rules. The Judge Advocate General's Corps provides the operative legal architecture. The Court of Appeals for the Armed Forces provides operative appellate review. The Supreme Court has appellate jurisdiction over certain UCMJ cases.

The second regime has been governed by an operative disciplinary code throughout American history. The 1775 Articles of War (adopted by the Continental Congress) and the 1799 Articles for the Government of the Navy were the colonial-era inheritances; the various nineteenth and early twentieth-century articles were the modern predecessors; the UCMJ as enacted in 1950 was the comprehensive modern codification. The second regime's operative discipline is, like the first regime's, the operative norm for the actors it governs.

### 1.3 The Third Regime — The Civilian Government Acting on Citizens

The third regime is the civilian government in its operative conduct toward citizens — the executive, legislative, and judicial actors at federal, state, and local levels, in their operative exercise of state power over citizens within the operative jurisdiction.

The third regime has not been governed by a comprehensive disciplinary code. The architecture that currently operates produces, in operative reality, a structural asymmetry:

- The substantive criminal code reaches government actors only as it reaches ordinary citizens (Title 76 of the Utah Code, the equivalent in other jurisdictions), without a comprehensive set of operative offenses tailored to the operative conduct that abuse of public authority produces.
- 42 U.S.C. § 1983 provides a civil-liability cause of action that has been hollowed out by qualified immunity, indemnification, and the broader operative pattern developed in [why_criminal_not_just_civil.md](why_criminal_not_just_civil.md).
- 18 U.S.C. § 242 provides federal criminal liability for civil-rights violations under color of authority, but is operatively used in only the most extreme cases and depends on federal prosecutorial discretion that has, in operative practice, been operatively constrained.
- State criminal prosecution of government actors for misconduct in office is, in operative practice, infrequent — for reasons including the prosecutorial-discretion problem developed in [why_no_major_party_has_pushed_this_bill.md](why_no_major_party_has_pushed_this_bill.md), the immunity-doctrine problem, and the broader institutional pattern.
- Administrative discipline, internal-affairs review, and civilian oversight boards exist but are operatively constrained by institutional capture, the absence of operative authority to impose criminal consequences, and the broader operative pattern developed in [why_no_major_party_has_pushed_this_bill.md](why_no_major_party_has_pushed_this_bill.md).

The cumulative operative effect is that the third regime — the regime whose conduct most directly affects the daily lives of ordinary citizens — operates under operative discipline substantially weaker than the operative discipline applied to the first regime (citizens under the ordinary criminal code) and substantially weaker than the operative discipline applied to the second regime (military actors under the UCMJ).

### 1.4 The Structural Anomaly

The structural anomaly is direct. The two regimes that have, in operative architecture, comprehensive criminal-law-style discipline — citizens and military — are subject to operative codes calibrated to the operative authority each exercises. The third regime, whose actors exercise the operative authority of the state apparatus toward citizens, has been left without the parallel code.

The anomaly is not justified by any operative principle. The operative reasons each of the two existing regimes is subject to comprehensive criminal-law discipline apply with at least equal force to the third regime:

- Citizens are subject to comprehensive discipline because their operative conduct affects other citizens, and the operative architecture of accountability is calibrated to the operative consequences. **Government actors' operative conduct affects citizens at greater operative scale, with greater operative resources, and with operative consequences calibrated to the institutional position of the actor.**
- Military members are subject to comprehensive discipline because they exercise operative authority — including the operative authority over life, liberty, and the operative conduct of the unit — that requires calibrated operative consequence. **Civilian government actors exercise comparable operative authority over citizens, including life, liberty, and the operative conduct of communities.**

The structural argument is therefore direct: the operative principle that has justified comprehensive criminal-law discipline for the other two regimes justifies, with at least equal operative force, the parallel discipline for the third regime. The Act is the operative codification of this structural conclusion.

## 2. The Substantive Parallels — Article by Article

The UCMJ's punitive articles map directly to the Act's substantive offenses across a wide range of operative conduct. The parallels are not coincidental. Both codes are responding to the operative reality that actors clothed with significant authority require operative discipline calibrated to the operative authority exercised.

### 2.1 Dereliction of Duty — UCMJ Article 92 / Act §17.5

UCMJ Article 92 (10 U.S.C. § 892) reaches dereliction of duty:

> "Any person subject to this chapter who — (1) violates or fails to obey any lawful general order or regulation; (2) having knowledge of any other lawful order issued by a member of the armed forces, which it is his duty to obey, fails to obey the order; or (3) is derelict in the performance of his duties; shall be punished as a court-martial may direct."

The Act's §17.5 (Gross Dereliction of Public Duty) reaches the operative parallel — civilian government actors' failure to perform the operative duties of office. The substantive content is parallel: actors exercising operative authority have operative duties; failure to perform those duties produces operative consequence calibrated to the operative magnitude of the failure.

### 2.2 Cruelty and Maltreatment — UCMJ Article 93 / Act §§ 1.1.12, 1.1.13, 1.1.47

UCMJ Article 93 (10 U.S.C. § 893) reaches cruelty and maltreatment:

> "Any person subject to this chapter who is guilty of cruelty toward, or oppression or maltreatment of, any person subject to his orders shall be punished as a court-martial may direct."

The Act's §1.1.12 (Terrorism — Government Context), §1.1.13 (Official Intimidation), and §1.1.47 (Carceralism — Criminalization of Citizens in Crisis) reach the operative parallel. The military's prohibition on cruelty and oppression of "any person subject to his orders" tracks the civilian-government parallel of cruelty and oppression of any citizen subject to the actor's operative authority. The substantive content is operatively analogous; the Act's broader set of substantive offenses develops the operative architecture across the range of conduct civilian government actors engage in.

### 2.3 Conduct Unbecoming an Officer — UCMJ Article 133 / Act §17.1

UCMJ Article 133 (10 U.S.C. § 933) reaches conduct unbecoming an officer:

> "Any commissioned officer, cadet, or midshipman who is convicted of conduct unbecoming an officer and a gentleman shall be punished as a court-martial may direct."

The Act's §17.1 (Civic Breach of Trust) reaches the operative parallel — the operative duty of a public officer to conduct themselves in a manner consistent with the operative trust of office. The military's "officer and gentleman" standard tracks the civilian-government parallel of the operative standard expected of officials holding public trust. The Act's broader Section 17 role-specific offenses develop the operative content across the operative offices the Act reaches.

### 2.4 The General Article — UCMJ Article 134 / Act §1.1.18

UCMJ Article 134 (10 U.S.C. § 934) is the general article reaching:

> "all disorders and neglects to the prejudice of good order and discipline in the armed forces, all conduct of a nature to bring discredit upon the armed forces, and crimes and offenses not capital, of which persons subject to this chapter may be guilty, shall be taken cognizance of by a general, special, or summary court-martial."

The Act's §1.1.18 (Terms Describing Unlawful Official Conduct) and the broader operative architecture of substantive offenses developed across §§ 1.1.2–1.1.47 and Section 17 reach the operative parallel. The "good order and discipline" framework tracks the civilian-government parallel of the operative integrity of the constitutional order; conduct "to bring discredit upon" the armed forces tracks the civilian-government parallel of conduct to bring discredit upon the public office or the constitutional order.

### 2.5 Specific Offenses — UCMJ Articles 119, 120, 121, 124, etc.

The UCMJ's specific punitive articles reach defined operative conduct: manslaughter (Article 119), rape and sexual assault (Article 120), larceny and wrongful appropriation (Article 121), maiming (Article 124), and others. Each article reaches operative conduct calibrated to the operative magnitude of the harm.

The Act's substantive offenses reach the operative parallels for civilian-government conduct. §1.1.3 (Battery — Government Context) reaches operative conduct producing physical or rights-based harm; §1.1.6 (Cabalism) reaches coordinated misconduct; §1.1.7 (Government Racketeering) reaches operative coordination for joint advantage; §1.1.12 (Terrorism) reaches conduct producing fear at operative magnitude; §1.1.14 (Malicious Prosecution) reaches abuse of the operative criminal-process apparatus; §1.1.25 (Legislate from the Bench) reaches judicial conduct exceeding the Article III operative function; §1.1.46 (Judicial Imposter) reaches executive or administrative conduct exceeding the operative scope of the office.

The substantive content of the Act's offenses parallels the operative architecture of the UCMJ's specific punitive articles. Both codes reach operative conduct calibrated to the operative authority each set of actors exercises.

## 3. The Parallel Command-Responsibility Doctrine

The UCMJ's command-responsibility doctrine reaches superior officers whose subordinate conduct produced operative harm to citizens or operative violations of the law of armed conflict. The doctrine has operative parallels in the Act's reach to supervisory and institutional actors.

### 3.1 The UCMJ's Command Responsibility

The operative foundation of military command responsibility under American law traces to *In re Yamashita*, 327 U.S. 1 (1946), in which the Supreme Court upheld the conviction of General Tomoyuki Yamashita for war crimes committed by troops under his command. The operative principle: a superior officer who knew, or should have known, of the operative misconduct of subordinates and failed to take operative action to prevent or punish the misconduct is criminally responsible for the misconduct.

The doctrine has been carried forward through the Geneva Conventions and their Additional Protocols, the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court (Article 28), the ICTY and ICTR jurisprudence, and the modern American military justice architecture. The operative principle is consistent: command responsibility reaches superior officers whose operative failure of operative oversight produced subordinate misconduct.

### 3.2 The Act's Parallel Architecture

The Act reaches operative parallels at multiple operative levels.

- **§1.1.6 (Cabalism)** reaches coordinated misconduct by two or more government actors, with operative reach to all actors in the coordination chain. A supervisor who coordinated with subordinates to produce operative misconduct is reached as one of the coordinating actors.
- **§5.1 (Fixing the Immunity Gap)** reaches both individual and entity-level government actors, with operative reach to institutional actors whose operative oversight failed to prevent or punish the operative misconduct.
- **§1.1.7 (Government Racketeering)** reaches operative coordination for joint advantage, with operative reach to the operative chain of actors participating in or directing the coordination.
- **§17 (Role-Specific Public Official Offenses)** reaches operative offenses calibrated to the operative role of the actor, including supervisory and executive roles.

The Act's operative architecture is structurally parallel to the UCMJ's command-responsibility doctrine. Where a supervisor or institutional actor knew of subordinate misconduct meeting the elements of the Act's substantive offenses, or where the supervisor's operative failure of oversight produced the operative conditions under which the subordinate misconduct occurred, the supervisor is reached under the Act's substantive provisions. The operative parallel is direct.

## 4. The Parallel Enforcement Architecture

The UCMJ's enforcement architecture provides the operative tribunals (courts-martial), the operative prosecution (Judge Advocate General's Corps), and the operative appellate review (the service courts of criminal appeals, the Court of Appeals for the Armed Forces, and the Supreme Court for certain categories). The architecture is operationally functional — courts-martial occur regularly, convictions are obtained, appellate review is conducted, the operative consequences are imposed.

The Act's parallel enforcement architecture operates through:

### 4.1 The Citizen Council Mechanism (Section 9)

The Citizen Council mechanism, developed in detail in [why_this_is_not_a_letter_of_mark.md](why_this_is_not_a_letter_of_mark.md), is the operative parallel to the UCMJ's court-martial structure for civilian-government discipline. The Council is convened under defined operative procedures, deliberates on a developed record, and issues a Joint Declaration of Prosecution with the legal force of a grand-jury indictment.

The structural function is parallel to the convening of a court-martial: a tribunal is empaneled, with operative authority to consider the operative facts, and operative process is initiated. The differences are operative — the Citizen Council operates in the civilian-court system rather than in a military tribunal, with the case proceeding through ordinary state-court criminal prosecution — but the operative architecture is functionally analogous.

### 4.2 The County Prosecutor's Office

The County Prosecutor's Office, where it functions as designed, operates as the operative parallel to the Judge Advocate General's Corps. The prosecutor brings the case, presents the operative evidence, examines witnesses, and seeks conviction. Where the County Prosecutor declines to charge a meritorious case, the Citizen Council mechanism provides the operative pathway. Where the Citizen Council issues a Joint Declaration of Prosecution and the County Prosecutor declines to act, the Act provides for designation of counsel through the Office of the Governor.

The operative architecture produces the parallel functional outcome: cases are prosecuted by competent counsel in the operative tribunal, with the operative consequences flowing from conviction.

### 4.3 The State-Court System

The Utah state courts operate as the operative parallel to the courts-martial system. The case proceeds through arraignment, discovery, motion practice, trial by jury under the Sixth Amendment, and conviction or acquittal on the merits. The operative consequences flow from conviction. Appellate review proceeds through the Utah Court of Appeals and the Utah Supreme Court, with potential certiorari to the U.S. Supreme Court.

The operative architecture is functionally analogous to the UCMJ's enforcement architecture, with the operative tribunals appropriately scaled to the civilian context.

### 4.4 The Operative Consequence Architecture

Section 11 of the Act provides the operative parallel to the UCMJ's punitive architecture. Conviction under the Act produces:

- Forfeiture of public office (§11.1) — parallel to the UCMJ's reduction in rank, dismissal from service, or dishonorable discharge consequences
- Permanent ineligibility for future public office (§11.2) — parallel to the UCMJ's permanent operative effect on the actor's military service record
- Automatic decertification, disbarment, and removal from professional standing (§11.4) — parallel to the UCMJ's operative effect on the actor's professional military standing
- Pension forfeiture where the offense involves serious breach of public trust — parallel to the UCMJ's forfeiture of pay and allowances and the operative effect on retirement benefits
- The standard operative consequences of criminal conviction under Utah law — parallel to the UCMJ's confinement and other punitive consequences

The operative consequence architecture is structurally parallel. The Act provides, in the civilian-government context, the operative consequence architecture the UCMJ provides in the military context.

## 5. The Historical Reason the Third Regime Was Never Given a Comprehensive Code

The historical reason the third regime — civilian government acting on citizens — was never given a comprehensive disciplinary code is itself instructive. The Founders did not consider the regime fundamentally different from the other two; they assumed that the operative architecture of constitutional government would produce sufficient operative discipline through the institutional checks they designed.

### 5.1 The Founders' Assumption

The Founders' framework, developed in [why_does_the_government_even_exist.md](why_does_the_government_even_exist.md) and [why_republican_form_of_government_has_to_be_a_christian_government.md](why_republican_form_of_government_has_to_be_a_christian_government.md), treated government actors as fiduciaries holding power in trust for the citizens. The operative discipline was, in the Founders' framework, to be produced by:

- The operative dispositional formation of the actors themselves (the Christian moral disposition developed in [why_we_are_a_christian_nation.md](why_we_are_a_christian_nation.md))
- The institutional checks among the branches of government (Madison's framework in *Federalist No. 51*)
- The electoral process producing accountability through periodic reelection
- The state-federal balance providing structural protection against federal overreach
- The Bill of Rights providing operative substantive limits on government conduct
- The criminal-law architecture (Title 76 and equivalents) reaching the most severe forms of misconduct

The Founders did not anticipate the operative architecture's failure across all of these joints simultaneously. The dispositional formation of officials has, in operative reality, degraded as the broader culture has drifted from the framework the Founders built on. The institutional checks have weakened as the federal architecture has grown and the operative balance has shifted. The electoral process has been operatively constrained by the gatekeeper dynamics developed in [why_no_major_party_has_pushed_this_bill.md](why_no_major_party_has_pushed_this_bill.md). The state-federal balance has been substantially compromised. The Bill of Rights has been hollowed out by judicial doctrine. The criminal-law architecture has, in operative practice, not been applied to government actors with anything approaching the operative consistency it is applied to ordinary citizens.

The operative reality, two and a half centuries into the constitutional experiment, is that the Founders' assumption has not held. The operative discipline of the third regime is, by every operative measure, weaker than the operative discipline of the first regime (citizens) and the second regime (military). The structural correction the Act produces is the operative response to the operative failure of the Founders' assumption.

### 5.2 The UCMJ's Different Historical Trajectory

The military's trajectory was different. The military was, from the colonial period forward, recognized as requiring operative discipline calibrated to the operative authority exercised. The 1775 Articles of War were among the first operative acts of the Continental Congress. The 1799 Articles for the Government of the Navy followed. The military's operative discipline has been continuously codified, revised, and applied across the entire arc of American history.

The reason for the different trajectory was the operative recognition that the military, exercising operative authority over its members and producing operative consequences on civilian populations through the conduct of operations, required calibrated operative discipline that the ordinary criminal-law architecture could not produce. The recognition was correct. The UCMJ's operative architecture has, across seventy-five years of operative practice, produced the operative discipline that the assumption applied to the military required.

The Act's operative argument is that the same operative recognition should now be applied to the third regime. Civilian government actors, exercising operative authority over citizens and producing operative consequences calibrated to the institutional position of the actor, require calibrated operative discipline that the ordinary criminal-law architecture has, in operative practice, not produced.

### 5.3 The Operative Lesson

The operative lesson is direct. The UCMJ exists because the operative reality of military authority requires calibrated operative discipline. The same operative reality applies, with at least equal force, to civilian government authority. The Act is the operative correction of the structural anomaly the Founders' assumption has produced.

## 6. The Operative Answer to the Foreseeable Objection

The foreseeable objection is that the UCMJ parallel is structurally inapt — that the military operates under operative conditions (combat, the operative chain of command, the operative requirement of immediate obedience) that distinguish military discipline from civilian-government discipline, and that the parallel therefore fails. The objection misreads the operative parallel in three respects.

### 6.1 The Operative Authority, Not the Operative Conditions, Is the Relevant Parallel

The relevant operative parallel is not the conditions under which the actors operate but the operative authority each set of actors exercises. The UCMJ's operative architecture is calibrated to the operative authority military members exercise (over each other and over civilians affected by operations). The Act's operative architecture is calibrated to the operative authority civilian government actors exercise (over citizens within the jurisdiction).

The conditions differ — the military operates in combat, in deployed environments, under the operative chain of command; civilian government operates in courthouses, in offices, under the operative chain of administrative hierarchy — but the operative principle is the same: where significant operative authority is exercised, calibrated operative discipline is required.

### 6.2 The Civilian Government's Operative Impact on Citizens Is Greater, Not Less

The operative impact of the civilian government on the daily lives of citizens is, by every operative measure, greater than the operative impact of the military on the daily lives of citizens. The vast majority of citizens encounter the civilian government regularly — in traffic stops, in regulatory enforcement, in custody proceedings, in tax administration, in education and licensing systems, in the operative architecture of permits and inspections — and rarely encounter the military.

The operative consequences of civilian government misconduct, accumulated across the operative range of citizen-government interactions, are, in operative reality, greater than the operative consequences of military misconduct against civilian populations in the modern American context. The operative argument for comprehensive operative discipline of the civilian government is therefore stronger, not weaker, than the operative argument for comprehensive operative discipline of the military.

### 6.3 The UCMJ's Operative Functionality Demonstrates the Architecture Works

The empirical record of seventy-five years of UCMJ enforcement demonstrates that comprehensive criminal-law-style discipline of a regime exercising significant operative authority is operatively feasible. The system produces courts-martial regularly, achieves convictions, applies operative consequences calibrated to the operative severity of the conduct, and maintains the operative functionality of the military's operational mission. The objection that comprehensive discipline of the civilian government would be operatively unfeasible cannot survive the operative demonstration of the UCMJ's functionality.

The Act applies the same operative architecture, scaled to the civilian-government context, with the operative enforcement mechanism (Citizen Council, state-court prosecution, Section 11 forfeiture) calibrated to the operative scope of the regime. The operative demonstration of the UCMJ is the operative answer to the feasibility objection.

## 7. The Moral Argument

The structural argument is, at its operative foundation, a moral argument. If it is right that the military be governed by a real disciplinary code — and it is; no serious argument disputes this — it is right that the civilian government be governed by one too.

### 7.1 The Operative Reasons the UCMJ Is Right

No serious argument disputes that the UCMJ should exist. The operative reasons are direct:

- Military members exercise operative authority that, if abused, produces operative consequences calibrated to the institutional position of the actor.
- The operative architecture of accountability must be calibrated to the operative authority exercised.
- The ordinary criminal-law architecture, applied uniformly across civilian and military contexts, cannot produce the operative discipline the military's operative authority requires.
- A comprehensive operative code, calibrated to the operative reality of military authority and enforced through operative tribunals calibrated to the operative context, is the operative response.

The UCMJ is therefore right. Its operative existence, its operative reach, and its operative enforcement are right because the operative reality of military authority requires them.

### 7.2 The Operative Reasons the Same Principle Applies to Civilian Government

The operative reasons the same principle applies to the civilian government are direct:

- Civilian government actors exercise operative authority that, if abused, produces operative consequences calibrated to the institutional position of the actor.
- The operative architecture of accountability must be calibrated to the operative authority exercised.
- The ordinary criminal-law architecture, applied to civilian government actors as it is applied to ordinary citizens, has, in operative practice, not produced the operative discipline the civilian government's operative authority requires.
- A comprehensive operative code, calibrated to the operative reality of civilian government authority and enforced through operative mechanisms calibrated to the civilian context, is the operative response.

The operative principle is symmetrical. The Act is right for the operative reasons the UCMJ is right.

### 7.3 The Operative Asymmetry the Act Corrects

The current operative asymmetry — comprehensive operative discipline for the military, comprehensive operative discipline for citizens, but no comprehensive operative discipline for the civilian government acting on citizens — is the structural anomaly the Act corrects. The correction is not novel. It is the operative application of the principle already accepted for the other two regimes.

If the UCMJ is right, the Act is right. If the Act is wrong, the UCMJ is wrong. The two stand or fall together on the operative principle. The Act's operative argument is that they stand together, and that the operative existence of the UCMJ is itself part of the moral case for the Act.

## 8. Conclusion

The Act is the parallel disciplinary code for the third regime of authority — the civilian government acting on citizens — because:

1. **American constitutional architecture recognizes three operative regimes of authority.** The citizen subject to the ordinary criminal code, the military subject to the UCMJ, and the civilian government acting on citizens. Two of the three have comprehensive operative discipline. The third has not.

2. **The UCMJ is operatively comprehensive, operatively functional, and operatively right.** Seventy-five years of operative practice demonstrate that comprehensive criminal-law-style discipline of a regime exercising significant operative authority is operatively feasible and operatively effective.

3. **The substantive parallels are direct.** UCMJ Article 92 (dereliction of duty), Article 93 (cruelty and maltreatment), Article 133 (conduct unbecoming an officer), Article 134 (the general article), and the specific punitive articles parallel the Act's substantive offenses across the operative range of conduct.

4. **The command-responsibility doctrine has direct parallels in the Act's operative architecture.** *Yamashita*'s command-responsibility principle reaches supervisors whose operative oversight failed to prevent or punish subordinate misconduct; the Act's §1.1.6, §5.1, and Section 17 reach the operative parallel for civilian-government supervisory and institutional actors.

5. **The enforcement architecture has direct parallels.** The Citizen Council mechanism of Section 9 parallels the court-martial structure; the County Prosecutor's Office (or designated counsel under §9) parallels the Judge Advocate General's Corps; the state-court system parallels the courts-martial system; Section 11's operative consequence architecture parallels the UCMJ's punitive architecture.

6. **The historical reason the third regime was never given a comprehensive code is the Founders' assumption that the operative architecture of constitutional government would produce sufficient operative discipline.** The assumption has not held. The operative architecture has failed across the joints the Founders relied on. The Act is the structural correction.

7. **The moral argument is symmetrical.** If the UCMJ is right (and it is, no serious argument disputes this), the Act is right. The operative principle that has justified comprehensive criminal-law discipline for the other two regimes justifies, with at least equal operative force, the parallel discipline for the third regime.

8. **The structural anomaly the Act corrects is not novel.** It is the operative application of a principle already accepted for the other two regimes. The Act's operative existence is the operative completion of the architecture the constitutional design implicitly requires.

The third regime of authority — the civilian government acting on citizens — exercises operative authority calibrated to the institutional position of its actors. The operative architecture of accountability must be calibrated to the operative authority exercised. The UCMJ demonstrates that the calibrated operative discipline is operatively feasible. The Act applies the same operative principle in the civilian-government context.

If the military be governed by a real disciplinary code, the civilian government be governed by one too. The Act is that code.
