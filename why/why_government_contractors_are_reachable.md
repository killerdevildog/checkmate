# Why Government Contractors Are Reachable Under the Act

§1.1.1 of Operation Checkmate defines "Government Actor" to reach any person exercising or purporting to exercise state or federal authority against a citizen — including private contractors performing government functions under contract, grant, deputization, or other operative arrangement that places the actor in the operative position of exercising state power. The operative principle: **if you are exercising the state's power over a citizen, you are bound by the state's rules**.

The Act's definition is broader than the federal §1983 state-actor doctrine but operates on the same operative foundation. Private contractors operating under color of state or federal authority — private prison and detention staff, private probation officers, private debt-collection contractors enforcing court-ordered debt, private vendors of government technology that exercises decisional authority over citizens, private security firms operating on government contracts, foster placements and institutional custodians, public-private partners performing coercive functions, and the broader category of actors who, in operative reality, exercise state authority without holding state employment — are within the operative reach of the Act.

The doctrinal grounding for the contractor reach is direct. The Supreme Court has, across the operative arc of state-action jurisprudence, consistently held that the constitutional and statutory architecture reaches conduct performed under color of state authority regardless of the actor's W-2. The operative line is the exercise of state power, not the institutional source of the actor's paycheck. This document develops the doctrinal grounding, the structural argument against privatization as an operative shield, the specific categories of contractor conduct the Act reaches, the operative reach to federal contractors under §14.7, and the operative consequence of the contractor-reach principle.

## 1. The Doctrinal Grounding — The State-Action Cases

The federal courts have, in the operative arc of §1983 jurisprudence, developed a consistent line of cases reaching private actors who exercise state authority. The Supreme Court's operative framework treats the question functionally: does the operative conduct meet the elements of state action, regardless of the actor's formal employment status?

### 1.1 *Lugar v. Edmondson Oil Co.*, 457 U.S. 922 (1982) — The Joint-Action Test

*Lugar v. Edmondson Oil Co.*, 457 U.S. 922 (1982), is the operative foundation of the joint-action test for state-actor status. The case involved a private creditor who obtained a state-court attachment order against a debtor's property, executed through the operative cooperation of state officers. The debtor sued the private creditor under §1983, alleging violation of due-process rights.

The Supreme Court held that the private creditor was operatively a state actor for §1983 purposes. The operative test:

> "A deprivation of a federal right can be inflicted under color of state law only when the conduct allegedly causing the deprivation of a federal right is fairly attributable to the State. ... [A]ction by a private party pursuant to this statute, without something more, was not sufficient to justify a characterization of that party as a 'state actor.' We turn now to the second test, which is whether 'the State has so far insinuated itself into a position of interdependence with [the private party] that it must be recognized as a joint participant in the challenged activity.'"

The operative principle: where a private actor operates in joint operative position with state actors, the private actor's conduct is fairly attributable to the State and is reached by the operative architecture that applies to state actors. The W-2 test is not the operative test; the joint-action test is.

The relevance for the Act: where a private contractor operates in joint operative position with state actors — coordinating with state officers, executing state-court orders, performing state-authorized operative functions — the contractor is operatively a state actor under the Act on the same operative reasoning *Lugar* applied.

### 1.2 *West v. Atkins*, 487 U.S. 42 (1988) — The Public-Function Test

*West v. Atkins*, 487 U.S. 42 (1988), is the operative foundation of the public-function test for state-actor status. The case involved a private physician who contracted with the state to provide medical care to inmates at a state correctional facility. An inmate sued the physician under §1983 for inadequate medical care.

The Supreme Court held that the private physician was operatively a state actor for §1983 purposes. Justice Blackmun's opinion stated the operative principle:

> "The State bore an affirmative obligation to provide adequate medical care to West; the State delegated that function to respondent Atkins; and respondent voluntarily assumed that obligation by contract. It is the physician's function within the state system, not the precise terms of his employment, that determines whether his actions can fairly be attributed to the State. Whether a physician is on the state payroll or is paid by contract, the dispositive issue concerns the relationship among the State, the physician, and the prisoner. ... In the State's employ or under contract to it, he is the state actor for purposes of providing medical care to inmates."

The operative principle: where a state has an operative obligation to perform a function (here, providing medical care to inmates the state has chosen to imprison), and where the state delegates that function to a private actor by contract, the private actor's performance of the function is fairly attributable to the State.

The relevance for the Act: where the State has an operative function it cannot operatively delegate away — providing medical care to those in state custody, providing supervision of those on state-imposed probation, providing housing for children removed by the state from their families — and where the State contracts the function out to private actors, the contractors are operatively state actors under the Act for the operative performance of the function.

### 1.3 *Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24 (1980) — Private Actors Conspiring With State Officials

*Dennis v. Sparks*, 449 U.S. 24 (1980), addressed the operative liability of private actors who conspire with state officials. The case involved a private party who allegedly bribed a state-court judge to issue an unfavorable injunction. The private party argued that the judge's absolute judicial immunity shielded the private party from §1983 liability.

The Supreme Court rejected the argument. Chief Justice White's opinion stated the operative principle:

> "Private persons, jointly engaged with state officials in the challenged action, are acting 'under color' of law for purposes of § 1983 actions. ... The judge's immunity, however, has historically been thought important enough to insulate even the corrupt judge from response in damages. Even if we should hold that the judge's accomplices are immune in some categorical sense from § 1983 liability, the practical effect would be to relieve those defendants of the consequences of their criminal acts."

The operative principle: private actors jointly engaged with state officials in the operative challenged conduct are state actors for §1983 purposes, and the state actor's immunity does not extend derivatively to the private actor. The private actor's liability is independent.

The relevance for the Act: where a private contractor operates in joint position with state officials to engage in conduct meeting the elements of an Act offense — supplying false evidence to a state prosecutor, executing an unlawful state-court order, coordinating with state officers in patterns of harassment against citizens — the contractor is operatively a state actor under the Act, and the state actor's operative position does not shield the contractor.

### 1.4 *Adickes v. S.H. Kress & Co.*, 398 U.S. 144 (1970) — Foundational Joint-Action

*Adickes v. S.H. Kress & Co.*, 398 U.S. 144 (1970), is the foundational case for the joint-action principle. The case involved a private restaurant that, with the alleged operative cooperation of local police, refused service to a white woman accompanied by a Black student and then summoned police who arrested the woman on vagrancy charges.

The Supreme Court held that the restaurant's conduct, if performed in operative cooperation with police, was state action for §1983 purposes. Justice Harlan's opinion stated the operative principle:

> "The involvement of a state official in such a conspiracy plainly provides the state action essential to show a direct violation of petitioner's Fourteenth Amendment equal protection rights, whether or not the actions of the police were officially authorized, or lawful. ... Section 1983 ... permits any 'person' to be held responsible for a deprivation of the rights of another."

The case established the operative principle that private-state coordination produces state-action status for the private actor. The principle has been applied across the operative arc of subsequent jurisprudence.

### 1.5 The Operative Synthesis

The combined operative effect of *Lugar*, *West*, *Dennis*, *Adickes*, and the broader state-action jurisprudence is direct. The Supreme Court has consistently held that:

- Private actors performing public functions delegated by the state are state actors for the operative performance of the function.
- Private actors operating in joint position with state officials are state actors for the operative conduct of the joint enterprise.
- The W-2 test is not the operative test; the operative function and the operative relationship to state authority are.
- State actors' immunity does not extend derivatively to private actors jointly engaged in the conduct.

The Act's reach to private contractors operates within this established operative architecture. The Act's "Government Actor" definition reaches actors exercising or purporting to exercise state authority, which is the operative position the state-action jurisprudence treats as the operative line.

## 2. The *Richardson v. McKnight* Confirmation

*Richardson v. McKnight*, 521 U.S. 399 (1997), addressed the operative question whether private prison guards are entitled to qualified immunity in §1983 actions. The Court held they are not. Justice Breyer's opinion stated the operative reasoning:

> "We have answered the immunity question narrowly, in the context in which it arose. ... Our examination of history and purpose nonetheless reveals nothing to warrant a special immunity for private guards. We do not deal with privatization in general but with the specific context of private prisons. ... In the context of privately operated prisons, market forces operate to keep the price of contract services down, even without the threat of legal action."

The operative principle: where the state delegates a function to a private actor and the private actor performs the function in a competitive market context, the operative reasons for state-actor immunity (history, public-policy concerns about timid performance of public duty, the need to attract qualified actors to public service) do not apply with the same operative force. The private actor faces market discipline; the private actor does not face the same operative concerns the public actor does; the private actor is not entitled to the public actor's operative immunity.

The relevance for the Act: where private contractors perform state functions and face civil or criminal liability under the Act, the contractors do not enjoy the operative immunity that has, in operative practice, attached to state actors. The contractor's operative position is structurally different from the state actor's; the operative immunity does not transfer; the contractor faces operative liability on the operative facts of the operative conduct.

### 2.1 The Asymmetric Result and Its Operative Significance

The operative significance of *Richardson* is the asymmetric result it produces. State actors performing a function may, under the immunity architecture as it has historically operated, face less operative liability than private contractors performing the same function. The asymmetry produces the operative anomaly: the more the state privatizes its operative functions, the more operative liability attaches to the conduct of the function. The state pays less for the operative performance of the function but, in aggregate, the operative liability of the actor performing the function is greater.

The Act's operative architecture eliminates this asymmetry. §5.1 abrogates the immunity doctrines for liability under the Act, applying the operative line to both state actors and private contractors performing state functions. The result is operative equality: state actors and private contractors face the same operative reach under the Act, calibrated to the operative conduct rather than to the institutional source of the actor's paycheck.

### 2.2 The *Filarsky v. Delia* Counter-Point and the Operative Resolution

*Filarsky v. Delia*, 566 U.S. 377 (2012), partially complicated the operative line by extending qualified immunity to certain private attorneys retained by the government to conduct internal investigations. The case is the operative counter-point to *Richardson*'s clean rule and produces some operative doctrinal incoherence in the qualified-immunity-and-contractors space.

The operative resolution for the Act: the immunity-doctrine incoherence is precisely the operative reason the Act's §5.1 abrogates the immunity architecture within the Act's operative scope. The Act does not depend on resolving the doctrinal tension between *Richardson* and *Filarsky*; the Act resolves the tension by abrogating both directions of the operative immunity for liability under the Act. The contractor faces operative liability on the operative facts; the state actor faces operative liability on the operative facts; the operative line is the substantive elements of the offenses the Act reaches.

The companion document [why_we_can_abrogate_supreme_court_cases.md](why_we_can_abrogate_supreme_court_cases.md) develops the broader two-grounds framework under which the Act addresses federal immunity doctrines. The contractor context is one of the operative applications of the framework.

## 3. The Structural Argument — Privatization Cannot Be the Operative Shield

The structural argument for the Act's contractor reach is direct. If private contractors performing state functions could commit conduct against citizens that state actors cannot, privatization would, in operative effect, become the operative shield for conduct the constitutional architecture forbids.

### 3.1 The Operative Asymmetry the Shield Would Produce

A shield model would produce the operative pattern in which:

- The State delegates an operative function to a private contractor.
- The contractor performs the function in operative coordination with state actors.
- The contractor commits conduct against citizens that, performed by state actors directly, would be reachable under the constitutional and statutory architecture.
- The operative shield of the contractor's private-actor status prevents the operative architecture from reaching the conduct.
- The State, having privatized the function, has operatively transferred the conduct to a position where the constitutional architecture does not reach.

The operative result is that privatization becomes the operative pathway by which the State escapes the constitutional architecture's reach. The operative incentive for the State is to privatize precisely those functions whose performance would expose state actors to liability. The privatization completes the operative architecture of impunity.

The operative pattern is not theoretical. The substantial growth of private prisons, private probation services, private debt-collection contractors enforcing court-ordered debt, private vendors of government technology that exercises decisional authority over citizens, and the broader privatization of operative state functions across the past several decades, has produced an operative reality in which a significant fraction of the operative conduct of state authority is performed by private contractors. Where the contractors are operatively shielded from the architecture of accountability, the operative architecture of impunity becomes structural.

### 3.2 The Operative Response

The operative response is to apply the architecture of accountability to the operative exercise of state authority regardless of the institutional source of the actor's paycheck. The Act's "Government Actor" definition does exactly this. The operative test is the operative exercise of state power, not the operative institutional position of the actor.

The result restores the operative architecture the constitutional design contemplates. The State's authority operates through its actors; where the actors are state employees, the operative architecture reaches them as state actors; where the actors are private contractors, the operative architecture reaches them as private contractors operating under color of state authority. The operative line is the operative exercise of state power. The line is the same line whichever side of it the actor stands on at the moment of conduct.

### 3.3 The Founders' Framework

The Founders' framework, developed in [why_does_the_government_even_exist.md](why_does_the_government_even_exist.md), did not contemplate the operative privatization architecture that has, in the past several decades, become a significant operative feature of state activity. The Founders' framework treats government as the citizen's agent, exercising operative authority delegated by the constitutional architecture, with the architecture of accountability operating against the operative exercise of the delegated authority.

The Founders' framework, applied to the operative privatization architecture, produces the operative conclusion: privatization does not move the operative exercise of state authority outside the architecture of accountability. The operative authority remains the State's; the State has merely delegated the operative performance of the authority to a contractor. The architecture of accountability operates against the operative exercise of the authority, by whichever actor is operatively performing it.

The Act's contractor reach applies the Founders' framework to the modern operative privatization architecture. The reach is not novel; it is the operative application of the constitutional architecture to operative conditions the Founders did not specifically anticipate but their framework structurally addresses.

## 4. The Specific Categories of Contractor Conduct the Act Reaches

The Act's operative reach extends across the operative range of contractor categories where state authority is exercised. The categories include but are not limited to:

### 4.1 Private Prison and Detention Staff

Private prisons and detention facilities operate under contract with state and federal authorities to hold persons in custody under state and federal authority. The operative performance of the custodial function — including the operative conduct of guards, administrators, medical personnel, and other operative staff — is performance of a state function under *West v. Atkins* and the broader state-action jurisprudence. The operative reach of the Act includes:

- Use of force against detainees outside the operative scope of legitimate custodial authority — reachable under §1.1.3 (Battery — Government Context).
- Sustained patterns of mistreatment producing fear, harm, or operative consequences calibrated to the operative facts — reachable under §1.1.12 (Terrorism), §1.1.13 (Official Intimidation), §1.1.47 (Carceralism), where the operative elements are met.
- Coordinated conduct among contractor staff and state actors producing operative harm to detainees — reachable under §1.1.6 (Cabalism), where the operative elements are met.
- Failure to provide medical care, food, or other operative custodial requirements — reachable under the operative architecture of the Act where the operative elements are met.

### 4.2 Private Probation Officers

Private probation officers operate under contract with state and county authorities to supervise persons on state-imposed probation. The operative performance of the supervisory function is performance of a state function. The operative reach of the Act includes:

- Imposition of fees, fines, or other operative conditions exceeding the operative scope of the court-imposed conditions — reachable under §1.1.10 (Anarchy) and §17.9E (Revenue Extraction Under Color of Law) where the operative elements are met.
- Threats, intimidation, or coercive conduct toward probationers — reachable under §1.1.13 (Official Intimidation) and §1.1.12 (Terrorism) where the operative elements are met.
- Coordination with prosecutors and judges to produce operative outcomes outside the operative scope of legitimate probation administration — reachable under §1.1.6 (Cabalism) and §1.1.14 (Malicious Prosecution) where the operative elements are met.

### 4.3 Private Debt-Collection Contractors Enforcing Court-Ordered Debt

Private debt-collection contractors operating under contract to enforce court-ordered debt — including child-support enforcement, court-imposed fines, court-imposed restitution, and other operative state debt — perform a state function. The operative reach of the Act includes:

- Threats of arrest, incarceration, or other operative consequences exceeding the operative scope of legitimate collection — reachable under §1.1.13 (Official Intimidation) where the operative elements are met.
- Coordination with state actors to produce operative outcomes outside the operative scope of legitimate collection — reachable under §1.1.6 (Cabalism) where the operative elements are met.
- Patterns of conduct producing financial collapse, housing crisis, or comparable operative consequences for the debtor — reachable under §1.1.47 (Carceralism, where the operative conditions include the carceral-threat element) and §5.7 (Business and Employee Reachability where the operative facts include economic injury producing operative harms).

### 4.4 Private Vendors of Government Technology Exercising Decisional Authority

Private vendors of operative government technology — algorithmic decision systems for benefits administration, child-welfare risk-assessment systems, predictive-policing systems, automated facial-recognition systems used by state law enforcement, automated traffic-enforcement systems, and the broader operative range — are increasingly operative actors in state decision-making against citizens. The operative reach of the Act includes:

- Conduct producing operative deprivation of rights through operative algorithmic decision-making — reachable under §1.1.3 (Battery — Government Context, with the broad reading that includes deprivation through indirect process) and §1.1.46 (Judicial Imposter, where the operative facts include executive or administrative application of doctrinal rules to citizens).
- Operative coordination between the vendor and state actors producing operative harm — reachable under §1.1.6 (Cabalism) where the operative elements are met.
- Operative failures to provide due process — reachable under the operative architecture of Section 2 (Due Process).

### 4.5 Foster Placements and Institutional Custodians

Foster-care providers, group homes, residential treatment facilities, and other institutional custodians of children removed by state action from their families perform a state function — the operative custody of children the State has chosen to remove. The operative reach of the Act includes:

- Abuse, neglect, or operative mistreatment of children in custody — reachable under §1.1.3 (Battery), §1.1.12 (Terrorism), §1.1.19 (Family Separation), and §17.9I (Poverty-Based Family Separation) where the operative elements are met.
- Coordination with state child-welfare actors producing operative harm — reachable under §1.1.6 (Cabalism) where the operative elements are met.
- Failure to facilitate operative reunification or operative parental contact — reachable under the operative architecture of family-rights provisions where the operative elements are met.

### 4.6 Private Security Firms on Government Contracts

Private security firms operating under government contracts to protect government facilities, government officials, or government-owned property are operative state actors for the operative performance of the security function. The operative reach of the Act includes:

- Use of force against citizens outside the operative scope of legitimate security authority — reachable under §1.1.3 (Battery) where the operative elements are met.
- Conduct producing operative fear or operative coercion of citizens — reachable under §1.1.12 (Terrorism) and §1.1.13 (Official Intimidation) where the operative elements are met.

### 4.7 Public-Private Partners Performing Coercive Functions

The broader operative category of public-private partners performing coercive functions — operative arrangements in which private entities cooperate with state authority to produce operative outcomes that exercise state power against citizens — is reached on the same operative principle. The operative test is the operative exercise of state power against the citizen, not the operative formal structure of the partnership.

### 4.8 Private Impersonators of Officials

A separate but related category: private actors who impersonate officials and use the apparent state authority to produce operative outcomes against citizens. The operative reach of the Act extends to these actors as developed in [why_is_not_a_bill_of_attainder.md](why_is_not_a_bill_of_attainder.md): the operative element is the apparent exercise of state power, regardless of whether the actor in fact holds state authority. A private impersonator pulling over a citizen, conducting a search, taking a child, imposing a citation, or asserting state-like authority to deprive a citizen of liberty or property is reached under the Act.

## 5. The Operative Reach to Federal Contractors Under §14.7

The Act's §14.7 (Federal Officer Status Not a Shield), developed in [why_federal_officer_status_is_not_a_shield.md](why_federal_officer_status_is_not_a_shield.md), reaches federal officers operating against Utah citizens within Utah's borders. The reach extends to federal contractors operating in the same operative position.

### 5.1 The Federal-Contractor Pattern

The federal-contractor pattern parallels the state-contractor pattern at the operative level. Federal contractors include:

- Private detention facilities operating under federal contract to hold immigration detainees, federal criminal detainees, or federal civil detainees.
- Private security contractors operating under federal contract to protect federal facilities or federal officials.
- Private investigators operating under federal contract to conduct operative federal investigations.
- Private vendors of operative federal technology — facial recognition systems used by federal agencies, surveillance technology, automated decision systems for federal benefits or federal enforcement, and the broader operative range.

The operative principle: where a federal contractor exercises operative federal authority within Utah's borders, against Utah citizens, in conduct that exceeds the operative scope of constitutional federal authority or constitutes an independent state criminal offense under the Act, the contractor is reached under §14.7 on the same operative principle that reaches federal officers.

### 5.2 The Operative Application

The operative application is direct. A federal-contractor employee at a private federal detention facility in Utah who commits operative battery against a detainee in conduct exceeding the operative scope of legitimate custodial authority is reached under §1.1.3 of the Act, as a Government Actor under §1.1.1 (operating under color of federal authority through the contractor relationship), with the operative shield-rejection of §14.7 preventing the federal-contractor status from operating as a categorical immunity.

The reach is consistent with the broader operative architecture of the Act's contractor provisions and with the operative architecture of §14.7. The operative test is the operative exercise of federal authority against the Utah citizen within Utah's borders, in conduct that meets the elements of an Act offense, regardless of the institutional source of the actor's paycheck.

## 6. The Operative Consequence

The contractor-reach principle has operative consequences across the architecture of the Act.

### 6.1 The Operative Architecture of Liability

Private contractors face the operative liability the Act establishes, calibrated to the operative facts of the operative conduct. Civil liability under §5.7 extends to contractors causing operative harm to businesses, employees, or natural-person beneficiaries. Criminal liability under the operative substantive offenses extends to contractors meeting the elements of the offenses. Forfeiture under Section 11 extends to contractor personnel convicted under the Act, with the operative consequences including loss of professional licensing where applicable, permanent ineligibility for future government contracts, and the broader operative architecture.

### 6.2 The Operative Architecture of Indemnification

The non-indemnification rule developed in [why_no_indemnification.md](why_no_indemnification.md) applies to contractors as it applies to state actors. The contractor faces personal operative consequence for liability under the Act; the contractor's employer (the contracting firm) cannot use government funds to indemnify the contractor for liability arising from the operative conduct meeting the elements of an Act offense.

### 6.3 The Operative Architecture of Enforcement

The Citizen Council mechanism of Section 9 reaches contractor conduct on the same operative terms it reaches state-actor conduct. A Council convened to address operative misconduct by a private prison contractor, a private probation officer, or any other operative category of contractor produces a Joint Declaration of Prosecution with the legal force of a grand-jury indictment, leading to operative prosecution through the State's criminal-process apparatus.

### 6.4 The Operative Effect on the Privatization Architecture

The cumulative operative effect on the privatization architecture is significant. Private contractors performing state functions face the same operative consequences for misconduct that state actors face. The operative incentive to privatize as a pathway around the architecture of accountability is eliminated. The operative reality of contracted-out state authority is brought within the operative architecture the constitutional design contemplates.

## 7. The Foreseeable Objection — And Its Operative Answer

The foreseeable objection is that the contractor reach extends the Act's operative scope beyond the operative range of legitimate state criminal-law authority — that private actors should not be subject to the operative architecture designed for state officials. The objection fails on three grounds.

### 7.1 The Operative-Function Argument

Private contractors performing state functions are, on the operative principles developed in *Lugar*, *West*, *Dennis*, *Adickes*, and the broader state-action jurisprudence, state actors for the operative performance of the function. The operative function is the operative line; the institutional source of the actor's paycheck is not. The Act applies the operative line consistently across both state employees and contractors performing state functions.

### 7.2 The Operative-Equality Argument

Equal application of the law is among the operative foundations of the Act. The same rules apply to every actor. A private contractor performing state functions and a state employee performing the same functions are exercising operative state authority against citizens; the operative architecture of accountability applies to both. Differential operative treatment based on the contractor's institutional position would produce the operative asymmetry developed in Section 3 above — privatization as the operative shield. The Act rejects the operative asymmetry.

### 7.3 The Operative-Necessity Argument

The empirical operative reality of the modern privatization architecture makes the contractor reach operatively necessary. A substantial fraction of operative state authority is performed by contractors. Limiting the operative architecture of accountability to state employees would, in operative effect, exempt the substantial fraction of state authority that is exercised through contractors. The operative architecture cannot be calibrated to a privatization-free state model that no longer exists. The Act addresses the operative reality.

## 8. Conclusion

Operation Checkmate reaches private contractors performing government functions because:

1. **The Supreme Court's state-action jurisprudence has consistently held that the constitutional and statutory architecture reaches conduct under color of state authority regardless of the actor's W-2.** *Lugar*, *West*, *Dennis*, *Adickes*, and the broader jurisprudence establish the operative line as the operative exercise of state power, not the institutional source of the actor's paycheck.

2. ***Richardson v. McKnight* confirms that private contractors performing state functions do not enjoy the operative immunity that has attached to state actors in modern doctrine.** The operative result is symmetrical contractor liability without the operative shield of qualified immunity. The Act's §5.1 abrogation extends the symmetry across the operative architecture of the Act.

3. **The structural argument against privatization as the operative shield is direct.** A shield model would produce the operative pattern in which the State escapes the architecture of accountability by privatizing operative functions whose performance would expose state actors to liability. The Act rejects this operative architecture.

4. **The Founders' framework supports the contractor reach.** Government's authority is delegated; the architecture of accountability operates against the operative exercise of delegated authority; the operative exercise reaches contractors performing state functions as it reaches state employees performing the same functions.

5. **The specific contractor categories the Act reaches** — private prisons, private probation, private debt collectors, private decisional-technology vendors, foster placements, private security on government contract, public-private partners, and private impersonators — cover the operative range of conduct that operative privatization has produced.

6. **The federal-contractor reach under §14.7** applies the same operative principle to federal contractors operating against Utah citizens within Utah's borders, on the operative foundation developed in [why_federal_officer_status_is_not_a_shield.md](why_federal_officer_status_is_not_a_shield.md).

7. **The operative consequences are calibrated across the Act's architecture.** Civil liability, criminal liability, forfeiture, non-indemnification, and the Citizen Council enforcement mechanism all reach contractors on the same operative terms they reach state actors.

8. **The foreseeable objections fail on operative-function, operative-equality, and operative-necessity grounds.** The contractor reach is the operative application of the architecture's operative principles to the operative reality of modern privatization.

If you are exercising the state's power over a citizen, you are bound by the state's rules. The operative test is the exercise of state power. The Act applies the operative test consistently. Private contractors performing state functions are within the operative reach of the Act, on the same operative terms as state employees performing the same functions.

The architecture of accountability reaches the operative exercise of state authority. Where state authority is exercised through contractors, the architecture reaches contractors. The operative line is the line the Constitution itself draws.
