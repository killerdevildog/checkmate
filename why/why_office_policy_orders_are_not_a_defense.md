## 1. The Nuremberg Principle

The Nuremberg Tribunal (1945–46), addressing operative state-actor conduct that produced the operative crimes of the Nazi regime, established the operative principle that operative superior orders do not operatively excuse operative wrongful conduct. ### 1.1 The Operative Foundation

The Charter of the International Military Tribunal (the London Charter, August 8, 1945), Article 8, provided:

> "The fact that the Defendant acted pursuant to order of his Government or of a superior shall not free him from responsibility, but may be considered in mitigation of punishment if the Tribunal determines that justice so requires."

The operative Tribunal rejected the operative defense uniformly. Justice Robert Jackson's operative opening statement framed the operative principle:

> "These men ... made a national policy of cruelty and injustice on a scale unprecedented in history. They threw aside all 'scruples about the propriety of using force,' as one of them put it, and adopted criminal methods because they were efficient. ... The operative principle: the operative invocation of operative office, operative policy, or operative orders does not operatively transfer the operative responsibility from the operative actor to the operative institution. The operative responsibility runs personally; the operative consequence runs personally.

### 1.2 The Nuremberg Principles as International Law

The Nuremberg Principles were operatively codified by the United Nations General Assembly in 1946 (Resolution 95(I)) and operatively formulated by the International Law Commission in 1950. Principle IV provides:

> "The fact that a person acted pursuant to order of his Government or of a superior does not relieve him from responsibility under international law, provided a moral choice was in fact possible to him."

The operative principle has been carried forward through the operative arc of subsequent international criminal-law architecture — the Genocide Convention (1948), the Geneva Conventions (1949) and their Additional Protocols (1977), the Statutes of the ICTY (1993) and ICTR (1994), and the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court (1998). Article 33 of the Rome Statute provides:

> "The fact that a crime within the jurisdiction of the Court has been committed by a person pursuant to an order of a Government or of a superior, whether military or civilian, shall not relieve that person of criminal responsibility unless: (a) The person was under a legal obligation to obey orders of the Government or the superior in question; (b) The person did not know that the order was unlawful; and (c) The order was not manifestly unlawful."

The operative principle is consistent across the operative international architecture. Operative superior orders may operate, in operative narrow circumstances, as an operative defense; operative orders that are operatively manifestly unlawful never operatively excuse the operative conduct; operative invocation of operative office does not operatively defeat operative personal responsibility.

### 1.3 The Operative Lesson

The operative lesson the Nuremberg Tribunal established is structurally foundational. Operative state apparatus has the operative capacity to produce operative wrongful conduct on a scale operative individual actors cannot.
