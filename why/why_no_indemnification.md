# Why the Act Forbids Indemnification of Government Actors

Section 5.2 of Operation Checkmate (Fixing the Personal Accountability Gap) — read alongside §5.1 (Fixing the Immunity Gap), §11.1 (Loss of State-Conferred Benefits), and the broader Section 11 forfeiture architecture — establishes the operative rule that **state funds may not be used to indemnify the individual government actor for liability under this Act**. The actor faces the consequences personally. Public bodies, including the State, its political subdivisions, agencies, departments, and authorities, are forbidden to absorb the actor's civil judgment, pay the actor's criminal fines, satisfy the actor's restitution obligations, or otherwise transfer the operative cost of the actor's misconduct from the actor to the taxpayer.

The rule is the linchpin of the entire deterrence theory of the Act. Without it, every other accountability provision — every substantive offense, every immunity abrogation, every civil cause of action, every criminal classification — becomes a transfer payment from taxpayers to victims, with no personal cost to the actor whose conduct produced the operative harm.

This document develops the empirical record of indemnification under current law, the structural argument for why indemnification operates as the equivalent of immunity from the actor's perspective, the doctrinal foundation for the Act's prohibition, the comparison to fiduciary-duty and professional-discipline doctrines in private law, and the operative consequence the rule produces.

---

## 1. The Empirical Record — Indemnification Has Hollowed Out Civil Liability

The companion document [why_criminal_not_just_civil.md](why_criminal_not_just_civil.md) develops the broader argument that civil liability alone has, over the past half-century, failed to produce operative changes in government conduct. Indemnification is the operative joint at which the civil-liability machinery breaks down most reliably.

### 1.1 The Schwartz Studies

Professor Joanna Schwartz, of UCLA School of Law, has conducted the most operatively detailed empirical research on the indemnification of police officers in civil-rights cases. Her findings, published in the New York University Law Review, the Yale Law Journal, the Stanford Law Review, the Columbia Law Review, and elsewhere, document an operative pattern that is consistent across the largest jurisdictions in the United States:

- In the largest jurisdictions Schwartz examined, officers personally contribute approximately **0.02 percent** of the operative dollar amount of civil-rights judgments and settlements.
- In many jurisdictions, the operative figure is precisely zero — no officer paid any portion of any judgment or settlement across the multi-year study period.
- Even in jurisdictions where officers are nominally responsible for some portion of the cost (typically a small deductible or a percentage above a certain threshold), the operative pattern is that the agency negotiates the settlement to keep the officer's exposure at zero or near-zero.
- In approximately 99.98 percent of the operative cost, the government employer absorbs the judgment or settlement, transfers the cost to taxpayers, and continues to employ the officer.

The empirical pattern produces an operative reality in which the officer's incentive structure is, with respect to civil liability, effectively unchanged by misconduct. The officer faces no personal cost; the institutional employer absorbs the cost; the taxpayer ultimately bears the cost; the operative deterrent effect on the officer is negligible.

### 1.2 The Pattern Beyond Police

The pattern is not limited to police. Similar empirical research on prosecutors (though more sparse, because absolute prosecutorial immunity removes most cases from civil reach), on regulators, on agency employees, and on judges (though absolute judicial immunity removes most cases from civil reach) shows the same operative pattern wherever civil liability does attach: the institutional employer absorbs the cost; the actor faces no personal financial consequence.

The operative norm across the modern American civil-liability landscape is that personal financial accountability for civil-rights misconduct, on the actor's part, is effectively zero.

### 1.3 The Operative Effect on Institutional Behavior

The combined empirical record of fifty-three years of § 1983 enforcement under modern immunity and indemnification practice produces an operative pattern in which institutional behavior toward misconduct settles into a recognizable equilibrium:

- Misconduct produces civil judgments at a predictable operative rate.
- The judgments are paid from a predictable operative budget (general fund, risk-management reserve, dedicated insurance).
- The cost is absorbed as a standard operating expense of the institution.
- Officers, supervisors, and managers face no operative personal consequence.
- The institutional response is to budget for the cost, not to change the conduct.

This is the operative state of the civil-liability machinery. It is the operative reason the Act's strategic choice is criminal liability with mandatory non-indemnification — not because criminal liability is preferred for its own sake, but because criminal liability with non-indemnification produces the operative deterrent that the civil-liability-with-indemnification system has demonstrably failed to produce.

## 2. Indemnification Operates as the Equivalent of Immunity From the Actor's Perspective

The doctrinal structure of indemnification is different from the doctrinal structure of immunity. The operative effect on the actor's incentives is identical.

### 2.1 The Doctrinal Difference

Immunity operates at the threshold of liability: it provides the actor a defense against the imposition of liability in the first place. Indemnification operates after liability has been established: the actor is nominally liable, but the cost of the liability is transferred to a third party (here, the government employer and ultimately the taxpayer).

The two mechanisms occupy different operative slots in the legal architecture. Immunity is a defense; indemnification is a cost-transfer arrangement that operates downstream of the defense or its absence.

### 2.2 The Operative Equivalence

From the actor's perspective, however, the two mechanisms produce the same operative outcome. The actor faces no personal cost for the misconduct. Whether the result is reached by (1) immunity preventing the imposition of liability or (2) indemnification transferring the imposed liability to a third party, the actor's operative experience is the same: misconduct produces no personal financial consequence.

The operative incentive of the actor is therefore identical under both mechanisms. The deterrent effect that the imposition of liability is designed to produce — the actor's anticipated personal financial loss should the actor engage in the misconduct — does not materialize. The actor's calculation runs: "If I engage in this conduct, what is my expected personal cost?" Under immunity, the answer is zero because liability does not attach. Under indemnification, the answer is zero because liability attaches but is paid by someone else. The expected personal cost is zero in either case.

### 2.3 Why the Equivalence Matters

The operative equivalence matters because it disposes of any objection to the Act's non-indemnification rule that would treat indemnification as somehow less significant than immunity. The two mechanisms produce the same operative deterrent failure. Abrogating immunity under §5.1 without prohibiting indemnification under §5.2 would leave the operative deterrent failure in place — and the entire architecture of substantive offenses, criminal liability, and forfeiture under Section 11 would, in the operative experience of the actor, be channeled into "the city pays" rather than "the actor pays."

The Act's structural choice is therefore to address both mechanisms together. §5.1 abrogates the immunity doctrines for the conduct the Act reaches. §5.2 forbids the operative substitute — indemnification — that would otherwise reproduce the same deterrent failure through a different doctrinal route.

## 3. The Doctrinal Foundation for the Prohibition

The Act's non-indemnification rule is grounded in the operative principle developed in [why_does_the_government_even_exist.md](why_does_the_government_even_exist.md): public power is a personal trust, held by the actor under oath, with the operative consequence that breach of the trust produces personal consequence to the actor who held it.

### 3.1 The Oath as the Operative Anchor

As developed in [why_a_violation_is_prima_facie_evidence_the_oath_was_abandoned.md](why_a_violation_is_prima_facie_evidence_the_oath_was_abandoned.md), every public officer is required by Article VI Clause 3 of the U.S. Constitution and by Utah Constitution Article IV Section 10 to take an oath of office. The oath is a personal commitment by the actor to the constitutional duties of office. The trust that flows from the oath is personal; the consequences for breach of the trust must, in operative consistency with the personal nature of the oath, also be personal.

Indemnification breaks this operative consistency. If the actor's personal commitment to the constitutional order under oath is met, on breach, with the institutional absorption of the operative consequence, the personal nature of the oath is converted, in operative reality, into an institutional formality. The oath becomes a piece of paper that the actor signed and the institution underwrites. The operative weight of the personal commitment is lost.

The non-indemnification rule restores the operative weight of the oath. The personal commitment is met, on breach, with personal consequence. The operative architecture of the oath operates as the constitutional design contemplates.

### 3.2 The Parallel to Fiduciary Duty in Private Law

The private-law parallel is direct and operatively informative. A corporate officer who commits fraud against the corporation faces personal criminal liability under state and federal law. The corporation cannot indemnify the officer for the criminal fine, the operative restitution, or the criminal sentence. The Delaware General Corporation Law § 145, as the leading example, expressly permits corporations to indemnify officers for civil liability arising from good-faith performance of duties but **expressly prohibits indemnification for liability arising from breach of the officer's duty of loyalty, knowing violations of law, or transactions producing improper personal benefit**.

The operative principle: where the officer's conduct constitutes a breach of the operative trust the office presupposes, the institution may not absorb the cost. The cost runs personally to the officer.

The same operative principle, applied to the public-office context, produces the Act's non-indemnification rule. The government actor holds public power under a trust analogous to (and in operative significance greater than) the corporate officer's fiduciary duty. Breach of the trust must, in operative consistency with the trust's personal nature, produce personal consequence.

### 3.3 The Parallel to Professional Discipline

The professional-discipline parallel is similarly direct. A lawyer disbarred for misconduct is not "indemnified" by the firm. The disbarment runs personally; the firm cannot un-disbar the lawyer; the firm cannot continue to employ the lawyer as a lawyer. A physician whose medical license is revoked for misconduct is not "indemnified" by the hospital. The license revocation runs personally; the hospital cannot un-revoke the license. A certified public accountant whose certification is revoked for misconduct is not "indemnified" by the firm.

The operative principle: certain consequences of misconduct run personally by their operative nature. They cannot be transferred to an institution. The Act's non-indemnification rule applies the same operative principle to the financial consequences of government-actor misconduct: the operative consequence runs personally, and the institutional employer is forbidden to absorb it.

The Section 11 forfeiture architecture extends the parallel further. §11.4 provides for automatic decertification, disbarment, and removal from professional standing on conviction under the Act. The professional-discipline consequences run personally on the same operative architecture the private-law analogs use. §11.1 provides for loss of state-conferred benefits (pension, certifications, contracts, licenses) — public benefits that turned on the trust are forfeit on breach. §11.2 provides for permanent ineligibility for future public office. The full forfeiture architecture is the operative form of the personal-consequence principle the non-indemnification rule anchors.

## 4. The Structural Reasons Indemnification Is Forbidden

Beyond the doctrinal foundation, several operative structural reasons support the non-indemnification rule.

### 4.1 The Deterrent Loop

The criminal-law architecture operates by way of an operative deterrent loop: the actor anticipates the personal cost of misconduct, weighs that cost against the operative benefit of the conduct, and (where the architecture is operatively calibrated) refrains from the conduct. The loop depends on the actor's anticipation of personal cost. Where the cost is institutionally absorbed, the loop is broken at the actor's calculation: the actor's anticipated personal cost is zero, the operative weight on the actor's calculation is zero, and the deterrent fails.

The non-indemnification rule restores the loop. The actor's anticipated personal cost is the operative cost of the criminal or civil judgment, plus the operative cost of the forfeitures under Section 11, plus the operative cost of the durable criminal record. The operative weight on the actor's calculation is calibrated to the operative severity of the conduct. The deterrent operates as the architecture is designed to produce.

### 4.2 The Taxpayer Externality

Indemnification operates as a transfer payment from the operative victim (and from taxpayers generally) to the actor. The actor commits the misconduct; the victim suffers the operative harm; the city pays the judgment to the victim (which is operatively appropriate compensation); the city recovers the cost through taxation; the actor faces no personal consequence; the taxpayers bear the cost of the actor's misconduct.

The operative externality is structurally regressive. The taxpayers who bear the cost include the same population — and often disproportionately the same population — that the misconduct most often targets. A pattern of officer misconduct against citizens in low-income neighborhoods, paid for through municipal general-fund taxation, transfers the cost of the misconduct to the population the misconduct affected. The operative architecture compounds the harm rather than internalizing it to the actor whose conduct produced it.

The non-indemnification rule eliminates the externality. The cost falls on the actor whose conduct produced the harm. The compensation runs from the actor to the victim. The taxpayer is not the operative cost-bearer for misconduct the taxpayer did not authorize.

### 4.3 The Institutional Incentive

Indemnification produces an operative institutional incentive structure that is the opposite of what accountable institutions require. Where the institution absorbs the operative cost of its actors' misconduct, the institution's operative incentive is to budget for the cost rather than to address the misconduct. The misconduct is, in the institutional operating model, an externalized line item — a cost of doing business — rather than a problem to be solved.

The non-indemnification rule changes the operative institutional incentive. Where the actor bears the operative cost personally, the institution's operative incentive shifts. The institution's exposure is reputational, structural (forfeiture of grants under §11, entity-level liability where the institution itself knowingly tolerated the misconduct), and operative (loss of the actor's services on conviction), not financial in the operative sense the indemnified system creates. The institution's operative response to the architecture is to align its conduct with the operative requirements of the law — because the alternative is to lose its actors to convictions and forfeitures rather than to continue them in office while paying judgments.

### 4.4 The Severability From Compensation

A foreseeable objection is that non-indemnification leaves victims under-compensated, because the individual actor may lack the operative resources to satisfy a substantial judgment that the institutional employer could have absorbed. The objection misreads the Act's operative architecture in two respects.

First, the Act's substantive offenses reach the institutional employer as well, where the institution knowingly tolerated, ratified, or encouraged the misconduct. §5.1.2 and §5.7's broader architecture allow recovery against the entity-level government actor in operative cases where the institutional conduct meets the elements. The non-indemnification rule applies to the **individual actor's** liability; it does not preclude the institution's own operative liability where the institution itself is a culpable actor.

Second, the Act's broader Section 11 forfeiture architecture, the Section 9 Citizen Council mechanism, and the criminal-law track produce operative remedies beyond the civil-damages calculation. A government actor convicted under the Act loses office, loses certifications, loses professional standing, loses pension, and faces criminal incarceration where applicable. The operative consequence to the actor is calibrated to the operative severity of the conduct regardless of whether the actor's personal assets are sufficient to satisfy a civil judgment. The compensation track and the consequence track operate in parallel; both produce operative remedies; neither depends on indemnification for its operative effect.

## 5. The Specific Operative Reach of the Non-Indemnification Rule

The Act's non-indemnification rule has defined operative reach. The rule does not abolish all indemnification of government actors for all conduct; it forbids indemnification for **liability under this Act**. The defined scope produces operative clarity for both the actor and the institutional employer.

### 5.1 What the Rule Forbids

The rule forbids the State of Utah, any political subdivision, any agency, department, board, authority, or other governmental entity from using public funds, public insurance, public risk-management reserves, public general fund, or any other public source of value, to:

- Pay any portion of a civil judgment entered against the government actor under the Act
- Pay any portion of a settlement of a civil claim against the government actor under the Act
- Pay any portion of a criminal fine imposed on the government actor for an offense under the Act
- Pay any portion of restitution ordered against the government actor under the Act
- Pay any portion of the operative costs of the actor's defense in any criminal proceeding under the Act
- Reimburse the actor for any personal payment the actor has made toward any of the foregoing
- Provide any other operative benefit that has the effect of transferring the cost of the actor's liability away from the actor and onto a public source of value

The rule reaches indirect indemnification as well as direct indemnification. An arrangement that operates, in operative effect, to transfer the actor's cost to a public source — a higher salary, a special bonus, a reclassification, a transferred-position arrangement — is reached if the operative purpose or effect is to indemnify the actor for liability under the Act.

### 5.2 What the Rule Does Not Forbid

The rule does not forbid indemnification of government actors for liability outside the Act's substantive offenses. A government actor who is sued for negligent operation of a state vehicle that produced a property-damage accident — conduct outside the operative scope of the Act's substantive offenses — remains eligible for indemnification under ordinary state law. The rule's operative reach is the Act's operative scope, not the entire universe of public-actor conduct.

The rule also does not forbid the operative provision of legal counsel to a government actor accused under the Act through public mechanisms, where the operative provision is part of the standard architecture of representation for any criminal defendant. A defendant in any criminal proceeding has the constitutional right to counsel; the Act does not abrogate that right with respect to defendants who are government actors. What the Act forbids is the operative payment of the defendant's privately retained counsel, the operative payment of the defendant's fines and judgments, and the operative absorption of the cost of the defendant's misconduct by public funds.

### 5.3 The Operative Effect on Existing Indemnification Agreements

Existing indemnification agreements between the State, its political subdivisions, and individual government actors are, with respect to liability under the Act, void as a matter of public policy. The non-indemnification rule operates as the operative ceiling on what public bodies may agree to provide. An employment contract, collective-bargaining agreement, or other instrument that purports to indemnify a government actor for liability under the Act is, as to that liability, inoperative; the instrument continues in force as to other liability and other operative provisions, but the indemnification provision is, with respect to Act liability, of no operative effect.

## 6. The Companion Provisions

The non-indemnification rule operates within a network of related operative provisions. The full architecture produces the operative consequence the rule alone cannot.

### 6.1 §5.1 — Immunity Abrogation

§5.1 abrogates the immunity doctrines for civil and criminal liability within the scope of the Act. As developed in [why_we_can_abrogate_supreme_court_cases.md](why_we_can_abrogate_supreme_court_cases.md), the operative effect is that the operative threshold for liability is the substantive elements of the Act's offenses, not the operative doctrines that have historically prevented liability from attaching. §5.1 establishes that liability attaches; §5.2 establishes that the actor bears the cost.

### 6.2 §11 — Forfeiture

Section 11 establishes the operative forfeiture architecture. §11.1 provides for loss of state-conferred benefits (pension, certifications, contracts, licenses). §11.2 provides for permanent ineligibility for future public office. §11.4 provides for automatic decertification, disbarment, and removal from professional standing. The forfeiture architecture is the operative form of the personal-consequence principle the non-indemnification rule anchors. The actor faces not only the operative cost of liability but the operative consequences for office, certification, profession, and pension.

### 6.3 §5.10 — Oath-of-Office Violation

§5.10 establishes that any violation of the Act by a government actor who has taken an oath of office constitutes prima facie evidence that the oath has been violated, with the operative consequences attaching cumulatively. As developed in [why_a_violation_is_prima_facie_evidence_the_oath_was_abandoned.md](why_a_violation_is_prima_facie_evidence_the_oath_was_abandoned.md), the oath-violation rule operates alongside the substantive offenses to produce the operative consequence that the actor's breach of the constitutional duties of office attaches to the actor personally.

### 6.4 §9 — Citizen Council

The Section 9 Citizen Council mechanism, as developed in [why_this_is_not_a_letter_of_mark.md](why_this_is_not_a_letter_of_mark.md), provides the operative pathway for prosecution where the County Prosecutor declines to charge. Combined with the criminal-liability track and the non-indemnification rule, the Citizen Council mechanism produces an operative architecture in which:

- The substantive elements of an offense, met by a developed record, produce indictment-equivalent process under §9.
- The case proceeds through ordinary state-court criminal prosecution.
- Civil-immunity defenses do not apply (per *Lanier* and the broader case law).
- Indemnification does not apply (per §5.2).
- The actor faces personal criminal consequence, personal civil consequence, and personal forfeiture under Section 11.

The cumulative operative architecture is calibrated to produce the operative deterrent that civil-liability-with-indemnification systems have demonstrably failed to produce.

## 7. The Strategic Centrality of the Rule

The non-indemnification rule is the linchpin of the deterrence theory of the Act. Without it, the rest of the operative architecture functions as a transfer mechanism rather than as a deterrent mechanism. With it, the architecture functions as the constitutional design contemplates.

The strategic centrality can be stated directly: every substantive offense in the Act, every immunity abrogation, every civil cause of action, every criminal classification, every forfeiture provision, and every operative consequence the Act creates ultimately turns on the operative question of who bears the operative cost. If the answer is the institutional employer, the operative effect on the actor's incentive is negligible regardless of how comprehensive the substantive offenses are. If the answer is the actor personally, the operative effect on the actor's incentive is direct, durable, and calibrated to the operative severity of the conduct.

The Act's choice is to assign the operative cost to the actor. The rule is therefore not a technical detail. It is the structural choice that determines whether the entire operative architecture functions or fails.

## 8. Conclusion

The Act forbids indemnification of government actors for liability under its substantive offenses because:

1. **The empirical record demonstrates that civil-liability-with-indemnification produces no operative deterrent.** Officers in the largest jurisdictions personally bear approximately 0.02 percent of the operative cost of civil-rights judgments. The pattern is consistent across categories of government actors and across jurisdictions. The operative deterrent loop is broken at the indemnification joint.

2. **Indemnification operates as the equivalent of immunity from the actor's perspective.** The doctrinal mechanisms differ; the operative effect on the actor's incentive is identical. Abrogating immunity without prohibiting indemnification leaves the operative deterrent failure in place.

3. **The doctrinal foundation is the personal nature of the oath of office.** Public power is held under a personal trust; breach of the trust must produce personal consequence; indemnification converts the personal nature of the trust into an institutional formality.

4. **The private-law parallels are direct.** Corporate officers cannot be indemnified for breach of duty of loyalty, knowing violations of law, or transactions producing improper personal benefit. Professional disciplinary consequences (disbarment, license revocation) run personally and cannot be transferred to the institution. The Act applies the same operative principle to public-office misconduct.

5. **The operative deterrent loop the criminal-law architecture depends on requires personal cost.** Where the actor's anticipated personal cost is zero — whether through immunity or through indemnification — the operative weight on the actor's calculation is zero and the deterrent fails. The non-indemnification rule restores the loop.

6. **The taxpayer externality is structurally regressive.** Indemnification transfers the cost of misconduct from the actor whose conduct produced it to the population the misconduct most often targets. Non-indemnification eliminates the externality.

7. **The institutional incentive structure depends on the rule.** Where the institution absorbs the cost, the operative response is to budget for the cost. Where the actor bears the cost personally, the operative response is to align institutional conduct with the operative requirements of the law.

8. **The operative scope of the rule is defined.** It forbids indemnification for liability under the Act; it does not abolish all indemnification of government actors for all conduct; it operates within the Act's operative scope and as the operative ceiling on what public bodies may agree to provide.

9. **The companion provisions complete the architecture.** §5.1 establishes that liability attaches; §5.2 establishes that the actor bears the cost; Section 11 establishes the operative forfeitures; §5.10 establishes the oath-violation consequence; §9 establishes the operative enforcement pathway. The full architecture produces the operative consequence the rule alone cannot.

10. **The strategic centrality of the rule is direct.** The Act's entire operative architecture turns on the operative question of who bears the operative cost. Assigning the cost to the actor is the structural choice that determines whether the architecture functions or fails.

Public power is a personal trust. The operative consequences for breach of the trust must run personally. The Act establishes that they will.
