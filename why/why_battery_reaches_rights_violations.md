# Why Battery Reaches Rights Violations

§1.1.3 of Operation Checkmate (Battery — Government Context) redefines the operative scope of battery to reach the unlawful violation of a citizen's constitutional or statutory rights through unauthorized use of state power — including through indirect process (orders, referrals, sign-offs, transmissions, paperwork chains). The provision substantially expands the common-law operative scope of battery, which the Anglo-American tradition has historically applied to the unlawful physical touching of another.

The expansion is doctrinally novel in its operative architecture but operatively continuous with the underlying principle the common-law tradition articulated. Blackstone's *Commentaries on the Laws of England* treated battery as the operative invasion of the citizen's security; Locke's *Second Treatise* identified the citizen's "lives, liberties and estates" as the operative properties the citizen joins political society to preserve; the operative architecture of rights-as-property runs through the founding tradition the Constitution embeds. §1.1.3's expansion restores the operative meaning of battery as the unlawful invasion of what the citizen has the right to be secure in.

This document develops the common-law foundation, the operative argument from the founding tradition, the structural argument for treating rights-violation as battery rather than as a separate operative category, the operative architecture of §1.1.3's subsections (the no-process-laundering rule, the elemental-predicate rule, the Citizen-Defender Presumption), the specific operative patterns the section reaches, and the operative consequence of the redefinition.

## 1. The Common-Law Foundation

Common-law battery has its operative foundation in the Anglo-American tradition's treatment of the citizen's operative security. Blackstone's *Commentaries on the Laws of England* (1765–1769), the operative legal text the Founders read and built American constitutional law on, treats battery as one operative category within the broader operative architecture of the citizen's absolute rights.

### 1.1 Blackstone's Operative Framework

Blackstone's Book III, Chapter 8 ("Of Wrongs, and their Remedies, Respecting the Rights of Persons") opens with the operative principle that the citizen's primary absolute rights are operatively secured by the operative architecture of remedies against unlawful invasions. The operative scope of the rights — developed in Book I, Chapter 1 ("Of the Absolute Rights of Individuals") — includes the right of personal security, the right of personal liberty, and the right of private property. The operative remedies — developed in Books III and IV — include the operative actions for trespass, including battery.

Blackstone's operative definition of battery treats the operative offense as the operative invasion of the citizen's security:

> "Battery is the unlawful beating of another. The least touching of another's person willfully, or in anger, is a battery; for the law cannot draw the line between different degrees of violence, and therefore totally prohibits the first and lowest stage of it: every man's person being sacred, and no other having a right to meddle with it, in any the slightest manner."

The operative principle: the citizen's person is operatively sacred; the operative architecture of remedies extends to operative invasions of the sacred operative scope; the operative reach is to "the slightest manner" of operative invasion. The operative framework is calibrated to the operative scope of the citizen's security, not to the operative physical magnitude of the invasion.

### 1.2 The Operative Scope Beyond Physical Touching

The operative reach of battery in the common-law tradition has, across the operative arc of subsequent jurisprudence, extended beyond the operative scope of physical touching. The operative offense reaches:

- The operative apprehension of imminent harmful contact (the assault-and-battery operative pairing)
- The operative use of an operative instrument producing the operative invasion (a weapon, a projectile, a device)
- The operative use of an operative agent producing the operative invasion (a confederate, an attack dog, a directed force)
- The operative direction of an operative process producing the operative invasion (a fraudulent operative arrangement, an operatively coerced action by an intermediary, an operative chain of operative consequences)

The operative principle is consistent: the operative offense reaches the operative cause of the operative invasion, regardless of the operative mechanism by which the operative cause produces the operative invasion. The operative law does not contain an operative escape valve for invasions produced through intermediaries, instruments, or processes; it reaches the operative actor whose operative cause produced the operative invasion.

### 1.3 The Operative Connection to Constitutional Architecture

The operative architecture of common-law battery connects directly to the operative architecture of the Bill of Rights' protections of the citizen's security. The Fourth Amendment protects the citizen's "right ... to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures." The operative principle is the same: the citizen has an operative right of security, and operative invasions of the security are operatively reached by the operative architecture of remedies.

The operative connection is not coincidental. The Founders, drafting the Fourth Amendment, were operating within the operative framework Blackstone had developed. The operative architecture of "persons, houses, papers, and effects" extends the common-law operative scope of the citizen's security to the operative range of operative invasions government authority might produce. The operative protection is calibrated to the operative scope of the operative invasions the architecture was designed to reach.

§1.1.3 of the Act operates within this operative connection. The operative reach to rights-violation as battery extends the operative scope of the offense to the operative invasions that the Bill of Rights' operative architecture protects against. The expansion is operatively continuous with the common-law principle, not a departure from it.

## 2. Locke and the Founding Tradition — Rights as the Citizen's Property

The operative argument for treating rights-violation as battery rests on the operative architecture of rights-as-property the Founders inherited from Locke and embedded in the constitutional design.

### 2.1 Locke's Operative Framework

Locke's *Second Treatise of Government* § 123 frames the operative purpose of political society:

The operative principle: Locke treats "lives, liberties and estates" as collectively the operative property the citizen joins political society to preserve. The operative term "property" reaches the operative range of what the citizen has the operative right to be secure in — life, liberty, and the operative material possessions that compose the citizen's operative position in the world.

The Founders' operative adoption of Locke's framework is direct. The operative purpose of government is the operative preservation of the citizen's rights, with the operative architecture of remedies extending to operative invasions of the rights.

The Fifth Amendment's operative formulation — "no person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law" — is operatively continuous with the same framework. The operative scope reaches the citizen's life, the citizen's liberty, and the citizen's property — the operative range of what the citizen has the operative right to be secure in. The operative protection extends to all operative invasions of the operative scope.

The operative architecture is consistent across the federal and state operative reach: the citizen's life, liberty, and property are operatively secure against operative invasions without operative due process; the operative invasions are operatively reached by the architecture of remedies.

### 2.3 The Operative Argument for Battery's Broad Reach

The operative argument follows directly. Battery is, in operative architecture, the operative offense reaching operative invasions of the citizen's most fundamental operative property — the operative security of the citizen's person and the operative possessions extending from it. The operative scope of the citizen's property, in the framework Locke developed and the Founders adopted, includes the citizen's rights. A government actor's operative invasion of the citizen's rights is therefore, in the operative framework, an operative battery — an operative invasion of the operative property the architecture of government was designed to preserve.

§1.1.3 of the Act applies this operative argument. The operative scope of battery extends to the operative invasions of the rights — through unlawful search, unlawful seizure, unlawful detention, unlawful family separation, unlawful retaliation, and the operative range of operative invasions a government actor might produce against the operative scope of the citizen's rights. ## 3. The Structural Argument — Why Rights-Violation Should Be Battery, Not a Lesser Operative Category

The structural argument for treating rights-violation as battery rather than as a separate operative category rests on the operative-severity test and the operative-equality principle developed across the architecture of the Act.

### 3.1 The Operative-Severity Test

A government actor's operative invasion of a citizen's rights — unlawfully separating a citizen from his children, unlawfully detaining a citizen without basis, unlawfully searching a citizen's person, papers, and effects, unlawfully retaliating against a citizen for protected operative conduct — produces operative consequences calibrated to the operative scope of the invasion. A citizen unlawfully detained without basis experiences operative consequences in operative liberty, dignity, and the operative architecture of the citizen's life that exceed many operative physical batteries the existing operative law treats as criminal. A citizen unlawfully retaliated against for protected operative conduct experiences operative consequences in operative livelihood, family, and operative position in the community that exceed many operative physical batteries.

The operative-severity test produces the operative conclusion: treating only the operative physical contact as battery, while leaving the operative rights-violation as a mere tort, produces the operative inversion the Act addresses. The operative invasion of operatively greater operative severity faces operatively lesser operative consequence; the operative invasion of operatively lesser operative severity faces operatively greater operative consequence. The operative architecture is inverted.

§1.1.3's expansion restores the operative architecture to operative consistency. The operative scope of battery reaches the operative invasions of the operative-severity range the operative concept of battery has historically addressed. The operative consequences are calibrated to the operative severity.

### 3.2 The Operative-Equality Principle

The operative-equality principle — that the same rules apply to every actor — extends to the operative scope of the offenses the rules reach. A private actor who unlawfully invades a citizen's operative person — through physical contact, through operative confinement, through operative direction of an operative process producing the operative invasion — faces operative consequences under the existing operative criminal law calibrated to the operative invasion. A government actor whose operative invasion produces the same operative consequences should, on the operative-equality principle, face operative consequences calibrated to the same operative invasion.

The existing operative law has, in operative practice, applied operatively different consequences to operatively analogous invasions based on the institutional position of the operative actor. A private actor who unlawfully separates a citizen from his children faces operative kidnapping charges; a government actor performing the operatively equivalent invasion faces, in operative practice, no comparable operative consequence. The Act's expansion of battery to reach the operative range of operative invasions, performed by either operative category of actor on the same operative terms, restores the operative-equality principle.

### 3.3 The Operative-Reality Test

The Act's drafting principle, developed in [why_the_definitions.md](why_the_definitions.md), requires that operative definitions test the operative reality of the operative invasion, not the operative formal labels. The operative-reality test applied to battery produces the operative conclusion: a citizen who has been unlawfully invaded in the operative scope of his security — whether through physical contact, through unlawful confinement, through unlawful invasion of his rights — has been operatively battered, in the operative meaning the common-law tradition developed and the constitutional architecture preserves.

§1.1.3 operates not as a single broad provision but as a network of operatively interlocking subsections, each calibrated to a specific operative element of the operative architecture. §1.1.3.2 addresses the apparent operative tension between the broad operative reach of §1.1.3 and the operative scope of legitimate government conduct. - **Liability for downstream harm runs to the actor who actually caused the harm** — where the operative chain produces operative harm to the citizen, the operative liability runs to the operative actor whose operative conduct produced the operative harm, not to the operative actors who, in operative compliance with the elemental-predicate rule, declined to participate in conduct without an operative predicate.
