# Why Operation Checkmate Is Not a Letter of Marque and Reprisal

A second foreseeable objection to Operation Checkmate is that, by authorizing twelve citizens — outside the executive branch, outside the prosecutorial office, outside the standing legal apparatus — to issue a Joint Declaration of Prosecution that compels the County Prosecutor to charge the case, the Act amounts to a state-issued **Letter of Marque and Reprisal**, prohibited to the states under Article I, §10, Clause 1 of the United States Constitution.

It does not. The objection misstates what a letter of marque is, misreads what the Citizen Council mechanism does, and ignores that every operative aspect of the Citizen Council process is parallel to existing American criminal procedure — grand-jury indictment, citizen-initiated criminal complaint, qui tam relator standing — none of which has ever been thought to be a letter of marque.

This document sets out the constitutional doctrine, identifies the historical and legal meaning of a letter of marque, and shows by direct comparison to existing American criminal-procedure mechanisms that Operation Checkmate's Citizen Council is no more a letter of marque than a grand-jury indictment is a privateering commission.

---

## 1. What a Letter of Marque and Reprisal Actually Is

A Letter of Marque and Reprisal is **a sovereign commission authorizing a private person to commit acts of war against the property and persons of a foreign enemy, which would otherwise be acts of piracy or unlawful violence, in pursuit of the issuing sovereign's military or diplomatic objectives**.

The constitutional text is Article I, §8, Clause 11 of the United States Constitution:

> "The Congress shall have Power ... To declare War, grant Letters of Marque and Reprisal, and make Rules concerning Captures on Land and Water."

The companion provision restricting the states is Article I, §10, Clause 1:

> "No State shall enter into any Treaty, Alliance, or Confederation; **grant Letters of Marque and Reprisal**; coin Money ..."

A letter of marque, in the historical and legal sense, has these defining structural features:

1. **It is an instrument of war.** The letter exists to commission private hostility against an identified foreign enemy of the issuing sovereign. It is part of the war power, alongside the powers to declare war, raise armies, and provide and maintain a navy.

2. **It authorizes acts that would otherwise be criminal.** Without the commission, a private actor attacking a foreign vessel and seizing its cargo would be a **pirate** — *hostis humani generis*, an enemy of all mankind, subject to capital punishment in every civilized jurisdiction. The letter functions to **lift the criminal character** of conduct that the underlying law treats as piracy, converting it into lawful privateering on behalf of the state.

3. **It commissions a private actor — the privateer — to act outside the standing military.** Privateers were privately owned, privately armed, privately financed, privately crewed, and privately profited from their captures (with a sovereign share). They operated under their own command, on their own ships, taking their own initiatives within the scope of the commission. They were not regular naval officers; they were licensed private combatants.

4. **It is exclusively a federal power.** The Constitution grants the power to Congress and **expressly forbids the states from granting letters of marque**. The reasoning is structural: the United States must speak with a single voice on questions of war and peace with foreign nations. If individual states could commission private warfare, the nation would be plunged into wars no general government had declared.

5. **It operates against foreign enemies — never against the issuing sovereign's own citizens.** The historical and modern conception of a letter of marque has always been an instrument turned outward, against the persons and property of an enemy state. A "letter of marque" turned inward — authorizing private violence against fellow citizens — has never existed in Anglo-American law and would be unconstitutional under several independent doctrines (due process, the prohibition on bills of attainder, the prohibition on summary punishment) before the marque-and-reprisal analysis even started.

The historical practice — *Privateers Acts*, the *Paris Declaration Respecting Maritime Law* of 1856 (which the United States did not formally join but has substantially observed), the limited modern resurrection of the power in periodic congressional proposals — confirms each of these structural features. The leading historical and academic treatment is the same: a letter of marque is a sovereign commission to a private actor to make war on a foreign enemy.

## 2. The Citizen Council Mechanism Has None of These Features

The Citizen Council mechanism in Section 9 of Operation Checkmate has none of the five defining structural features of a letter of marque. The two mechanisms are not similar in design, similar in effect, or similar in any element that the constitutional doctrine reaches.

### 2.1 The Citizen Council Is Not an Instrument of War

The Citizen Council exists to **initiate criminal prosecution under defined offenses against persons who, on a developed record, are alleged to have committed those offenses**. The Joint Declaration of Prosecution that twelve citizens produce has the legal force of a grand-jury indictment when filed in court. The case then proceeds through ordinary criminal procedure — arraignment, discovery, motion practice, jury trial, conviction or acquittal, sentencing if convicted, appeal as of right. Every step is an ordinary criminal-procedure step in a state court.

The Council does not declare war. It does not commission combat. It does not target a foreign enemy. It does not authorize the use of force. It does not authorize the capture of property. It does not authorize hostility of any kind. It produces a **legal document** that triggers a **judicial proceeding**. The proceeding ends with a verdict — not with the seizure of an enemy vessel.

A grand jury indicting a defendant under existing state law is doing exactly the same thing structurally as a Citizen Council issuing a Joint Declaration. No one thinks a grand jury is conducting privateering operations.

### 2.2 The Citizen Council Does Not Authorize Acts That Would Otherwise Be Criminal

A privateer without a letter of marque is a pirate. The marque converts the underlying piracy into lawful warfare.

The Citizen Council does not authorize any conduct that would otherwise be criminal. Filing a legal document with a court is not, under any body of law, a crime requiring sovereign authorization. Twelve citizens meeting and producing a written declaration is not, under any body of law, a violent act, a hostile act, an act of war, or an act of any kind requiring sovereign immunization from criminal liability. The Citizen Council mechanism does not function to **lift the criminal character of underlying conduct**, because there is no underlying criminal conduct to lift.

This is the structural test that most clearly distinguishes a letter of marque from any ordinary legal-process mechanism. A letter of marque is necessary because the privateer would, without it, be subject to prosecution for the conduct the letter authorizes. The Citizen Council requires no such immunization. Its participants are doing things that ordinary citizens have always been free to do: meeting, deliberating, agreeing, signing, and filing.

### 2.3 The Citizen Council Does Not Commission a Private Actor to Operate Outside the Standing Apparatus

A privateer operates **outside** the standing military, on a private vessel, with a private crew, taking captures on private initiative for private profit (with sovereign share). The Citizen Council, by contrast, operates **as the trigger to the standing prosecutorial apparatus**, not as a substitute for it.

When the Citizen Council issues a Joint Declaration of Prosecution, the case is prosecuted by **the County Prosecutor**, in the ordinary courts, by the ordinary prosecuting attorneys, under the ordinary rules of criminal procedure. The County Prosecutor's office is the standing executive apparatus of the state for criminal prosecution; the Council does not displace it, replace it, or operate alongside it. The Council provides the operative predicate — the indictment-equivalent — that the prosecutor's office is required to charge.

In cases where the County Prosecutor has a conflict, declines to prosecute, or fails to act within the timeline this Act specifies, the case proceeds through counsel designated by the Office of the Governor. Even in that case the prosecution is conducted by **state-designated counsel**, not by private actors operating on their own account.

No Council member captures property. No Council member retains profit from outcomes. No Council member is privately armed, privately financed, or privately commissioned to engage in any conduct. The Council member's role ends with the filing of the Declaration. From that point forward, the case is in the hands of state officers, in state courts, under state rules.

### 2.4 The Citizen Council Operates Under State Criminal-Law Authority, Not Under Any War Power

The constitutional power to grant letters of marque is part of the **war power** — vested in Congress, denied to the states. The constitutional power to define and prosecute crimes is part of the **police power** — reserved to the states under the Tenth Amendment, exercised under ordinary state legislative and judicial authority.

Operation Checkmate is, in every operative dimension, an exercise of state police power over criminal conduct occurring within state borders. It defines offenses, specifies elements, sets penalties, provides for prosecution, requires trial, and provides for appeal — the same architecture as Utah's existing criminal code (Title 76), simply applied to a category of conduct (abuse of public power) that the existing code has historically underreached. Nothing in the Act invokes the war power, the foreign-affairs power, the treaty power, or any other federal power reserved to Congress. The Act does not implicate Article I, §8, Clause 11 at any point; therefore Article I, §10, Clause 1 does not reach it.

If the Act were a letter of marque, Utah could not pass it, because Article I, §10, Clause 1 forbids state grant of letters of marque. The fact that Utah's police power authorizes the Act confirms its nature as ordinary criminal-law authority, not a marque-and-reprisal instrument.

### 2.5 The Citizen Council Operates Against Persons Within the Sovereign's Own Jurisdiction, Not Against Foreign Enemies

The defining outward-facing character of a letter of marque — commissioning hostility against the persons and property of a foreign enemy — is absent from the Act entirely.

The Act reaches the conduct of **persons exercising or purporting to exercise state authority against Utah citizens within the State of Utah**. Those persons are, themselves, almost always Utah citizens or residents. They are not enemies of the state. They are not foreign nationals. They are not subject to the law of war. They are subject to **ordinary state criminal jurisdiction**, in the ordinary courts, with the ordinary protections of state and federal due process.

A letter of marque turned inward against the issuing sovereign's own citizens has never existed in Anglo-American constitutional law. It would be foreclosed by due process, by the prohibition on bills of attainder, by the prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment, and by the requirement of trial — all before the marque-and-reprisal analysis even began. The Act has none of those problems because it is not an inward-turned letter of marque. It is an ordinary criminal statute with a citizen-initiated charging mechanism.

## 3. The Citizen Council Is Parallel to Long-Standing American Criminal-Procedure Mechanisms

The clearest way to see that the Citizen Council is not a letter of marque is to compare it to existing American mechanisms that share its operative features — citizen participation in the initiation of criminal proceedings — and that have stood for centuries without ever being thought to raise a marque-and-reprisal concern.

### 3.1 The Grand Jury — Twelve to Twenty-Three Citizens Issuing Indictments

The **grand jury** is a body of ordinary citizens, typically twelve to twenty-three in number, who hear evidence presented in secret and decide whether to issue an indictment that charges a defendant with a crime. The grand jury is older than the Constitution; the Fifth Amendment makes federal felony prosecutions contingent on grand-jury indictment ("No person shall be held to answer for a capital, or otherwise infamous crime, unless on a presentment or indictment of a Grand Jury").

A grand-jury indictment is the **paradigm** of citizen participation in criminal-process initiation. Twelve or more ordinary citizens, sitting outside the prosecutorial office, deliberating in their own room, on their own schedule, on a developed record, decide whether the state will charge a specific person with a specific offense. If they vote to indict, the prosecutor is required to proceed.

Nobody, in two and a half centuries of American constitutional law, has ever suggested that a grand jury issuing an indictment is acting as a privateer under a state letter of marque. The grand jury is the structural cousin of the Citizen Council. The Council operates on the same logic, with the same kind of citizen participation, producing a document with the same legal effect (indictment-equivalent), triggering the same kind of subsequent state-conducted prosecution.

### 3.2 Citizen-Initiated Criminal Complaints

Most American jurisdictions, including Utah, recognize a **citizen-initiated criminal complaint** procedure under which a private citizen can swear out a complaint before a magistrate alleging that another person has committed a crime. The magistrate evaluates the complaint and, if probable cause is established, issues a warrant or summons and the case proceeds through ordinary prosecution.

The citizen complaint is, like the Citizen Council, a mechanism by which private actors can initiate criminal proceedings. It has existed in American criminal procedure since the founding. It has never been thought to raise a letter-of-marque concern.

### 3.3 Qui Tam Actions and Private Attorney General Statutes

Under the False Claims Act, 31 U.S.C. §§ 3729–3733, and a variety of state analogues, private persons ("relators") may initiate civil actions on behalf of the government against parties who have defrauded the government, with the relator entitled to a share of any recovery. *Vermont Agency of Natural Resources v. United States ex rel. Stevens*, 529 U.S. 765 (2000), upheld this structure. Qui tam actions have been a fixture of Anglo-American law since the medieval period.

A qui tam relator is, in operative effect, a privately initiating actor in a government enforcement proceeding — even closer in some respects to the privateer paradigm than the Citizen Council is, because the relator receives a financial share of recovery (as a privateer historically received a share of captures). And yet qui tam actions are not letters of marque. They have never been thought to be. The reason is the same: they operate within the standing legal apparatus, against persons within the sovereign's own jurisdiction, under ordinary judicial procedure, with full due process for the defendant.

### 3.4 Public-Initiation Statutes in State Practice

A number of states maintain procedures by which private citizens may petition for prosecution, request the appointment of a special prosecutor, or initiate disciplinary proceedings against officials. Texas, for instance, has historical experience with citizen-initiated grand-jury inquiries. Pennsylvania's investigating grand jury can be convened on a private citizen's petition. Florida's citizen-petition mechanism for grand-jury investigations is recognized in state practice. None of these state mechanisms has been treated as a letter of marque.

## 4. The "Privateer" Analogy Breaks Down at Every Element

If one tried to construct an analogy between Citizen Council members and privateers, the comparison fails at every operative point:

| Element                                                | Privateer (Letter of Marque)                                         | Citizen Council Member (Operation Checkmate)                                                                  |
| ------------------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Authorization source**                               | Sovereign commission specifically lifting criminal character of acts | Statutory criminal procedure recognizing citizen role in initiating ordinary indictment-equivalent document   |
| **Target**                                             | Foreign enemy's vessels, cargo, persons                              | Utah resident alleged to have committed a defined offense under Utah criminal law                             |
| **Means**                                              | Armed force, ship-to-ship combat, capture, seizure                   | Deliberation, written declaration, filing with court                                                          |
| **Outcome**                                            | Property captured, persons taken, vessels prized                     | Court case opened, defendant arraigned, prosecution conducted by state                                        |
| **Position relative to state apparatus**               | Outside the standing military, operating on private initiative       | Triggering the standing prosecutorial and judicial apparatus, which then conducts the case                    |
| **Compensation**                                       | Private share of captures and prizes                                 | None                                                                                                          |
| **Underlying conduct's character without the mechanism** | Piracy — a capital crime in most jurisdictions                       | Lawful citizen activity (meeting, deliberating, signing, filing) — no underlying criminal character to lift   |
| **Constitutional power invoked**                       | Article I §8 cl. 11 war power (federal exclusive)                    | Article I §10 reserved police power (state ordinary criminal jurisdiction)                                    |
| **Subject of the proceeding**                          | Foreign enemy with no due-process rights against the issuing power   | Defendant with full state and federal due-process protections, including jury trial and right to counsel      |

The two mechanisms are not similar. They are not parallel. They are not adjacent. They occupy different constitutional spheres, perform different functions, operate by different means, against different parties, with different consequences.

## 5. The Constitutional Text Is Not Ambiguous on This Point

The Bill of Attainder Clauses, the Ex Post Facto Clauses, and the Marque-and-Reprisal Clause are sometimes invoked together in litigation as a triad of "structural-protection" objections to creative legislation. Each operates differently, and the marque-and-reprisal objection is the most narrowly cabined of the three.

The text of Article I, §10, Clause 1 prohibits states from "grant[ing] Letters of Marque and Reprisal." The constitutional doctrine surrounding this clause has, in the entire history of American jurisprudence, been confined to the maritime-warfare context — privateering authorizations, prize courts, and the question of whether a particular instrument of executive authorization constitutes a marque under the war power.

There is no body of doctrine treating ordinary criminal-process mechanisms — grand juries, citizen complaints, qui tam actions, special-prosecutor petitions, victim-initiated charging requests — as letters of marque. There is no Supreme Court case applying the Marque-and-Reprisal Clause to a state criminal procedure. There is no scholarly tradition arguing that the Clause reaches anything other than what its text plainly addresses: state authorization of private warfare.

Operation Checkmate falls entirely outside that doctrine. It is not a state authorization of private warfare. It is not an authorization of any kind of warfare. It is not an authorization of any conduct that would otherwise be a crime. It is a state criminal statute with a state criminal-procedure mechanism for triggering prosecution. The Marque-and-Reprisal Clause does not reach it.

---

## 6. The Utah Constitution Confirms the Same Result

The Utah Constitution does not contain a separate Marque-and-Reprisal provision; the federal constitutional restriction in Article I, §10, Clause 1 governs directly. The Utah Supreme Court has had no occasion to apply the doctrine to anything resembling Operation Checkmate's Citizen Council mechanism, because no Utah statute or precedent comes within the doctrine's textual reach.

Utah's existing criminal code already contains numerous mechanisms for non-prosecutorial initiation of charges or processes — citizen complaints under state rules of criminal procedure, grand-jury practice under Title 77, Chapter 10a; victim-petition mechanisms for various protective orders and proceedings — none of which has been treated as a letter of marque. The Citizen Council mechanism is, structurally, a member of the same family.

---

## 7. Conclusion

Operation Checkmate is not a Letter of Marque and Reprisal because:

1. **It is not an instrument of war.** It is an instrument of ordinary criminal procedure.
2. **It authorizes no conduct that would otherwise be criminal.** Filing legal documents in a court is not a crime requiring sovereign immunization.
3. **It commissions no private actor to operate outside the standing legal apparatus.** The Council triggers the standing prosecutorial and judicial apparatus, which then conducts the case.
4. **It does not invoke the war power.** It exercises the state police power over criminal conduct within state borders.
5. **It does not operate against foreign enemies.** It operates against persons within Utah's ordinary criminal jurisdiction, with full due-process protections.

The Citizen Council mechanism is structurally parallel to the grand jury, to citizen-initiated criminal complaints, and to qui tam actions — long-standing American mechanisms of citizen participation in the initiation of legal proceedings, none of which has ever been thought to be a letter of marque. The Marque-and-Reprisal Clauses of Article I, §8 and Article I, §10 do not reach Operation Checkmate at any point. The objection is foreclosed by the text of the Constitution, by the entire history of American jurisprudence applying the Marque-and-Reprisal Clauses, and by the existence of multiple parallel American criminal-procedure mechanisms operating without any constitutional difficulty under the same structural logic the Council uses.

A letter of marque is a sovereign commission to a private actor to make war on a foreign enemy. Operation Checkmate is a state criminal statute with a citizen-initiated charging mechanism for ordinary domestic offenses. The two are different instruments, in different constitutional spheres, for different purposes — and no reading of the Marque-and-Reprisal Clauses can collapse the distinction.
