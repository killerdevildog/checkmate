# Why No Major Party Has Pushed This Bill

A foreseeable question for any citizen encountering Operation Checkmate for the first time is: **if this bill addresses such a deep structural problem, why has no major party championed it?**

The short answer is structural and verifiable. **Both major parties depend, in different operative ways, on the gatekeeper class the bill reaches.** Police unions, public-sector unions, prosecutor associations, the judicial-confirmation industry, regulator alliances, federal-law-enforcement bargaining units, and donor classes connected to government contracting are constituencies of one or both major parties. A bill that reaches **all** of these constituencies — applying the same criminal-law standard to every government actor regardless of office, party, or rank — does not have a natural champion in either party's existing coalition.

This document develops that answer with documented examples. It identifies the specific structural constraints that operate on each party, the specific reform bills each party has actually advanced (and the categorical limits of those bills), the donor-class and constituency dependencies that produce the limits, the mirror-image pattern in which each party reforms its opponents' immunity while protecting its own, and the operative reason Operation Checkmate is, by design, a citizen instrument rather than a party instrument.

The point of the document is not to attack either party. The bill's premise is that the architecture both parties have helped sustain — the immunity architecture, the selective-prosecution patterns, the gatekeeper class with different rules for officials and citizens — is a **bipartisan failure**, not a failure of one side or the other. The point is to explain why that failure has persisted: not because either party is uniquely corrupt, but because both party coalitions, in their structural composition, would have to break with significant constituencies to advance the kind of equal-application reform the bill represents.

## 1. What the Bill Reaches That Threatens Both Parties' Coalitions

The bill applies the same criminal-law standard to every government actor whose conduct violates the substantive provisions of the Act. The categories of actors the bill reaches include:

- **Police officers, sheriffs, deputies, and state law-enforcement personnel** (sworn or unsworn, federal-deputized or state-only)
- **Prosecutors** at every level — state district attorneys, U.S. attorneys, federal-prosecutor leadership
- **Judges** — state and federal, trial and appellate, civil and criminal
- **Administrative agency personnel** exercising substantive decisional authority
- **Regulators and inspectors** exercising compliance and enforcement authority
- **Federal law-enforcement personnel** under §14.7 (Federal Officer Status Not a Shield), including ICE, CBP, FBI, DEA, ATF, U.S. Marshals, federal protective services, and others operating against Utah citizens
- **Government contractors** performing coercive functions under federal or state contract (per the structural analysis in `why_this_is_not_a_letter_of_mark.md`)
- **Elected officials** — legislative, executive, judicial
- **Appointed officials** at every level
- **Public-employee union officials** whose conduct in the union's exercise of state-derived authority reaches the bill's substantive offenses

Each of these categories includes constituencies aligned with one or both major parties. The bill is, by design, agnostic about which party the actor belongs to. Conduct that violates the Act is reachable on the same terms regardless of party. **That symmetry is what makes the bill structurally unattractive to either party's existing coalition: each party would have to accept that the bill applies to its own people on the same terms it applies to its opponents.**

## 2. The Democratic Party's Structural Constraints

The Democratic Party's coalition includes several constituencies that the bill reaches in ways that produce structural friction with party advancement of the bill.

### 2.1 Public-Sector Unions

Public-sector unions — AFSCME (American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees), AFGE (American Federation of Government Employees), NEA (National Education Association), AFT (American Federation of Teachers), SEIU's public-sector locals — are among the largest organized constituencies in the Democratic coalition. They represent federal, state, and local government employees, including many categories the bill reaches: child-welfare workers, regulatory inspectors, administrative-agency employees, public-defender investigators, and others.

The bill's substantive offenses reach the conduct of these employees when their official conduct violates the Act's provisions — and the bill's removal of immunity defenses under §5.1 reaches the protections public-sector employees have traditionally invoked through civil-service procedural protections and union grievance procedures. A bill that makes a child-welfare worker personally liable for an unlawful removal (§1.1.3 Battery, family-separation prong) cuts against the immunity assumptions that public-sector union representation has historically operated within.

Democratic resistance to **Schedule F** reclassification of federal employees (Trump's 2020 EO and the second-administration reissuance) reflects this structural dependency. Schedule F made federal employees in policy-influencing positions easier to remove for performance and conduct reasons; the Democratic position has consistently been that civil-service protections should be preserved against such reclassification. The bill, by removing immunity protections within the scope of its substantive offenses, operates in a similar structural direction — and produces the same kind of friction with the public-sector union constituency.

### 2.2 Progressive Prosecutors and Prosecutorial Discretion

A significant strand of the contemporary Democratic coalition has been the **progressive-prosecutor movement** — Larry Krasner (Philadelphia), Alvin Bragg (Manhattan), Chesa Boudin (San Francisco, recalled), George Gascón (Los Angeles), Kim Foxx (Cook County), and analogous prosecutors elected on platforms emphasizing prosecutorial discretion as a tool of criminal-justice reform.

Whatever one thinks of the substantive merits of progressive prosecution, the political constituency it represents has a structural interest in preserving broad prosecutorial discretion — including discretion not to prosecute certain categories of conduct. The bill's §1.1.8 (Obstruction of a Private Citizen) reaches **selective non-prosecution**, including non-prosecution of a favored official or political ally for conduct that would be prosecuted in an ordinary citizen. The progressive-prosecutor model is built on the assumption that prosecutorial discretion is a legitimate and broad tool; the bill treats selective application of that discretion against politically-disfavored citizens, or in favor of politically-favored officials, as a substantive criminal offense.

Democratic prosecutors operate under **prosecutorial immunity** as developed in *Imbler v. Pachtman*, 424 U.S. 409 (1976). The bill abrogates that immunity within its scope (§5.1.1). A bill that makes a Democratic prosecutor personally liable for malicious prosecution, knowing use of false testimony, or coordinated political targeting under §1.1.14 reaches the same prosecutors whose discretion the progressive coalition has championed.

### 2.3 Federal Administrative State

The Democratic Party has, for decades, been the principal defender of the federal administrative state — EPA, HHS, Department of Education, OSHA, EEOC, FTC, FCC, CFPB, and the broader regulatory architecture. The administrative state's exercise of substantive authority depends in significant part on doctrines the bill's §1.1.46 (Judicial Imposter) reaches — agency adjudication where the agency is both party and decision-maker, agency-imposed penalties without prior independent judicial determination, and the various ways executive-branch actors perform judicial functions without the constitutional independence the judicial function requires.

The Supreme Court's recent decisions in *SEC v. Jarkesy*, 144 S. Ct. 2117 (2024), and *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*, 144 S. Ct. 2244 (2024), have begun to operate against the administrative state's traditional discretion in ways the Democratic coalition has resisted. The bill operates in a parallel structural direction — making the conduct itself reachable as a substantive criminal offense rather than just as a basis for regulatory invalidation. The Democratic political coalition's investment in defending the administrative state is structurally inconsistent with championing a bill that criminalizes core features of how the administrative state has operated.

### 2.4 Civil-Rights Litigation Architecture

A significant Democratic donor and constituency block is the **plaintiffs' civil-rights bar** — the trial-lawyer associations (American Association for Justice, formerly ATLA), the NAACP Legal Defense Fund, the ACLU, the Southern Poverty Law Center, and various other organizations whose institutional model depends on litigating §1983 civil-rights cases through the federal courts.

The plaintiffs' bar has been the most consistent advocate for **qualified-immunity reform** through legislative channels — supporting bills like Rep. Ayanna Pressley and Sen. Ed Markey's *Ending Qualified Immunity Act* (introduced 2020, 2021, 2023). But the reform model advanced by the plaintiffs' bar has been **narrow and civil**, not broad and criminal — focused on removing the qualified-immunity defense in §1983 civil suits, principally as applied to police, while preserving the broader litigation architecture in which the plaintiffs' bar operates. The bill goes substantially beyond this: it makes the underlying conduct a state criminal offense, abrogates **all** immunity doctrines within its scope (judicial, prosecutorial, sovereign, legislative, executive), and creates a citizen-driven enforcement mechanism that does not depend on plaintiffs'-bar litigation at all.

A bill that bypasses the plaintiffs'-bar litigation model in favor of criminal-law accountability with citizen-initiated enforcement is, in operative effect, a competitor to the existing reform pathway the Democratic-aligned plaintiffs' bar has championed. The political coalition's investment in the existing reform pathway produces structural friction with the bill's broader approach.

### 2.5 Documented Democratic Reform Efforts and Their Categorical Limits

The principal Democratic-advanced reform bill on government accountability has been the **George Floyd Justice in Policing Act**, passed by the Democratic House in March 2021 (by a vote of 220-212), where it died in the Senate. The bill's substantive content was significant but **categorically limited**:

- Modified, but did not eliminate, qualified immunity — and only as to police officers, not other officials
- Created a federal registry of police misconduct
- Banned chokeholds and no-knock warrants in federal cases
- Required body cameras for federal officers
- Did not reach judicial immunity (judges)
- Did not reach prosecutorial immunity (prosecutors)
- Did not reach sovereign immunity (the State itself)
- Did not reach administrative-agency conduct (regulators, inspectors)
- Did not reach federal law-enforcement personnel outside the police context

The categorical limit is structural, not accidental. The Democratic coalition could advance a bill reaching police misconduct because the coalition's relationship with police unions is more attenuated than with the other constituencies. Reaching judges, prosecutors, administrative-agency personnel, and the broader gatekeeper class would have required breaking with the public-sector unions, the progressive prosecutors, the federal-administrative-state defenders, and the civil-rights plaintiffs' bar that the coalition relies on.

Operation Checkmate, by contrast, reaches all of those constituencies, on the same terms. The categorical scope of the bill is what no major party reform effort has matched, precisely because no major party can match it without breaking with significant pieces of its own coalition.

## 3. The Republican Party's Structural Constraints

The Republican Party's coalition includes several constituencies that the bill reaches in ways that produce structural friction with party advancement of the bill.

### 3.1 Police Unions and "Law and Order" Positioning

The largest law-enforcement organizations — the Fraternal Order of Police (FOP), the National Association of Police Organizations (NAPO), the National Sheriffs' Association, the International Union of Police Associations — have, for the most part of the past several decades, endorsed and worked closely with the Republican Party. The FOP endorsed Donald Trump for President in 2016, 2020, and 2024. Republican candidates campaign on "law and order" themes that depend significantly on police-union political support and on the implicit assumption that police misconduct will not be subject to the same kind of accountability the bill imposes.

Republican opposition to comprehensive qualified-immunity reform has been consistent across decades. Senator Tim Scott's 2020 *JUSTICE Act* — the Republican response to the George Floyd Justice in Policing Act — preserved qualified immunity in modified form, requiring greater data collection but not eliminating the doctrine. Republican leadership has repeatedly characterized broad QI elimination as "defunding the police by other means," reflecting the structural dependence on police-union political support.

A bill that makes police officers personally liable for unlawful detention, unlawful force, search-and-seizure violations, and the full range of misconduct reachable under §1.1.3 (Battery), §1.1.10 (Anarchy), §1.1.12 (Terrorism), and §1.1.32 (Pretextualism) — without the qualified-immunity defense — is, in operative effect, the bill the police-union political coalition has spent decades resisting. Republican political support for such a bill would require breaking with one of the party's most reliable organized constituencies.

### 3.2 Federal Law Enforcement

The Republican coalition has invested significantly in federal law enforcement in specific categories — Customs and Border Protection (CBP), Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE), the Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA), the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives (ATF) in some respects, the U.S. Marshals Service, federal protective services, and others. The political coalition's emphasis on immigration enforcement, drug enforcement, and federal law-enforcement deployment depends on broad federal-officer discretion and on the absence of state-level criminal-law accountability for federal-officer conduct.

The bill's §14.7 (Federal Officer Status Not a Shield) makes federal officers reachable under Utah criminal law for conduct violating the Act against Utah citizens within Utah. CBP and ICE personnel operating in Utah, federal protective service officers operating at federal facilities in Utah, DEA personnel operating in Utah — all are reachable under the bill on the same terms as state officers.

This produces structural friction with the Republican coalition's investment in robust federal law-enforcement authority. A bill that exposes ICE personnel to Utah felony prosecution for unlawful family separation, or CBP personnel to Utah felony prosecution for excessive force, or DEA personnel to Utah felony prosecution for fabricated probable cause, is the kind of accountability mechanism the political coalition that defends those agencies has resisted.

### 3.3 The Judicial-Confirmation Industry

The Republican Party has, since the 1980s and especially since the 2010s, invested heavily in **judicial confirmation politics** as a central organizing project. The Federalist Society, the Heritage Foundation's judicial advocacy programs, the Judicial Crisis Network (later the Article III Project, then the Bouvier Institute), and various other Republican-aligned organizations have built an apparatus for identifying, vetting, supporting, and confirming federal judges — particularly Supreme Court justices but also appellate and district judges.

The judicial-confirmation industry operates within an assumption about judicial discretion and judicial immunity that the bill substantially constrains. Federal judges confirmed through this apparatus operate under **absolute judicial immunity** as developed in *Bradley v. Fisher*, *Stump v. Sparkman*, and *Mireles v. Waco* — doctrines the bill explicitly abrogates within its scope (§5.1.1). A bill that makes federal judges personally liable, under Utah state criminal law, for conduct violating the Act against Utah citizens is structurally inconsistent with the deference the judicial-confirmation industry's model presupposes.

Additionally, the bill's §1.1.25 (Legislate from the Bench) makes judicial doctrinal invention beyond the Article III power itself a substantive criminal offense. The judicial-confirmation industry's investment in particular jurisprudential schools — originalism, textualism, structuralism — does not insulate confirmed judges from §1.1.25 where their substantive conduct violates the provision's elements. The bill makes judicial accountability operate regardless of the jurisprudential orientation of the judge.

### 3.4 Federal Contractor Donor Class

The Republican Party's donor base includes significant federal-contractor representation — defense contractors (Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, Boeing, General Dynamics, Northrop Grumman), security contractors (Constellis/Academi, DynCorp, Triple Canopy), and federal-services contractors broadly. The bill's reach to coercive-function contractors under the color-of-authority element (developed in `why_government_contractors_are_reachable.md`) produces structural friction with this donor class.

A bill that makes private military contractors operating under federal contract reachable under Utah criminal law for conduct violating the Act against Utah citizens is, in operative effect, a constraint on the contractor model the donor class profits from.

### 3.5 Documented Republican Reform Efforts and Their Categorical Limits

The principal Republican-advanced reform bills on government accountability have been **categorically limited in mirror-image ways to Democratic reform efforts**.

Senator Tim Scott's 2020 *JUSTICE Act*:
- Required data collection on police misconduct
- Provided incentives for state-level reform without federal mandate
- Modified but preserved qualified immunity
- Did not reach prosecutors, judges, or administrative personnel
- Did not reach federal law-enforcement conduct

Various Republican-introduced bills on **prosecutorial reform** have focused principally on federal prosecutors' conduct in politically-charged cases (Russia investigation, January 6 investigation, Mar-a-Lago search) — but have not advanced comprehensive reform of prosecutorial immunity applicable to all prosecutors regardless of political alignment.

Various Republican-introduced bills on **DOJ accountability** have focused on the same politically-charged categories — but again, have not advanced symmetric reform that would apply on the same terms to Republican-appointed prosecutors in other contexts.

The mirror-image pattern is direct: each party's reform efforts have categorically excluded the gatekeeper class the party itself depends on. **Republicans reform federal-prosecutor conduct in cases against Republicans; they do not reform police-officer immunity. Democrats reform police-officer immunity; they do not reform prosecutor immunity or federal-administrative-state conduct.** Neither party has advanced reform that reaches the full gatekeeper class on equal terms.

## 4. The Bipartisan Pattern: Reform Their Opponents, Protect Themselves

The structural pattern visible in both parties' reform efforts is the same: **reform the immunity that protects your political opponents; protect the immunity that protects your political allies**.

- Democrats favor reforming police qualified immunity (police unions endorse R); preserve prosecutorial discretion (prosecutors include progressive D allies), judicial immunity (D-appointed federal judges), and administrative-agency authority (federal regulatory state is D-aligned).
- Republicans favor reforming federal-prosecutor discretion (DOJ is operationally adverse to recent R candidates) and federal-administrative-state conduct (regulators are D-aligned); preserve police qualified immunity (police unions endorse R), federal law-enforcement discretion (ICE/CBP/DEA are R-aligned), and judicial-immunity assumptions (R-confirmed federal judges).

The pattern is not malicious in either case. It is the predictable operation of party coalitions whose composition includes constituencies the bill would reach. Each party's reform efforts are calibrated to what the party's coalition can support; the bill is calibrated to what equal application of law actually requires. The two calibrations are structurally different, and the bill is the more demanding one.

The bipartisan failure the bill exists to address is precisely this pattern: each party's selective reform leaves the underlying architecture intact. **An immunity system that exempts some officials but not others is not equal application of law; it is the institutional preference of whichever party's allies are being protected.** The bill's premise — that the same rules apply to every government actor regardless of office, party, or rank — is structurally inconsistent with either party's selective-reform model.

## 5. The Citizen Council Mechanism — Why Neither Party Wants It

Beyond the substantive reach of the bill, the **Citizen Council mechanism** (Section 9) creates structural friction with both parties' political models.

The Citizen Council allows twelve or more citizens, convened outside the prosecutorial office, to issue a Joint Declaration of Prosecution that has the legal force of a grand-jury indictment. The County Prosecutor must prosecute it; if the Prosecutor declines or fails to act, the Office of the Governor must designate counsel. The mechanism explicitly does not depend on prosecutorial discretion — it operates **around** prosecutorial discretion.

Both major parties have a structural interest in **preserving prosecutorial discretion**. Each party controls some prosecutorial offices at some times. Each party has used prosecutorial discretion to advance its policy priorities — Democratic prosecutors declining to prosecute certain categories of conduct under progressive-prosecutor platforms; Republican prosecutors declining to prosecute certain categories of conduct as a matter of "law and order" prioritization; both parties using discretion in politically-charged cases involving party allies and party opponents.

A mechanism that allows citizens to bypass prosecutorial discretion is, in operative effect, a constraint on the discretion both parties have used. Neither party's political coalition gains from such a mechanism in the abstract; each party's coalition is better off preserving the discretion when its prosecutors hold it and contesting the discretion when the opposing party's prosecutors hold it. The Citizen Council mechanism eliminates the variable entirely — by routing citizen-driven enforcement around the prosecutorial office.

Republican prosecutors who would decline to prosecute Republican officials for misconduct cannot rely on that discretion under the Citizen Council mechanism. Democratic prosecutors who would decline to prosecute Democratic officials for misconduct cannot rely on that discretion either. Both parties' selective enforcement is constrained by the same structural feature, on the same terms.

## 7. Why This Is a Citizen Bill, Not a Party Bill

The conclusion that follows from the structural analysis above is the conclusion the original FAQ entry stated, now developed with documented examples:

**Operation Checkmate cannot have a natural champion in either party's existing coalition because neither party's existing coalition can accept the bill's symmetrical reach without breaking with significant constituencies.** The bill, by reaching every government actor under color of authority, by abrogating every category of immunity within its scope, by providing citizen-driven enforcement that bypasses prosecutorial discretion, and by treating selective enforcement of the law itself as a substantive criminal offense, is structurally inconsistent with the coalition-management imperatives of both major parties.

This is not a defect. It is a design feature.

The bill is designed to advance through **grassroots citizen support rather than party sponsorship**. The mechanism by which it advances — citizen-signed petitions, citizen-initiated legislative pressure, citizen-driven enforcement once enacted through Citizen Councils — does not require the cooperation of either party's political infrastructure. The bill's success depends on whether enough citizens, across enough political identities, conclude that the structural problem is real and that the structural remedy is correct. The bill does not depend on whether either party concludes it can support the bill within its existing coalition.

The cross-spectrum support documented in the bill's other community guides — for Black citizens (`culture/blacklivesmatter.md`), LGBTQ+ citizens (`culture/lgbtq.md`), constitutional conservatives and libertarians (`culture/righists.md`), citizens in financial hardship (`culture/financial_hardship.md`), public servants (`culture/public_servants.md`), and the structural alignment with stated positions of the Trump administration ([`trump_administration_alignment.md`](../trump_administration_alignment.md)) — together demonstrate the cross-spectrum citizen support the bill is designed to advance through.

Each of those guides demonstrates, from a different starting point, the same structural conclusion: citizens of every political identity have reasons to support a bill that applies the same rules to every government actor. The parties cannot champion the bill because their coalitions include the gatekeeper class the bill reaches. But the citizens whose protection the bill is for — every citizen, regardless of party — can champion it.

## 8. The Operative Conclusion

To answer the question the FAQ entry posed:

**No major party has championed Operation Checkmate because both major parties' coalitions include constituencies the bill reaches.** Public-sector unions and the federal administrative state for the Democratic coalition; police unions and the judicial-confirmation industry for the Republican coalition; the donor class of government contractors, the prosecutor associations, the trial-lawyer associations, and the gatekeeper class generally for both. A bill that applies criminal-law accountability to all of them simultaneously does not have a coalition home in either party.

The bill is, by design, a **citizen instrument**. It advances through citizen petition, citizen support, citizen pressure on legislators, and — once enacted — through citizen enforcement under Section 9. It does not require either party's cooperation to be drafted, to be advocated for, to be supported, or to be put on a legislator's desk. The citizens supporting the bill are the principal political force the bill depends on.

That is why this is a citizen-written bill rather than a party bill. That is why it exists on GitHub for any citizen to read, criticize, and improve. That is why support is recorded through grassroots channels (citizens.checkmate@gmail.com with subject CHECKMATE, name and zip code) rather than through party infrastructure. And that is why the bill, when it advances, will advance through citizen movement rather than through party endorsement.

The same rules apply to everyone. That principle has no natural party home — but it has a natural citizen home, in the protection of citizens from government conduct that exceeds constitutional limits. Operation Checkmate is the operative legal mechanism for that protection, and the citizens for whom the protection exists are the constituency the bill is for.
