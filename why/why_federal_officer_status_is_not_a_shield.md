# Why Federal Officer Status Is Not a Shield

Section 14.7 of Operation Checkmate ("Federal Officer Status Not a Shield") reaches federal officers acting within the State of Utah and abrogates *In re Neagle*, 135 U.S. 1 (1890), to the extent the case has been read to shield federal officers from state criminal prosecution for conduct that constitutes a state-law offense within the State. The section's operative principle:

> A federal officer's status as a federal officer does not shield the officer from Utah criminal liability for conduct that constitutes a state-law offense under this Act. Federal officers acting within the State of Utah are subject to Utah's criminal jurisdiction for state-law offenses they commit. The Supremacy Clause grants federal officers protection only against state laws that would obstruct the legitimate exercise of federal authority; it does not — and was never intended to — confer immunity from state criminal prosecution for conduct that exceeds federal authority or that constitutes an independent state criminal offense.

The provision is among the most pointed in the Act. Federal officers operating in Utah — Immigration and Customs Enforcement, Customs and Border Protection, the Federal Bureau of Investigation, the Drug Enforcement Administration, the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives, the U.S. Marshals Service, federal protective service, and others — exercise operative authority against Utah citizens within Utah's borders. Where that authority is exercised within constitutional and statutory limits, the State has no operative quarrel with the conduct. Where the authority is exceeded, where the conduct meets the elements of a state criminal offense under the Act, the State has the operative duty and the operative authority to apply its criminal law to the actor.

This document develops the doctrinal grounding for §14.7. It addresses the Supremacy Clause's actual operative scope, the Tenth Amendment's reserved police power over crimes within state borders, the dual-sovereignty doctrine confirming the independence of federal and state criminal jurisdiction, the original meaning of *In re Neagle* and the operative gap between that holding and the broader reading the case has been given, the structural argument the Founders built into the constitutional design, and the operative consequence of the section's application.

The companion document [why_we_can_abrogate_supreme_court_cases.md](why_we_can_abrogate_supreme_court_cases.md) develops the broader two-grounds framework under which the Act addresses federal judicial doctrine that exceeds constitutional authority. This document develops the specific operative application to *In re Neagle* and the federal-officer context.

## 1. The Supremacy Clause's Actual Operative Scope

Article VI, Clause 2 of the United States Constitution provides:

> "This Constitution, and the Laws of the United States which shall be made in Pursuance thereof; and all Treaties made, or which shall be made, under the Authority of the United States, shall be the supreme Law of the Land; and the Judges in every State shall be bound thereby, any Thing in the Constitution or Laws of any State to the contrary notwithstanding."

The clause is the operative foundation of any argument that federal authority preempts state authority. The operative content is, however, narrower than the broad readings the clause has sometimes been given. The Supremacy Clause grants supremacy only to federal law **made in Pursuance** of the Constitution. Federal action exceeding constitutional authority is not "made in Pursuance" of the Constitution and is not entitled to Supremacy Clause status. The principle is developed in detail in [why_we_can_abrogate_supreme_court_cases.md](why_we_can_abrogate_supreme_court_cases.md).

### 1.1 The "Pursuance" Qualifier

The "in Pursuance" qualifier is the operative limitation. Federal law that is constitutionally authorized — enacted within the operative scope of congressional power, signed by the President or enacted over veto, applied within the operative scope of executive authority by federal officers — is supreme. Federal action outside this operative scope is not. The text of the Supremacy Clause is direct on this point; the operative implications are also direct.

A federal officer exercising authority granted by constitutional federal law, within the operative scope of that grant, is engaged in conduct that the Supremacy Clause's operative architecture protects against state interference that would obstruct the constitutional exercise. A federal officer exercising authority outside the constitutional grant, or beyond its operative scope, is not engaged in conduct the Supremacy Clause protects. The actor has stepped outside the operative scope of "Pursuance" and is, in the operative sense developed in [why_anarchy_names_lawless_government.md](why_anarchy_names_lawless_government.md), exercising power outside *archē*.

### 1.2 The Operative Effect on Federal-Officer Liability

The operative effect for federal-officer liability under state law is direct. Where the federal officer's conduct is within the operative scope of federal authority, the Supremacy Clause prevents states from applying their criminal law to obstruct the conduct. Where the conduct is outside the operative scope of federal authority — where the officer has exceeded the constitutional grant, committed conduct constituting an independent state criminal offense, or both — the Supremacy Clause does not shield the conduct. The State retains its operative authority to apply its criminal law to the conduct as it would to any other criminal conduct within its borders.

§14.7 operates within this operative architecture. The section does not purport to reach federal officers' conduct within the operative scope of federal authority; it reaches conduct that exceeds federal authority or that constitutes an independent state criminal offense under the Act. The operative reach is calibrated to the operative scope the Supremacy Clause actually protects.

## 2. The Tenth Amendment's Reserved Police Power

The Tenth Amendment provides:

> "The powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution, nor prohibited by it to the States, are reserved to the States respectively, or to the people."

The operative implication for §14.7 is direct: the police power — the operative authority to define and prosecute crimes occurring within state borders — is one of the powers reserved to the States. The States have, throughout American constitutional history, exercised plenary operative authority over crimes within their jurisdiction. The Tenth Amendment's reservation extends to all crimes within the operative jurisdiction, whether the actor is a private citizen, a state officer, a federal officer, or a person operating without any official capacity at all.

### 2.1 The Plenary Nature of the State Police Power

The state police power has been recognized as plenary across the operative arc of American constitutional jurisprudence. The Supreme Court has consistently treated it as the operative core of state sovereignty. *Lopez v. United States*, 514 U.S. 549 (1995) — the operative limit on federal commerce-clause expansion — rests in part on the structural premise that the states' plenary authority over crimes within their borders is not displaced by federal regulatory authority over interstate commerce. *Bond v. United States*, 564 U.S. 211 (2011), reaffirmed the structural principle that federalism operates as a structural protection for citizen liberty, with state authority over crimes within state borders as one of its operative features.

The plenary state police power reaches crimes committed by any person within state borders. The actor's federal-officer status is not, by the operative architecture of federalism, a categorical exemption from the state's operative authority to apply its criminal law. The operative architecture of dual sovereignty (developed in Section 3 below) confirms this conclusion through the related question of double jeopardy: federal and state criminal jurisdiction are operatively independent, and either jurisdiction may apply its criminal law to the same conduct.

### 2.2 The Anti-Commandeering Doctrine Reinforces the State's Authority

The anti-commandeering doctrine — *New York v. United States*, 505 U.S. 144 (1992); *Printz v. United States*, 521 U.S. 898 (1997); *Murphy v. NCAA*, 138 S. Ct. 1461 (2018) — reaches the operative principle that the federal government cannot commandeer state legislative or executive function for federal regulatory purposes. The operative principle is the structural counterpart of the operative principle for §14.7: as the federal government cannot compel the state to enact federal regulatory schemes or to enforce federal mandates, the federal government cannot compel the state to refrain from applying its criminal law to conduct occurring within its borders.

The operative architecture is symmetrical. The federal government cannot direct the state to do its bidding through commandeering; the federal government also cannot direct the state to refrain from exercising its plenary criminal-law authority through doctrinal immunization of federal officers from state criminal law. Both would convert the operative architecture of federalism into a one-way ratchet operating against state authority. The constitutional design does not contemplate this.

### 2.3 The State as the Citizen's First Protective Layer

The structural argument developed in [why_does_the_government_even_exist.md](why_does_the_government_even_exist.md) treats the state as the citizen's first organized protective layer in the constitutional hierarchy. The operative implication for §14.7 is direct: where federal officers' conduct against Utah citizens within Utah's borders meets the elements of a state criminal offense, the operative authority and the operative duty of the State to protect its citizens through application of its criminal law is at its operative maximum.

The Founders' framework, developed in *Federalist No. 28* (Hamilton on the operative axiom that state governments will, in all possible contingencies, afford complete security against invasions of the public liberty by the national authority), *Federalist No. 46* (Madison on the state as the citizen's protective resource against federal overreach), and the broader operative architecture of federalism, treats this as a structural feature of the constitutional design. §14.7 operates within this design.

## 3. The Dual-Sovereignty Doctrine

The dual-sovereignty doctrine confirms the operative independence of federal and state criminal jurisdiction. The doctrine has been articulated by the Supreme Court repeatedly and most recently reaffirmed in *Gamble v. United States*, 587 U.S. ___ (2019), 139 S. Ct. 1960.

### 3.1 The Doctrine's Operative Content

The dual-sovereignty doctrine treats federal and state governments as operatively distinct sovereigns, each with its own operative criminal-law authority, each operating independently of the other. The operative consequence: an act may constitute an offense against both sovereigns, and either sovereign may prosecute the act without operative interference from the other. Double jeopardy does not preclude successive prosecution by the two sovereigns for the same act.

The leading cases include:

- *United States v. Lanza*, 260 U.S. 377 (1922) — successive federal and state prosecution for the same act of liquor possession during Prohibition; the Court held both prosecutions permissible under the dual-sovereignty doctrine.
- *Bartkus v. Illinois*, 359 U.S. 121 (1959) — state prosecution following federal acquittal; the Court upheld the state prosecution under the dual-sovereignty doctrine.
- *Heath v. Alabama*, 474 U.S. 82 (1985) — successive prosecution by Alabama and Georgia for the same murder; the Court held both prosecutions permissible under the dual-sovereignty doctrine as applied to two states (the doctrine reaches not only federal-state successive prosecution but also state-state successive prosecution).
- *Gamble v. United States*, 587 U.S. ___ (2019), 139 S. Ct. 1960 — Justice Alito's opinion reaffirming the dual-sovereignty doctrine over a substantial constitutional challenge.

The doctrine has been reaffirmed across nearly a century of operative jurisprudence and across multiple operative contexts.

### 3.2 The Operative Implications for Federal-Officer Conduct

The operative implication for federal-officer conduct in Utah is direct. A federal officer who commits conduct constituting both a federal offense and a Utah state offense — or conduct constituting solely a Utah state offense — is, by the operative architecture of dual sovereignty, subject to Utah's criminal jurisdiction independent of any federal prosecution.

The proposition is not novel; it is the operative architecture the Supreme Court has applied for nearly a century. The dual-sovereignty doctrine does not contain an "if the actor is a federal officer" exception. The doctrine applies to all operative acts within both sovereigns' jurisdiction, regardless of the actor's identity or office. A federal officer who commits state-defined criminal conduct within the operative jurisdiction of a state is, by the operative reach of the doctrine, subject to the state's criminal jurisdiction.

### 3.3 The Combined Effect

The Tenth Amendment's reservation of the police power, the Supremacy Clause's "Pursuance" qualifier, and the dual-sovereignty doctrine combine to produce a coherent operative architecture: the State of Utah retains plenary operative authority over crimes within its borders; federal officers operating within Utah's borders are subject to that operative authority for conduct constituting state-defined offenses; the Supremacy Clause protects federal officers only insofar as their conduct is within the operative scope of constitutional federal authority. §14.7 operates within this combined architecture. The operative reach of the section is the operative space the constitutional design preserves for state authority.

## 4. *In re Neagle* — The Operative Scope of the Holding

*In re Neagle*, 135 U.S. 1 (1890), is the case most often invoked for the proposition that federal officers are categorically immune from state criminal prosecution for conduct within the operative scope of federal authority. The case's operative holding is, however, substantially narrower than the broad reading sometimes given.

### 4.1 The Operative Facts

The operative facts of *Neagle* are unusual. David Neagle was a federal marshal assigned to protect Supreme Court Justice Stephen J. Field, who had received credible threats from David S. Terry, a former California Supreme Court Justice with whom Field had personal and professional animosity going back many years. The operative threats were violent; Terry had threatened to kill Field; Neagle was assigned as Field's personal protection. In August 1889, Terry approached Field in a restaurant in Lathrop, California, and physically attacked him. Neagle, perceiving Terry to be reaching for a weapon, shot and killed Terry. California sought to prosecute Neagle for murder. Federal authorities sought a writ of habeas corpus to release Neagle from state custody on the operative theory that Neagle was acting within the operative scope of federal authority — protecting a federal officer — when he killed Terry.

The Supreme Court granted the writ. The operative holding: where a federal officer is acting within the operative scope of a constitutional federal duty, the State cannot apply its criminal law to obstruct the federal duty.

### 4.2 The Operative Boundaries of the Holding

The operative boundaries of *Neagle's* holding are critical. The case turned on three specific operative findings:

- **The federal duty was constitutionally grounded.** The Court found that the protection of federal judges was an operative federal duty grounded in the operative architecture of Article III's judicial power. The duty was not a matter of mere institutional preference; it was traced to constitutional text and operative function.
- **The officer was acting within the operative scope of the duty.** Neagle's conduct — protecting Justice Field from an attempted assault by a person who had previously threatened to kill Field — was operatively within the protective duty. The officer was not exceeding the duty; he was performing it.
- **The state prosecution would have obstructed the operative federal duty.** Allowing California to prosecute Neagle for protecting Field would have, in operative effect, prevented the federal protective duty from being carried out. The operative obstruction would have been direct.

Where all three operative elements are met, *Neagle* protects the federal officer's conduct from state prosecution. Where any element is not met, *Neagle's* operative protection does not extend. The case is not a categorical shield for federal officers; it is an operatively bounded protection calibrated to the operative elements of the holding.

### 4.3 The Operative Gap Between *Neagle* and the Broad Reading

The broad reading of *Neagle* — that federal officers are categorically immune from state criminal prosecution for conduct within the general operative scope of their office — substantially exceeds the case's operative holding. The broad reading would, in operative effect, treat any conduct nominally connected to a federal duty as immune from state prosecution, regardless of whether the operative conduct was constitutionally grounded, within the operative scope of the duty, or actually obstructed by the state prosecution.

The broad reading produces operative absurdities. A federal officer who commits murder against a Utah citizen as a personal grudge is not engaged in a federal duty; the operative conduct is not within the operative scope of any constitutionally grounded federal authority; the state prosecution does not obstruct any operative federal duty. The broad reading of *Neagle*, applied to such a case, would nonetheless shield the federal officer from state criminal prosecution because the officer was nominally clothed with federal authority at the time of the conduct. The result is the operative inversion the Act exists to reverse.

The narrow reading, faithful to *Neagle's* operative holding, treats the case as protecting only conduct meeting all three operative elements. The Act's §14.7 abrogates the broad reading and operates within the narrow one. Federal officers engaged in conduct constitutionally grounded, within the operative scope of the duty, and the state prosecution of which would obstruct the operative federal duty, are not reached by §14.7. Federal officers engaged in conduct that exceeds the operative scope of constitutionally grounded federal authority, or that constitutes an independent state criminal offense not necessary to any operative federal duty, are reached.

### 4.4 The Operative Lesson From *Neagle's* Unusual Facts

The unusual operative facts of *Neagle* are themselves part of the case's operative significance. The protection of a Supreme Court Justice from a credible homicidal threat is not the operative conduct most federal officers in Utah engage in on a daily basis. The case's operative facts produced an operative holding tailored to those facts. The extension of the holding to the broad operative range of federal-officer conduct in modern American practice is a doctrinal accretion, not a textual extension of the case itself.

§14.7's operative approach is to apply *Neagle* to the operative facts it actually decided and to refuse the broader operative extension that doctrinal accretion has produced. The result is consistent with the case's own operative holding and consistent with the broader operative architecture of federalism.

## 5. The Structural Argument

The structural argument for §14.7 combines the doctrinal grounds developed above with the operative reasoning the Founders built into the constitutional design.

### 5.1 The Operative Premise

The operative premise is that the State of Utah's authority to apply its criminal law to crimes committed within its borders is one of the structural protections the Constitution provides to citizens. The state's plenary police power is, in the operative architecture of federalism, the citizen's first organized protective layer against any government actor — state, federal, or operating without any official capacity at all — who commits state-defined criminal conduct within the operative jurisdiction.

The premise is developed across [why_does_the_government_even_exist.md](why_does_the_government_even_exist.md) and [why_we_can_abrogate_supreme_court_cases.md](why_we_can_abrogate_supreme_court_cases.md). The structural conclusion: where federal action exceeds constitutional federal authority, particularly where it harms citizens within state borders, the State has both the operative authority and the operative duty to protect its citizens through the application of its operative law.

### 5.2 The Reverse Operative Question

The structural argument can be stated through the operative reverse. If federal-officer status were a categorical shield against state criminal prosecution for conduct within state borders, what operative architecture would result?

- A federal officer could commit conduct constituting murder, assault, kidnapping, false imprisonment, theft, fraud, or any other state-defined offense, against a Utah citizen, within Utah's borders, and the State would have no operative authority to apply its criminal law to the conduct.
- The State would be operatively prevented from protecting its citizens against the operative class of actors (federal officers) most likely to exercise significant operative authority over them.
- The dual-sovereignty doctrine would, in operative effect, be inverted: federal officers would be protected by federal status against state prosecution, while ordinary citizens (including state officers) would be subject to both federal and state prosecution under the doctrine.
- The structural protection of federalism — designed to provide redundant operative protection through state authority — would be operatively voided in precisely the operative cases where the federal actor is the operative threat.

This operative architecture is the inverse of what the Founders designed. The Founders treated the state as the citizen's protective resource against federal overreach (developed in [why_does_the_government_even_exist.md](why_does_the_government_even_exist.md)); a categorical federal-officer shield against state criminal law converts the protective architecture into its opposite. §14.7 rejects the inverse operative architecture and restores the operative architecture the constitutional design contemplates.

### 5.3 The Operative Conclusion

The structural argument produces a direct operative conclusion: federal officers exercising authority within constitutional federal scope are protected against state interference that would obstruct the legitimate exercise; federal officers exceeding constitutional federal scope or committing conduct that constitutes an independent state criminal offense are subject to the operative authority of the State of Utah to apply its criminal law. The operative line is constitutional federal scope. Within it, the Supremacy Clause's "Pursuance" protection operates. Outside it, the State's plenary police power operates.

§14.7 codifies this operative line in state law. The line is the operative line the Constitution itself draws.

## 6. The Operative Reach of §14.7

§14.7's operative reach extends to federal officers operating against Utah citizens within Utah's borders. The section's operative application is calibrated to the operative line developed above.

### 6.1 What §14.7 Reaches

§14.7 reaches federal officers' conduct that:

- Occurs within the State of Utah
- Constitutes an offense defined under the Act
- Is committed under color of federal authority
- Exceeds the operative scope of constitutional federal authority, or constitutes an independent state criminal offense, or both

The operative pattern covers ICE personnel committing unlawful family separation against Utah citizens; CBP personnel using force outside the operative scope of border-enforcement authority; FBI agents committing battery, malicious prosecution, or fabrication of evidence within Utah; DEA agents committing cabalist coordination with state actors against Utah citizens; U.S. Marshals exceeding the operative scope of judicial-protection authority; federal protective service officers using force against Utah citizens outside the operative scope of federal-facility protection; and the broader operative range of federal-officer conduct that meets the elements of the Act's substantive offenses.

### 6.2 What §14.7 Does Not Reach

§14.7 does not reach federal officers engaged in conduct within the operative scope of constitutionally grounded federal authority, performed proportionately, on a developed record of operative justification, without operative conduct constituting an independent state criminal offense. Federal officers performing the operative duties of their office, within the operative scope of the duties, with operative compliance to constitutional and statutory limits, are not reached by §14.7. The operative reach is calibrated to the operative line.

§14.7 also does not reach federal officers operating outside Utah's borders. The State's plenary police power extends to crimes within its operative jurisdiction; conduct outside the jurisdiction is not reached. The Act is calibrated to the operative scope of Utah's authority and operates within that scope.

### 6.3 The Companion Provisions

§14.7 operates within a network of related operative provisions:

- **§5.9.6** (Application to Federal Officers Reachable Under §14.7) applies the operative pretrial-release rules of §5.9 to federal officers reached under §14.7. The operative architecture treats federal officers the same as state officers for purposes of pretrial-release, conditions of release, and operative bail considerations.
- **§11.4** (Automatic Decertification, Disbarment, and Removal from Professional Standing) applies the operative forfeiture architecture to federal officers convicted under the Act where the operative conditions are met (federal POST-certification analogs, federal bar admission, federal professional standing).
- **§16** (Nullity of Government Instruments Repugnant to This Act or to Constitutional Authority) reaches federal instruments that, in operative effect, attempt to immunize federal officers from state criminal law contrary to the operative architecture developed above.

The network architecture produces operative coherence. §14.7 establishes the operative reach; the companion provisions apply the operative consequences.

## 7. The Foreseeable Objection — And Its Operative Answer

The foreseeable objection is that §14.7 violates the Supremacy Clause and the operative architecture of federalism. The objection misreads both.

### 7.1 The Supremacy Clause Argument

The Supremacy Clause argument is that federal officers are categorically immune from state criminal prosecution for conduct within the operative scope of federal authority, and that §14.7's operative reach therefore obstructs federal authority.

The operative answer is in Section 1 above. The Supremacy Clause protects federal law made in Pursuance of the Constitution. Federal action outside the operative scope of constitutional federal authority is not made in Pursuance of the Constitution and is not entitled to Supremacy Clause status. §14.7's operative reach is calibrated to the operative line the Supremacy Clause itself draws. The section does not obstruct federal authority within constitutional federal scope; it reaches federal-officer conduct outside that scope, which is not protected by the Supremacy Clause.

### 7.2 The Federalism Argument

The federalism argument is that the operative architecture of federal-state relations precludes states from applying criminal law to federal officers. The operative answer is in Sections 2 and 3 above. The Tenth Amendment reserves the police power to the states; the dual-sovereignty doctrine confirms the operative independence of federal and state criminal jurisdiction; the anti-commandeering doctrine reinforces the principle that the federal government cannot direct states to refrain from applying their operative law. The operative architecture of federalism supports §14.7's reach; the contrary argument inverts the operative architecture.

### 7.3 The *Neagle* Argument

The *Neagle* argument is that the case categorically shields federal officers from state criminal prosecution. The operative answer is in Section 4 above. *Neagle's* operative holding is bounded by three operative elements: constitutionally grounded federal duty, conduct within the operative scope of the duty, and state prosecution that would obstruct the operative duty. The broad reading exceeds the operative holding. §14.7 operates within the narrow reading and refuses the broader doctrinal accretion.

### 7.4 The Composite Answer

The composite answer is that §14.7's operative reach is calibrated to the operative line the Constitution itself draws between federal authority and state authority. Where federal authority is operatively present, the Supremacy Clause protects it; where federal authority is operatively absent, the state's plenary police power operates. The Act operates within this constitutional architecture, not against it.

## 8. Conclusion

§14.7 of Operation Checkmate ("Federal Officer Status Not a Shield") reaches federal officers operating against Utah citizens within Utah's borders for conduct that constitutes a state-law offense under the Act, because:

1. **The Supremacy Clause protects federal law made in Pursuance of the Constitution, not federal action outside the operative scope of constitutional federal authority.** Federal officers exceeding constitutional federal scope, or committing conduct constituting an independent state criminal offense, are not within the operative reach of the Supremacy Clause's protection.

2. **The Tenth Amendment reserves the police power to the states.** The State of Utah's plenary operative authority over crimes within its borders is one of the structural protections the Constitution provides to citizens. The authority extends to all crimes within the operative jurisdiction, regardless of the actor's identity or office.

3. **The dual-sovereignty doctrine confirms the operative independence of federal and state criminal jurisdiction.** *Lanza*, *Bartkus*, *Heath*, *Gamble* establish that federal and state governments are operatively distinct sovereigns; either may apply its criminal law to the same conduct; double jeopardy does not preclude successive prosecution. The doctrine contains no "if the actor is a federal officer" exception.

4. ***In re Neagle's* operative holding is bounded by three operative elements** — constitutionally grounded federal duty, conduct within the operative scope of the duty, and state prosecution obstructing the operative duty. The broad reading exceeds the operative holding and produces the operative inversion the Act addresses. §14.7 operates within the narrow reading and abrogates the broader doctrinal accretion.

5. **The structural argument from the Founders' framework supports the section.** The state is the citizen's first organized protective layer; the operative architecture of federalism was designed to provide redundant operative protection through state authority; a categorical federal-officer shield against state criminal law would invert the protective architecture. §14.7 restores the operative architecture the constitutional design contemplates.

6. **The section's operative reach is calibrated to the operative line.** Federal officers operating within constitutional federal scope are not reached; federal officers exceeding constitutional federal scope or committing conduct constituting an independent state criminal offense are. The line is the operative line the Constitution itself draws.

7. **The companion provisions complete the operative architecture.** §5.9.6 applies the operative pretrial-release rules; §11.4 applies the operative forfeiture architecture; §16 reaches federal instruments that attempt operative immunization contrary to the operative line.

The State of Utah's authority to apply its criminal law to crimes committed within its borders does not stop at the federal-officer boundary. The Founders did not design federalism to operate as a one-way ratchet against state authority. The Constitution's operative architecture treats the state as the citizen's first protective resource against any government actor whose conduct meets the operative elements of state criminal law.

§14.7 applies the constitutional architecture. The State protects its citizens. Federal officer status is not a shield.
