# Why Government Conduct Can Constitute Terrorism

Section 1.1.12 of Operation Checkmate names "Terrorism (Government Context)" as a substantive offense reaching government actors who use threat, fear, or coercive harm to a citizen's safety, livelihood, family, business, or rights, on a scale or with a malice sufficient to constitute terror against the citizen.

The naming choice will be one of the first things critics attack. The argument that the offense should be renamed — that "terrorism" is "a strong word," that the conduct is better described by some softer term such as "official misconduct," "abuse of authority," or "civil-rights violation under color of law" — is itself a tell. The pressure to find a less alarming label for government conduct that, performed by a private actor, would unhesitatingly be called terrorism, is the same operative pressure that produces every other piece of the architecture this Act exists to correct: the operative inversion under which the most powerful actors face the softest descriptions of their conduct while the least powerful face the harshest.

This document develops the operative basis for the naming choice. It traces the word "terrorism" to its origin — a description of **government** conduct, not non-state conduct. It documents the international-law and historical record that has consistently recognized state terror as a category. It develops the operative-effect test the Act applies. And it identifies the structural argument: exempting the most powerful actors from the legal category their conduct most clearly fits produces the operative inversion the Act exists to reverse.

The word is right. The naming is grounded. The structural argument is direct.

---

## 1. "Terrorism" Originates as a Description of Government Conduct

The English word "terrorism" enters political vocabulary as the name for a specific period of **government conduct**. The first recorded uses describe not non-state insurgent violence but the operations of a state in the immediate aftermath of revolution.

### 1.1 The Reign of Terror, 1793–94

The Reign of Terror (*la Terreur*) refers to the period from approximately September 1793 to July 1794, during which the Revolutionary Government of France — operating under the Committee of Public Safety, with Maximilien Robespierre as its principal figure — conducted a campaign of mass arrest, summary trial, and execution of perceived enemies of the Revolution. The numbers are disputed, but the operative range is forty thousand executed by guillotine or summary killing, hundreds of thousands arrested, and an extended pattern of denunciation, fear, and coerced compliance that characterized political life across the territory the Revolutionary Government controlled.

The word "terror" (*la terreur*) is the term **the Revolutionary Government itself used** to describe its mode of governance during this period. Robespierre, in his February 1794 speech "On the Principles of Political Morality," defended the policy directly:

> "If the basis of popular government in peacetime is virtue, the basis of popular government during a revolution is both virtue and terror; virtue, without which terror is fatal; terror, without which virtue is impotent. Terror is nothing but prompt, severe, inflexible justice; it is therefore an emanation of virtue."

The word entered ordinary political English through translations and reports of these events. The Oxford English Dictionary's first recorded citation for "terrorism" in English is from 1795 — within a year of the fall of Robespierre — and the definition is, in operative terms, "government by intimidation as conducted by the party in power in France between 1789 and 1794." Edmund Burke, writing in 1796, refers to "those Hell-hounds called Terrorists" — meaning the Jacobin officials who had conducted the Terror as a mode of governance.

The original operative meaning of "terrorism," in the language that produced the term, is **the use of state power to produce fear among the population as a mode of political control**. The word names a kind of government conduct.

### 1.2 The Inversion of the Word's Public Meaning

Over the two centuries between 1795 and today, the public-meaning emphasis of the word has shifted. In ordinary contemporary usage — newspaper headlines, government press conferences, popular discourse — "terrorism" is most often used to describe the conduct of non-state actors: bombings, hijackings, mass shootings carried out by individuals or small groups against civilian targets. The shift is a function of the political and media context of the late twentieth century, not a change in the underlying operative meaning of the word.

The shift has been challenged from multiple directions. International-law scholarship has consistently recognized state terror as a category alongside non-state terrorism. Historians of the twentieth century have applied the term to the conduct of the Soviet NKVD under Stalin, the Nazi state apparatus, the Khmer Rouge, the Argentine military junta during the Dirty War, the Pinochet regime in Chile, and a series of other governments whose conduct toward their own populations meets every operative element of the original meaning of the word. The Encyclopedia of Genocide and Crimes Against Humanity, the relevant volumes of the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, and the foundational works of political theory on the subject (Hannah Arendt's *On Violence*, Walter Laqueur's *A History of Terrorism*, Bruce Hoffman's *Inside Terrorism*) all preserve the state-terror category as part of the operative scope of the word.

The Act's use of "terrorism" to describe a category of government conduct is therefore not a coinage. It is a return to the word's original operative meaning, alongside the international-law and academic tradition that has consistently preserved it.

## 2. International Law Recognizes State Terror as a Distinct Category

The international legal architecture developed after World War II to address mass governmental wrongs against civilian populations has, at multiple operative joints, recognized state terror as a category subject to legal accountability.

### 2.1 The Nuremberg Principles

The Nuremberg Tribunal (1945–46) established that **government officials acting under color of state authority** could be held criminally responsible for conduct against civilian populations that, performed by private actors, would have constituted serious crimes. The Tribunal's Charter (the London Charter, August 8, 1945) defined "crimes against humanity" to include "murder, extermination, enslavement, deportation, and other inhumane acts committed against any civilian population, before or during the war, or persecutions on political, racial or religious grounds in execution of or in connection with any crime within the jurisdiction of the Tribunal, whether or not in violation of the domestic law of the country where perpetrated."

The operative principle: government conduct that produces civilian harm at sufficient scale, with sufficient malice, is reachable as criminal — even where the conduct was authorized, sanctioned, or directly commanded by the state. The Tribunal explicitly rejected the defense that the defendants had been operating under lawful authority of their state. Justice Robert Jackson, the lead American prosecutor, framed the principle directly in his opening statement:

> "The privilege of opening the first trial in history for crimes against the peace of the world imposes a grave responsibility. The wrongs which we seek to condemn and punish have been so calculated, so malignant and so devastating, that civilization cannot tolerate their being ignored because it cannot survive their being repeated."

The Nuremberg framework was codified into customary international law through the Nuremberg Principles (1950), the Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide (1948), the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948), the Geneva Conventions (1949) and their Additional Protocols, and the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court (1998). Each of these instruments recognizes that **state actors are criminally accountable for conduct against civilian populations that meets defined operative thresholds**. The threshold language varies — "crimes against humanity," "genocide," "war crimes," "grave breaches" — but the underlying principle is consistent: there is no immunity for state actors who direct, authorize, or participate in conduct producing civilian harm at sufficient scale and with sufficient malice.

### 2.2 The Twentieth-Century State-Terror Record

The twentieth century produced an unprecedented record of state terror as a mode of governance. The accounting includes, at a minimum:

- **The Soviet system under Stalin** — collectivization-induced famines (Holodomor 1932–33, estimated three to seven million dead), the Great Purge (1936–38, with hundreds of thousands executed and millions sent to the Gulag), the Katyn Forest massacre (1940, twenty-two thousand Polish officers executed), the extended Gulag system reaching every part of Soviet society.
- **The Nazi state apparatus** — the camp system, the Holocaust (six million Jews killed and millions of others), the operations against political opponents, the campaigns of state terror against occupied populations.
- **Maoist China** — the Anti-Rightist Campaign (1957–59), the Great Leap Forward (1958–62, with famine-induced deaths in the tens of millions), the Cultural Revolution (1966–76).
- **The Khmer Rouge in Cambodia** (1975–79) — approximately 1.5 to 2 million dead in a population of eight million, the most lethal per-capita state-terror regime of the twentieth century.
- **The Argentine military junta** (1976–83) — the Dirty War, with approximately thirty thousand "disappeared" by state security forces; the systematic torture and execution of political opponents.
- **The Pinochet regime in Chile** (1973–90) — the systematic detention, torture, and execution of opponents of the regime; Operation Condor; the use of DINA and CNI as instruments of state terror.
- **The North Korean regime** — the political-prison camp system (kwan-li-so), the systematic surveillance and ranking of the population by perceived loyalty (songbun), the famine response of the 1990s and following.
- **The various Latin American military regimes of the 1960s–80s**, the apartheid-era South African security state, the Romanian Securitate under Ceaușescu, and a wider set of regimes that produced documented patterns of state terror against their own populations.

Each of these regimes operated through the formal structures of state authority. Each authorized, directed, or tolerated conduct against its own population that meets every operative element of the word "terrorism" in its original meaning. Each is recognized in the historical record as a state-terror regime. The category is not theoretical; it is the descriptive name for a recurring pattern of state behavior, documented across continents and ideologies.

### 2.3 The International Criminal Court and the Continuing Recognition

The Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court (1998) gives the ICC jurisdiction over genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, and aggression. The Statute's Article 7 (Crimes Against Humanity) lists, among the underlying acts that constitute a crime against humanity when committed as part of a widespread or systematic attack against any civilian population: "(f) Torture; ... (h) Persecution against any identifiable group or collectivity on political, racial, national, ethnic, cultural, religious, gender ... or other grounds that are universally recognized as impermissible under international law; ... (j) The crime of apartheid; (k) Other inhumane acts of a similar character intentionally causing great suffering, or serious injury to body or to mental or physical health."

The Statute reaches **state actors** performing these acts. The operative scope of "crimes against humanity" in modern international law is the same operative scope the Act's §1.1.12 reaches — government conduct using state authority to inflict harm on civilian populations at sufficient scale or with sufficient malice.

The Act's choice to use the word "terrorism" rather than "crime against humanity" tracks two considerations. First, the Act operates at the level of Utah state criminal law, not international law, and uses vocabulary native to American criminal jurisprudence rather than the specialized vocabulary of international tribunals. Second, "terrorism" carries the operative connotation the Act intends to reach — conduct producing fear in the citizen as a mode of governance — more directly than the international-law alternatives. The translation from international vocabulary to state-law vocabulary is therefore deliberate, and the underlying operative category the Act reaches is the same category international law has recognized for three quarters of a century.

## 3. The Act's Operative-Effect Test

§1.1.12 of the Act is not a rhetorical use of the word. It is an operative offense with defined elements. The structural test the Act applies is straightforward and rooted in the original meaning of the word.

### 3.1 The Elements

The offense reaches a government actor's conduct that:

- Uses threat, fear, or coercive harm
- Against a citizen's safety, livelihood, family, business, or rights
- On a scale or with a malice sufficient to constitute terror against the citizen

Each element has operative content. "Threat, fear, or coercive harm" reaches the mechanism by which the conduct operates — the production of fear in the citizen as a means of compelling behavior, suppressing dissent, extracting compliance, or punishing for past acts. "Against a citizen's safety, livelihood, family, business, or rights" reaches the operative targets the conduct touches. "On a scale or with a malice sufficient to constitute terror against the citizen" reaches the operative magnitude — the conduct must rise above the threshold at which it crosses from official misconduct, official intimidation, or other lesser-included offenses into conduct that actually constitutes terror in the operative sense.

The element structure tracks the original meaning of "terrorism" precisely: government conduct that produces fear as a mode of control, directed against the citizen's most fundamental interests, at a magnitude sufficient to mark the conduct as terror rather than as ordinary abuse of authority.

### 3.2 The Operative-Reality Test, Not the Formal Label

The Act's drafting principle, developed in [why_the_definitions.md](why_the_definitions.md), requires that operative definitions test what actually happens in real life, not what the formal labels say is happening. §1.1.12 applies this principle directly. The question is not whether the conduct is **called** terrorism by the actor, by the agency, by the press, or by the political class. The question is whether the conduct **operates** as terrorism — whether the citizen who experiences it is, in operative effect, being subjected to a regime of fear sufficient to constitute terror.

A judge who, in a single hearing, threatens contempt against a litigant who has asked for an accommodation has committed Official Intimidation (§1.1.13). A pattern of agency action against a single family — children removed without basis, business inspected and cited weekly, criminal complaints filed and dismissed and refiled, eviction proceedings stacked, every member of the family separately investigated, every encounter with state authority producing a new procedural threat — across years, with documented malice from the actors involved, crosses from Official Intimidation into the operative pattern §1.1.12 reaches. The same elements that distinguish ordinary law enforcement from sustained campaigns of state harassment distinguish Official Intimidation from Terrorism under the Act. The line is the operative magnitude and the operative effect.

### 3.3 Cabalism, Carceralism, and the Companion Offenses

§1.1.12 does not operate alone. The Act's substantive offenses form a network in which Terrorism is one of several umbrella offenses reaching distinct patterns of government conduct. §1.1.6 (Cabalism) reaches the coordinated-actors version; §1.1.10 (Anarchy) reaches conduct outside any identifiable lawful authority; §1.1.13 (Official Intimidation) reaches the lesser-included single-act version; §1.1.47 (Carceralism) reaches the use of criminal process against citizens in crisis where the government has both the duty and the means to address the crisis. Where the elements of multiple offenses are met by the same conduct, cumulative charging is contemplated under §1.1.1.1.

The network architecture is itself a defense against the misreading that §1.1.12 is overbroad. The Act does not channel every form of government misconduct into the terrorism category. It defines distinct categories, with distinct elements, and applies them where the operative facts fit. Terrorism, under the Act, is the offense reserved for conduct that actually constitutes terror — sustained patterns or single acts of sufficient magnitude — and the lesser-included offenses reach the conduct that does not rise to that level.

## 4. The Structural Argument — Why the Naming Choice Is Correct

The argument that the offense should be named something softer than terrorism is, in operative terms, an argument that **government conduct should be described in less alarming terms than identical conduct performed by private actors**. The argument fails on three independent grounds.

### 4.1 The Operative-Severity Argument

If a private actor — a single individual, a small criminal group, a non-state network — conducted, against a Utah family, the kind of sustained pattern the Act's §1.1.12 reaches (multi-year coordinated harassment producing fear sufficient to constitute terror, directed at the family's safety, livelihood, children, business, and rights), no observer would hesitate to call the conduct terrorism. The federal terrorism statutes (18 U.S.C. § 2331 et seq.) reach conduct of this kind. State terrorism statutes reach conduct of this kind. The mass-media and political-discourse categories treat conduct of this kind as terrorism without qualification.

Where the same conduct is performed by actors clothed with state authority — by a coordinated group of officials using state power to produce the same effect on the same family — the conduct's operative severity is, if anything, greater than the private-actor equivalent. State power amplifies the operative effect; institutional resources extend the conduct's reach across time and geography; immunity architecture has historically removed the consequence the private actor faces. The conduct is more severe, not less, and the appropriate legal description is terrorism, not less.

### 4.2 The Operative-Inversion Argument

The current operative practice — under which "terrorism" is reserved for non-state conduct and government conduct producing the same effect is described as "misconduct" or "civil-rights violations" — is itself a piece of the operative inversion the Act exists to correct. The inversion is the same one developed in [why_does_the_government_even_exist.md](why_does_the_government_even_exist.md): the constitutional hierarchy treats the citizen as the constituent sovereign and the government as the agent, while the operative reality has, through accretion, treated the government as supreme over the citizen. The vocabulary the operative reality uses reflects the inversion. "Terrorism" becomes a category reserved for conduct against the state; conduct by the state escapes the category by linguistic convention rather than by operative argument.

The Act reverses this by naming the conduct what it operatively is. The naming choice is part of the broader operative restoration the Act performs — restoring the constitutional hierarchy by, among other operative steps, applying the legal categories to actors regardless of position.

### 4.3 The Founders' Own Usage

The Founders, in their own time, used the strongest available vocabulary to describe government conduct that produced fear in the population. The Declaration of Independence's catalog of grievances against the Crown is the leading example. Among the named acts:

> "He has erected a multitude of new Offices, and sent hither swarms of Officers to harass our people, and eat out their substance."

> "He has affected to render the Military independent of and superior to the Civil power."

> "For protecting them [armed troops], by a mock Trial, from punishment for any Murders which they should commit on the Inhabitants of these States."

> "For depriving us in many cases, of the benefits of Trial by Jury."

> "He has plundered our seas, ravaged our Coasts, burnt our towns, and destroyed the lives of our people."

The Founders used the operative vocabulary appropriate to the conduct: "harass," "Murders," "plundered," "ravaged," "destroyed." They did not describe the Crown's conduct in softer institutional terms — "regulatory enforcement," "official excess," "policy difference" — even though the formal structures of British colonial governance authorized many of the underlying acts. The Founders' operative judgment was that conduct producing the effect on the population of fear, plunder, killing, and arbitrary deprivation was to be described by the operative effect, not by the formal label.

The Act follows the Founders' operative judgment. Where government conduct produces the operative effect of terror, the conduct is to be described as terrorism, not in softer institutional terms. The Founders would not have hesitated. The Act does not hesitate.

## 5. The Naming Choice Is Part of the Anti-Drift Architecture

The drafting principle developed in [why_the_definitions.md](why_the_definitions.md) — that each defined term must specify both what it IS and what it IS NOT in operative detail — applies to §1.1.12 as it applies to every other defined offense in the Act. The naming choice is part of the anti-drift architecture.

### 5.1 What §1.1.12 IS

§1.1.12 IS: government conduct using threat, fear, or coercive harm against a citizen's safety, livelihood, family, business, or rights, on a scale or with a malice sufficient to constitute terror against the citizen. The offense reaches the operative pattern — sustained or single-act conduct of sufficient magnitude — that, when performed by a private actor, would unhesitatingly be called terrorism in ordinary American legal and political usage.

### 5.2 What §1.1.12 IS NOT

§1.1.12 IS NOT:

- Routine law enforcement performed within lawful authority, with proportionate force, on a developed record of probable cause, and without malice toward the citizen affected.
- Single-incident official misconduct that does not rise to the operative magnitude required to constitute terror in the operative sense — such conduct is reachable under §1.1.13 (Official Intimidation) or other lesser-included offenses.
- Policy disagreement between the citizen and the government, regardless of the citizen's intensity of feeling about the policy.
- Lawful exercise of regulatory authority under valid predicate, performed proportionately, with due process for the citizen.
- Ordinary criminal prosecution of citizens for offenses they have, in fact, committed — even where the citizen experiences the prosecution as harsh.

### 5.3 The Edge-Case Anchor

The structural test, applied at the edges, is the operative-magnitude test. Did the government conduct, in its actual operation against the citizen, produce the kind of effect that — performed by any other actor — would be called terror? Was the conduct of sufficient magnitude, sustained over sufficient duration, with sufficient malice from the actors involved, to cross from ordinary official misconduct into the operative category of terror? Where the answer is yes, §1.1.12 applies; where the answer is no, the lesser-included offenses are the operative reach.

The test is not metaphorical, not rhetorical, and not vague. The test is operative.

## 6. The Practical Consequence — Restoring the Operative Word to the Operative Conduct

The Act's naming choice has a practical consequence beyond the doctrinal one. It restores, in the operative vocabulary of state criminal law, the word that fits the conduct. When a Utah citizen, prosecutor, Citizen Council, or court encounters conduct that meets the elements of §1.1.12, the conduct is to be described as terrorism — in the charging document, in the proceedings, in the verdict, in the appellate record, in the press coverage, and in the public memory. The operative word is restored to its operative referent.

The consequence is significant. Language shapes operative reality. A government actor described in charging documents as committing "official misconduct" enters the operative public record at one operative magnitude; the same actor described as committing terrorism enters at another. The Act's choice is to use the operative word the operative conduct actually fits — to refuse the institutional convention under which government conduct is softened by vocabulary even where it would not be softened by any other operative measure.

This is part of the larger operative restoration the Act performs. The same rules apply to everyone — and the same words apply to everyone. A pattern of conduct that produces terror in a citizen's life is terrorism, regardless of whether the actor performing it carries a badge, holds an elected office, sits behind a judicial bench, signs an agency citation, or operates outside any formal authority at all. The word does the operative work the conduct calls for.

## 7. Conclusion

§1.1.12 names "Terrorism (Government Context)" as a substantive offense because:

1. **The word "terrorism" originates as a description of government conduct.** Its first recorded uses describe the Reign of Terror — government by intimidation as conducted by the Revolutionary Government of France between 1793 and 1794. The operative meaning of the word names a kind of government conduct, not a kind of non-state conduct.

2. **The international-law and historical record has consistently preserved state terror as a category.** Nuremberg, the Genocide Convention, the Geneva Conventions, the Rome Statute, and the twentieth-century historical accounting all recognize state actors as subject to criminal accountability for conduct against civilian populations that meets defined operative thresholds.

3. **The Act's operative-effect test reaches the same operative magnitude.** §1.1.12 reaches government conduct using threat, fear, or coercive harm against a citizen's safety, livelihood, family, business, or rights, on a scale or with a malice sufficient to constitute terror — the same operative test the historical record applies to recognized state-terror regimes.

4. **Exempting the most powerful actors from the legal category their conduct most clearly fits is the operative inversion the Act exists to reverse.** The current convention under which "terrorism" is reserved for non-state conduct, while identical operative conduct by state actors is described in softer institutional terms, is part of the operative architecture the Act addresses. The naming choice is part of the operative restoration.

5. **The Founders used the strongest available vocabulary to describe government conduct producing fear in the population.** The Declaration of Independence's catalog of grievances is the model. The Act follows the same operative judgment: conduct producing the operative effect of terror is to be described as terrorism, not in softer institutional terms.

6. **The naming choice is part of the anti-drift architecture.** §1.1.12 specifies what terrorism IS and what it IS NOT, with operative elements, an operative-magnitude test, and edge-case anchors. The naming is not rhetorical; it is structural.

The word is right. The naming is grounded in two centuries of operative meaning and three quarters of a century of international-law recognition. The structural argument is direct.

Where government conduct produces the operative effect of terror in a citizen's life, the conduct is terrorism. The Act names it.
